Chapter 519: The Nameless Swordsman (3)

When Liz returned home, Rian and Sylvia were talking.

According to what Rian had heard, the leaders of the Red Spear Bandits were mostly composed of remnants of the Baiden Kingdom's army. The leader, in particular, was a man in his mid-40s named Faiken, who had served as an infantry battalion commander before the border was pushed back.

"A battalion commander of the infantry."

Having wandered countless battlefields, Rian could gauge Faiken's prowess just by hearing his name. In a war-torn area, rank was a direct measure of skill, especially for a battalion commander in a border region where the pride of both nations was at stake.

"Hmm, why would someone like that become a bandit?"

"I don't know. He might be tired, or there could be personal reasons…"

Sylvia hesitated for a moment before speaking her mind.

"But in the end, isn't that just human nature?"

Rian found himself persuaded by her last words. There might be reasons, but perhaps it's simply human nature to choose a slightly better path, even if it means choosing evil.

"Red Spear Faiken is known even in the enemy country. That's why the Baiden security forces can't easily touch him. They have set up their base on Arch Mountain, 3 kilometers from here, and are expanding their influence by plundering nearby villages."

Fox, whom Rian had killed, was also part of this expansion.

"Arch Mountain…"

"Brother."

When Liz approached, Rian realized and apologized.

"Sorry, you had to see such a horrible sight."

"It's okay. I wasn't that scared."

Seeing dead bodies wasn't pleasant, but seeing them every day made them no more repulsive than cockroaches.

"Be honest. Brother, you're really famous, right? You lied about your name because people would flock to you if they knew your real name, right?"

"No. My name is Rian. And I'm not famous. The bandits were just weak. I didn't win because I was strong."

Liz lowered her head, looking dejected.

"But we… we live under those weak bandits."

Rian had nothing to say to that. Being weak wasn't a crime, but on the battlefield, such words were just empty ideals.

"I'd like to rest a bit. My wounds haven't fully healed yet."

Rian retreated to his room to escape the uncomfortable situation.

As Sylvia watched the firmly closed door with a worried expression, Liz hugged her and asked,

"Mom, what will we do if Brother leaves?"

"Liz, it's wrong to expect too much from others. Brother saved us."

"But I don't want to live like this anymore."

Who would want to stay in pain?

But Sylvia couldn't tell her young daughter the harsh reality.

One thing she had learned in life was that you can't understand something you haven't experienced.

In her youth, she had often said, "This is how life is," or "This is the right way to live," but when she faced those situations herself, she felt completely different from what she had said.

"Liz, you'll understand when you grow up. No one can fully understand you. But you have me. I'll protect you."

"I don't want you to protect me. I want someone to protect you."

Sylvia gave a sad smile. That was also a fear only a child could have.

"I'll talk to him. But even if no one helps, I'll always be on your side."

Sylvia hugged Liz tightly.

Rian didn't come out of his room until late at night.

He had skipped meals since breakfast, but the overwhelming burden on his shoulders made him forget his hunger.

"Red Spear Faiken. A battalion commander?"

When he had fought his brother in the Ogent family, Ogent Rai was an 8th-grade sergeant.

Of course, Rian knew Rai was a genius, so he never thought Rai would only be a battalion commander at Faiken's age.

But comparing their current skills, Faiken likely surpassed Lai.

"Can I… defeat him?"

According to Sylvia, the Red Spear Bandits had seven leaders.

Including Fox, there were three deputy leaders, so the number of subordinates was likely in the hundreds.

He had to fight them all.

He had to face the bandits that even the kingdom's security forces had given up on, all by himself.

"I'll die."

If he was going to die anyway, was there a reason to fight?

Justice that can't be upheld isn't justice.

"I don't know, Shirone. What should I do…"

Knock, knock.

As Rian spent a night of torment, there was a knock on the door.

"It's Sylvia."

"Come in."

Sylvia opened the door and entered.

Seeing Rian sitting on the bed without any sign of having slept, she sat down on a chair with a slightly relieved expression.

"What brings you here at this hour…"

"I wanted to thank you for your help. You are a benefactor to me and my daughter."

Rian reflected on his journey as a knight-in-training.

More than half of the people who had called him a benefactor eventually called him an enemy.

That's what responsibility is.

"I fought for myself. The bandits would have killed me anyway."

Sylvia nodded as if she understood.

As the silence grew longer, Rian couldn't bear it and spoke honestly.

"I'm sorry. I can't face the bandits alone. The border security forces have given up, and the villagers won't step up either."

"It's not your fault. This place wasn't always so desolate."

Those who could fight were already dead or had joined the bandits.

"I'll leave in the morning. I'm sorry."

"I'm not asking you to destroy the bandits."

"Then what?"

When Rian looked up, Sylvia averted her gaze and spoke.

"Can you be Liz's father?"

Rian waited for her to continue without showing any emotion.

It was an unreasonable request.

"I'm sorry. At thirty-three, I can't bring myself to talk about marriage, so I used my daughter. But I'm sincere. I'm not asking you to stay forever. Just for five years, or even three years, until Liz can leave this place and live on her own."

Rian felt a lump in his throat.

If he refused, would he become the enemy who ruined Liz's life again?

"I'll be a good wife."

As the silence grew longer, Sylvia seemed to make up her mind and began to untie her clothes.

Relying on Fox, her husband's friend, was worse than death.

But if it wasn't that disgusting, she knew what she had to give up to protect her daughter alone.

Rian firmly extended his hand.

"I'm sorry."

Sylvia's shameful hands stopped abruptly.

She hadn't expected this, as many had left after spending just one night.

"I have someone in my heart. And this won't change my mind."

"I see."

Sylvia fastened her clothes and stood up.

She had nothing more to give, so she had nothing more to ask for.

"Thank you anyway. Fox was… truly an enemy. I'll pack some food for you to take in the morning."

Knowing that meeting again would only bring embarrassment, Sylvia left the room, considering Rian's feelings.

Silence returned, and moonlight streamed through the window.

Rian spent the remaining time in contemplation.

The things he could and couldn't do flowed without any standard.

As dawn approached, Rian's confused eyes gradually regained stability.

He sheathed his greatsword, strapped it to his back, and stepped out of the room to find a bundle of food Sylvia had prepared.

Rian glanced at the room where the mother and daughter were sleeping.

"……"

Without touching anything, he slowly opened the door and went outside.

He would never return to Rafne Village.

An hour after Rian left, Sylvia's house was filled with Liz's cries.

"Mom! Brother left! He abandoned us and left!"

"No, Liz. Brother went on his own path."

"Waah!"

Even with her young mind, Liz seemed to understand the world a little, as she didn't say anything more and just cried.

At that moment, someone knocked on the door.

"Is anyone there? May I ask you something?"

Sylvia cautiously opened the door and peeked outside.

Then she raised her head to the doorframe, her expression turning blank.

A body that defied the average human size.

He was over 2 meters tall, and his shoulders were so broad they almost got stuck in the door.

From her experience, such people always wielded swords.

When the man bent down and suddenly stuck his face in, Sylvia flinched and stepped back.

His face was long like a sweet potato, with thick eyelashes and a thin mustache growing under his nose.

"Oh, sorry to startle you. May I come in for a moment…?"

Sylvia hugged Liz and asked,

"What is it?"

Without waiting for permission, the man entered the house, followed by a squire who barely reached his chest.

The squire wasn't short, but compared to the man in black iron armor with a finely crafted iron sword, he looked like a dwarf.

Sniffing the air, the man nodded as if he understood and spoke.

"I heard a rumor that a knight with blue hair and a greatsword came to this village a few days ago. Have you seen him?"

There was only one person who fit that description.

"No. I don't know anyone like that. Please leave."

"Hmm, is that so?"

The man pulled out a chair and sat down heavily.

Every action he took conveyed a sense of intimidation to the mother and daughter.

"Sorry for the late introduction. I'm Lambda, a knight-in-training. I held an official position in a distant country but recently started wandering aimlessly."

Lambda began to speak at length without being asked.

"Then I heard a rumor. A story about a young knight with blue hair and a greatsword."

"I told you, I don't know anyone like that!"

"Indeed, he may not be that famous. But I don't judge people by their fame. What particularly piques my interest is the path of that young swordsman."

Sylvia remained silent, having given up.

"The bodies he cut down, while some may have been by chance, had a consistent quality: boldness!"

When Lambda slammed the table, the mother and daughter flinched.

"They say everything ended with a single strike. Whether it was a horse, armor, shield, or weapon, he cut through it all."

Sylvia recalled Fox's final moments.

"Because of that, he's being regarded as a rising star among knight trainees. Thanks to his bold swordsmanship, he's recently been given the nickname 'Knight of Maha.'"

"Knight of Maha?"

"Yes. It means greatness, like the sky, the sea, or the earth. But from what I've heard, well, from what the villagers have said, a young man cut Fox into pieces along with his sword and armor yesterday morning. And also from what the villagers have said, that young man is staying here."

Sylvia's lips trembled.

"Do you really… not know?"

Lambda's eyes, as pretty as a woman's, pierced Sylvia's heart like an awl.
