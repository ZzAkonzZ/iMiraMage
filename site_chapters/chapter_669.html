Chapter 669:

A shockwave-based spell formed by the combination of relatively minimal mass and relatively immense speed.

Its destructive power was so overwhelming that it looked like an explosion spell, but the essence of Angel’s Punishment was the shockwave created at supersonic speed.

As the torn and twisted structures stopped swelling and began returning to their original forms, only fragmented remains of the Gallopers—burst apart by the vibration—were left behind.

“It’s being absorbed.”

Shirone watched as the creatures’ remnants soaked into the fibrous floor.

The thought that the entire structure of this place might be built from corpses sent a chill down his spine.

“Is… is it over?”

In the silence that followed, Kon muttered.

Next to him stood two golems wounded by the Gallopers' attacks. As he deactivated the Spirit Zone, they crumbled back into earth and vanished.

Even his pride as a veteran couldn’t compare to the spell that had just been cast. It was clear that this was no ordinary magic.

“So this is the magic of an Ivory Tower candidate…”

Ackman had already accepted defeat, but for the masters of the other two guilds, it was their first time witnessing Shirone’s overwhelming power.

No—such a fantastical spell was unprecedented, regardless of who had cast it.

“Maybe…”

Maybe they could actually make it out of this hell alive.

While some were catching a glimpse of hope, others, swallowed by the atmosphere of fear, were sinking into despair.

“H-Hnn…”

At the sound of sobbing, heads turned to see Elwi of the Blood Rose Guild trembling with clenched fists.

“Elwi, get it together. You can’t lose your courage,” said Guild Master Evian, trying to comfort her. But once emotions boiled over, they couldn’t be contained so easily.

“I can’t stop crying. I’m going to die!”

“That’s not true. You’re still alive, aren’t you?”

It was the first time anyone had seen Elwi, a poison mage who had once swept through battlefields with nerve gas, look so weak.

“I tried—I really tried—but I can’t get rid of the fear! I’m going to die…!”

Suddenly, she began to gag and open her mouth wide.

The real horror wasn’t fear itself—it was being consumed by it. Once you lost hold of your mind, you would be endlessly pulled into darkness.

“Huh? H-Huh…?”

Elwi’s eyes began to roll back.

“Elwi! No! Hang in there!”

Everyone could tell—another death was coming.

Then—

“It’s okay.”

Shirone embraced Elwi.

“Ah… aaah…”

Feeling his warmth, her pupils slowly returned to their place. Death, for now, had been deferred.

“Don’t worry. No matter what this place is, I won’t let you die meaninglessly. Just trust me and stay with us.”

Relief flickered across Elwi’s face—but her lips quickly curled down again.

“You’re lying! You can say that because you’re strong! In the end, it’s always the weak who die first! Even my nerve gas doesn’t work here!”

That was the spark.

“You’ll abandon me! You’ll hope I die first instead of someone else!”

You could see with your own eyes just how much fear could distort a person’s mind.

“Poison’s useless here,” Shirone said, meeting her startled eyes with a calm, gentle tone.

“But once we get out of here, your magic will be a huge asset. If you weren’t someone we needed, I wouldn’t have picked you in the first place. So yes, I mean it. You can trust me.”

“Really? You still need me?”

“Of course. And look, you’re still alive right now, aren’t you?”

Only then did Elwi realize that death had passed her by.

“A mage of your caliber can absolutely recover. So come on, let’s keep going—together.”

Though unofficial, Elwi was still a mage.

“Thank you…”

Finally regaining her senses, she smiled. Shirone smiled back.

But by the time he turned to give instructions to the group, the smile had already disappeared.

“According to our body clocks, it’s probably just before dawn. Pushing forward in this condition won’t help us, so let’s find a place to rest.”

No one objected. The overwhelming fatigue from the horrific environment left them with no room to argue.

The place they arrived at—avoiding the ghouls as best they could—was a massive heart-like chamber that had probably once been a warehouse.

“It’s… warm.”

That was the only comfort, and it filled them with disgust.

“What kind of world did we use to live in?”

As the mood darkened at Kon’s words, Shirone spoke up to clear the air.

“We’ll take watches. We have enough people, so let’s assign shifts and rotate until the mission is done.”

Kargin thought of cracking a joke—What if someone dies during their shift?—but held his tongue.

“I’ll go first.”

Kuan, already hardened by experience, limped out of the heart chamber.

“With him out there…”

The others exhaled sighs of relief, quiet enough not to be heard.

His prowess had already been proven in the fight against the Gallopers—Kuan was without a doubt the strongest in the group.

“What a privilege.”

To be guarded by the personal bodyguard of a diplomat like Aria—it gave them some peace of mind. That was precisely why Kuan had volunteered to go first.

“Get some rest. We leave in three hours.”

It wouldn’t be a full recovery, but here in this battlefield, sleep was merely a way not to die.

Even so, most of them couldn’t sleep.

As their consciousness drifted, the heartbeat of the living structure around them seemed to pound louder and louder.

“Leo…”

Joshua, the archer, rose from her seat and opened the pendant hanging from her neck.

“Lover?”

Startled by the sudden voice, she turned her head quickly—Kargin was smirking at her.

“Buzz off. Old guys make me gag.”

“Ever been interested, though?”

She thought for a moment before replying.

“What do you want?”

Kargin nodded toward the pendant.

“Lover?”

“No.”

Inside was a drawing of a four-year-old child.

“My son.”

“Huh. Didn’t know you were married.”

“I’m not. Don’t even know who the father is. I was too naïve back then…”

If you worked long enough as a mercenary, you’d meet plenty of men willing to pay for a night.

Sometimes the pay was better than the missions, so when she felt like it, Joshua accepted—but one night, she hadn’t used protection. That was her mistake.

Kargin knew enough about mercenary life not to ask further.

“So… is that why you’re obsessed with money? Because of your son?”

“He’s dead.”

Clack. The pendant snapped shut.

“Heatstroke. They said it was some virus or whatever. There was a cure. I just didn’t have the money.”

That was all Joshua said.

“Tough luck.”

Maybe it was the hellish place they were in, but it sounded sincere.

“What about you? Got family?”

Kargin gave a bitter smile.

“Unfortunately, I’m still a bachelor. A true bachelor. Though, I’m not exactly a virgin.”

At that old joke, Joshua snorted.

“I’m not joking. Want proof?”

As Kargin moved to lower his pants, Joshua turned away coldly and said,

“You dirty old man.”

“Pfft. Why not? Nothing kills fear better than a good time. And with our senses all scrambled, maybe we’ll reach some new dimension.”

“Why me? There’s that cute priestess over there. Or an actual diplomat.”

“Well, you’re the prettiest.”

It was an obvious lie—but it didn’t feel bad.

“How much you gonna pay me?”

“…What?”

“You said I’m pretty. So name your price.”

Kargin hesitated, then pulled a top-grade gold coin out of his cube brick.

When Joshua had insisted on receiving her payment in physical gold, he’d quietly accepted—because he, too, had no family.

“This is everything I have.”

All 100,000 gold he’d received from Lupist—laid out in the palm of his hand.

“Even a mackerel rots with dignity, huh.”

He was an old man with a knack for moving people.

Joshua snatched 50,000 gold from Kargin, tucked it into her CubeBrick, and stood up.

“I’ll take only half. Let’s go out the back.”

Feeling awkward, Kargin pocketed the remaining gold and followed her, asking:

“I mean, I don’t mind, but...”

“Don’t throw your life away for something like this. We’re getting out of here alive, so wait and enjoy it then, old man.”

Some had dozed off, but the core members, including Shirone, were still lost in thought in a corner. Joshua glanced at Shirone for permission.

When Lupist gave a wave of his hand as if to say “Do as you wish,” the two exited through the back door.

“Are you okay?”

“What? You coming to unwind a bit too?”

Shirone wasn’t in the mood for jokes.

“This is dangerous. Given the nature of this world, emotional fluctuations might have unforeseen effects…”

“Don’t expect too much. They’re hanging on by a thread. Push them any further, and someone will die.”

It was something only someone like Lupist—who had led countless subordinates—could understand.

While the core members were known across the kingdom for their mental resilience, for ordinary mercenaries, dying just from fear wasn’t a matter of weakness, it was simply a terrifying reality. Shirone, recalling Elwi’s state earlier, held his tongue.

“Hey… Meirei.”

“Yes?”

She turned her head, having still been covering her ears, experimenting with the divine frequency.

“You said it was an oracle earlier. What exactly do you mean by that?”

With Jane having made no progress on an escape plan, Meirei’s information was more important than ever.

“Oh, right.”

She sat beside Shirone and began to explain.

“It’s hard to say they’re memories, really. The oracles are so dreamlike. But once we entered this place, I started recalling bits and pieces. Some information surfaces that way.”

“You called this the Underworld, right?”

“Yes. But that’s just my interpretation. They perceive the world with far more complex senses than humans. I don’t think they even distinguish between the world we live in and this one.”

“Why didn’t you tell us earlier?”

“Honestly… I just remembered it now.”

If it was something like free association, more could be drawn out through conversation.

“What do you mean by ‘integrated’?”

Meirei blinked for a moment and then spoke.

“I once told you that my brother had a ‘sense,’ remember?”

“Yes. You’re ‘hearing,’ and your brother is…?”

“When I heard about Time Explosion, that was the first thing I thought of. My brother probably unlocked the sixth sense.”

“I did?”

“The Theraphos Order—or more accurately, the gods of Theraphos—classify senses into eleven categories. Humans only have five, but certain sensory beings perceive the universe through far more. Do you know what the eleventh and final sense is called?”

When Shirone shook his head, she pointed to him.

“Kyunggam (窮感), the Ultima System.”

“Ultima is… the eleventh and final sense?”

“I know you possess the final Gaia sense. But interpreting signals isn’t the essence of Ultima. The human sixth sense manifests from the integration of the five senses. The seventh sense arises from the integration of six. And so on. When one reaches the eleventh stage through continuous integration, everything becomes one. That’s the knowledge of the Theraphos gods.”

Meirei began speaking more quickly, as if more and more information was flooding in.

“Humans are beings of five senses. That’s why the sixth sense, when it manifests, is only temporary. But you’ve fully opened it. What they call the sixth sense—Shibokgam (時爆感)—the sense of exploding time. It’s a vivid awareness that transcends time.”

Before they knew it, even Lupist and Jane had come to listen intently.

“To truly unlock the eleventh sense, you have to keep integrating and opening new ones. Biologically, I don’t know if it’s possible. But they did it.”

“How many senses does Theraphos have, exactly?”

“I’m not sure. It’s certain they haven’t reached the eleventh, but they’re not far off. At the very least, having reached the seventh means they’re aware of this place.”

Jane asked, as if struck by a sudden thought:

“You said Shibokgam breaks time. Then the seventh sense…?”

“Yes. It breaks spatial barriers. The sense that shatters the line between existence and nonexistence.”

Meirei spread her hands as if touching an invisible wall.

“The seventh sense—Pakji (薄知).”

Another sense on the path toward ultimate awareness.
