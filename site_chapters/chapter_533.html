Chapter 533: Final World (1)

The Abyss is a simulated world built from present information that manifests the end of the future.

Trying to measure the size of a garbage heap of information is pointless—but even so, Miro’s group was certain they could find Shirone here.

No matter how vast and disorderly the Abyss might be, all humans inevitably connect to the informational patterns most familiar to them. And because this place is the final destination for information, it represents the furthest point into the future that current data can reach.

Naturally, Shirone’s information had also been absorbed into the Abyss in whatever form the system deemed “most compatible.”

The name someone first gave this world was Apocalypse.

Skyscrapers in ruins.

Buildings had lost their skin, exposing steel frames like the raw flesh of some creature. Cars sat frozen, fossilized in place, their engines long dead.

“Hamei, hurry up! Come on!”

Through the decaying wasteland, a boy and girl not yet ten years old emerged.

They were part of a race known as the Children of the Sun, the final descendants of humanity after countless nuclear wars and disasters on a cosmic scale.

With green hair and sun-darkened skin, they lived through photosynthesis. Their digestive systems were only used for water absorption—anything heavier wasn’t consumed, even for fun.

“Thanks for coming, brother. I’m so glad you said you’d come with me. Sorry for asking something so difficult.”

“Don’t talk like some adult. You still haven’t even dropped your stem yet.”

Just a year apart in age, to Woodga, Hamei still seemed like a child—this was her first time going out for “gathering.”

“Hmph! Like you’ve dropped yours!”

Children of the Sun had a stem-like growth at the top of their heads, which gradually shrank and eventually fell off with age.

“Alright, let’s move. We can only enter the Tree of Life at a specific time.”

Woodga urged her along.

“You’re eight now—you’ve got to start gathering. It’s too important to leave to the slaves. Watch the route carefully and memorize it.”

“But we have to pass through the Contaminated Zone.”

“Don’t worry. Just do exactly what I say.”

Expertly navigating the hazards of the ruined city, the Children of the Sun slipped deeper into its heart.

“Krhahaha! There they are!”

A shrill voice suddenly rang out.

Instinctively, Woodga shoved Hamei down and peeked around a corner.

“Undergrounders…”

Slender-limbed, fur creeping up past their ears—they climbed the wreckage with agile ease, heading higher.

Though the Children of the Sun refused to acknowledge them, the beings known as Undergrounders were another branch of humanity surviving in Apocalypse.

They had the strength of apes and the intellect of humans. And they hunted.

Real hunting.

Why’d we have to run into them here?!

What made Undergrounders truly dangerous was their use of ancient weapons buried beneath the earth.

These ones were wielding machine guns and bombs slung around their shoulders.

“Meat! It’s food!”

The seven Undergrounders burst from the rubble and aimed skyward.

After the seas dried up, some fish had evolved to float through the air using gas-filled swim bladders. The one they were after now was a skyfish called Rajette.

“Shoot it down!”

Gunfire lit up the sky, their long arms flailing with the recoil until their magazines clicked empty.

Woooooohm!

The Rajette let out a strange noise and blasted compressed gas from its rear, its only defense in exchange for lightweight evolution.

A bullet ripped through its balloon-like body. It shriveled midair and plummeted to the ground.

“Got it! The eyes are mine!”

There wasn’t much meat, but it would be just enough for seven to survive another day.

As the fish hit the ground, the Undergrounders pounced, tearing it apart barehanded.

“Delicious! So good!”

Shoving unidentifiable bits into their mouths without hesitation, they devoured it like animals.

Watching the scene, Hamei trembled.

“Horrible…”

For the Undergrounders, eating was survival. But to the Children of the Sun—who recoiled at the very act of consumption—it was a nightmare.

“Now’s our chance. Let’s get out while they’re distracted.”

Woodga took her hand and led her across the street.

They ran for three more blocks, hearts pounding, before finally pausing to wipe the sweat from their brows.

“Whew… we made it. From here on—”

Woodga suddenly stopped mid-sentence.

Across the road, an Undergrounder was walking straight toward them.

Wearing a rusted pot for a helmet and belts of bullets slung over his shoulders, he held a heavy machine gun in both hands.

“Meat!”

Like a molting monkey, the Undergrounder grinned wide and opened fire.

He didn’t aim. Just sprayed the area in front of him, demolishing building fronts as he advanced.

“Run! Hamei, run!”

They sprinted for their lives, metal beams scratching their skin.

“Ah!”

Hamei tripped over something.

Woodga turned, horrified, as she sprawled face-first.

No! Not now!

“Yaaahooooo!”

From the traffic light above, the Undergrounder leapt like a monkey from a tree, gun aimed.

“All mine!”

KREEEEE!

Just then, a giant bird swooped in from the opposite direction and snatched the Undergrounder with its massive beak.

Wings meant meat-eater.

In a world where energy was scarce, birds took a gamble—sacrificing fuel efficiency for sheer speed that could rival the jet-fueled skyfish.

As the monstrous bird vanished with its prey, Woodga collapsed to the ground, legs giving out.

“Phew… that was way too close. Hamei, are you okay?”

No answer.

He looked up and found her staring at the ground, wide-eyed.

“Hamei? What are you doing? Are you hurt?”

“…Brother, look at this.”

Woodga stood and followed her gaze—and his eyes widened in shock.

“Move over a sec.”

He brushed dirt from a glass capsule protruding from the ground and gasped.

“It’s real. This is an Ancient’s Rest!”

Before the world ended, people had entered these pods for stasis.

To the Children of the Sun, they were a new source of slave labor. To Undergrounders, gourmet meat.

Either way, finding one was a jackpot.

“Let’s get him out! Help me!”

“But what about the Tree of Life? We only have a short window to get inside.”

“There’ll be another chance before sunset. Just say you found it. You’ll be blessed by Ra.”

“...Blessing of Ra?”

Tempted by visions of luxury, Hamei reluctantly helped.

Though not as strong as Undergrounders, the Children of the Sun were capable of surviving Apocalypse. Together, they unearthed the capsule easily.

Inside slept a pale-skinned, golden-haired boy.

“Wow, his skin’s so white.”

“Let’s open it. I’ve seen the older guys do it before.”

Brushing off the dirt, they found a label engraved into the pod:

Silence Cryo Corp.

“There should be a switch somewhere…”

He pressed a green button.

The capsule hummed to life, lights blinking on as an engine spun up. A woman’s voice came through.

–System online. Deactivating stasis for subject code 10111-111001.

–Stasis duration: 2,473 years, 287 days, 14 hours, 9 minutes, and 42 seconds.

After a pause, the system continued:

–Memory data lost due to low power. Proceeding with stasis deactivation.

With a loud clang, the glass panel shot open.

Smoke poured out, prompting Hamei to cling to Woodga’s arm.

“Is this really okay?”

“Don’t worry. It won’t explode. The older guys said the ancient ones always did this.”

As the smoke cleared, they finally got a good look at the capsule’s contents.

All systems had finished, yet the blond boy remained still, seemingly deep in sleep.

“What now? Should we wake him? Hey. Hey! Open your eyes.”

“Gasp!”

As Woodga grabbed his shoulders and gave him a shake, the boy’s eyes suddenly snapped open, and he bolted upright.

“…It worked. He’s really awake.”

The boy gasped for air, disoriented, like someone jolted from a nightmare. He didn’t even blink—just breathed heavily as if trying to remember how to exist. Slowly, his awareness began to return, and he looked around in confusion.

Where… is this? What the hell happened?

Large portions of his memory were missing. He couldn’t even recall who he was with any certainty.

Locking eyes with the boy, Woodga straightened up and spoke in a forced, authoritative tone—just like he’d heard the older boys use.

“What… what are you staring at? If you’re awake, get out already! From now on, you’re a slave of our settlement!”

I… don’t understand what he’s saying.

The boy ignored Woodga and fell back into thought.

His memories were a jumbled mess—information fused into the form most “suitable” for the world of Apocalypse.

I remember entering a cryo chamber… but…

At the same time, faint memories clung to him—like a dream—of a world where strange powers were used.

The problem was, he couldn’t tell which memories were real.

“Tch! Hey! Aren’t you getting out?”

Woodga grabbed his arm and began to pull him from the pod, prompting Hamei to panic and hold him back.

“Brother, don’t! What if he gets angry and does something to us?”

“That’s why we have to act strong. Until he receives the Blessing of Ra, he’s not a full slave yet.”

Whoa… how does a kid have this much strength?

The boy wasn’t offended—he was just surprised by how strong these children were. Still, he stepped out of the capsule, figuring he owed them something for waking him up.

“Uh… thanks. But… where am I? No—how much time has passed?”

He wasn’t all that shocked by the ruined world around him. After all, he’d known this would happen from the moment he chose to enter stasis.

Not understanding a word, Woodga pounded his chest and said proudly:

“I’m Woodga! And this is Hamei! When we go back to the settlement, you have to say Hamei found you, got it?”

Woodga. Hamei. Must be their names.

“What’s your name?” Woodga asked, pointing at him.

The boy paused again, trying to remember.

My name… who am I, really?

Suddenly, a stabbing headache struck—splitting pain that sent fragmented memories flashing through his mind like shattered glass.

—“Sector 2 is collapsing! There’s not enough power! We can’t put everyone into stasis!”

—“Shut up! There are still people in the capsules waiting! They’re not asleep yet!”

—“But—!”

—“Save as many as you can! Even one more! For the future of humanity! We survive, no matter what!”

The shouting of the researchers rang clearly in the ears of the boy lying in the pod, even though he hadn’t moved.

Golden code flickered across the interior of the capsule. The boy’s eyes darted in fear as the system kicked in.

—Haa… haa… haa…

—“Activating cryostasis. Molecular activity ceasing. Temperature dropping to minus 273 degrees Celsius.”

—“Aaaaagh!”

The memory cut off, like a thread severed by a blade.

“Ghhk…!”

The boy clutched his head, writhing under the flood of conflicting and possibly false memories.

“What’s wrong? Are you okay?” Woodga asked, stepping toward him in concern.

And just then—amid the chaos—a single piece of information surfaced in the boy’s mind. It felt real.

“Ro…”

“Ro?” Woodga echoed.

“Roshine. I think… my name is Roshine.”
