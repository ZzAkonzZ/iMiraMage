Chapter 792:

When Shirone and Jaestin returned to the guild, her subordinates were busily working to repair the damage they’d caused to the building.

Despite the commotion, no one from outside had come to investigate, aside from a few acquaintances from nearby guilds. Shirone was sure the city guard had quietly stepped in behind the scenes.

"Alright. Everyone stop what you're doing," Jaestin said.

The woman at the counter, sniffling with frustration, stopped cleaning.

“Damn it!” Eyepatch threw his hammer after failing to fix a board into place.

You’re the one who broke it all in the first place...

Even though Shirone had repeatedly said he wasn’t there to fight.

“So, is the talk over?” one of the mercenaries asked. “It better be something worth not billing for.”

Jaestin turned to the black-haired man. “Gather all our members—the ones slacking off somewhere too. Meet at Guder’s Inn. We’ll talk there.”

“Yes, ma’am.”

As the black-haired man quickly left, Jaestin looked at Shirone.

“We live and die together. These are people I’ve been with since the beginning, back when I first fled into exile. Whatever you told me, you’d better be able to tell them too.”

“Of course.”

As Shirone and Jaestin left the building, the two mercenaries with longswords scratched their heads.

“What kind of serious stuff are they dealing with?”

At Guder’s Inn, Shirone rented the largest room and explained everything to the eight gathered mercenaries.

“So... you want the captain to assassinate Havitz? As in, the Emperor of the Gustav Empire?”

“That’s correct.”

Eyepatch squinted. “Are you insane?”

“I don’t think it’s a bad offer.”

Shirone turned to Jaestin.

“You can’t hide forever. And if you want to break this cursed connection, this is your chance.”

Still, Jaestin remained silent.

“I’m not saying you have to do it alone. My team—our best—will handle all the prep work.”

“Then what exactly do you want from me?”

“We need your hatred,” Shirone said plainly. “We need your rage.”

“Hatred...”

Jaestin remembered every second of her life on the run from Havitz.

Of course I hate him.

She had crawled, been beaten, killed when she had to. Before she met her team, she lived a life lonelier than any criminal’s—more like a beast.

I want him dead!

Her clenched fists trembled as her teeth ground together.

Havitz...

But when she imagined the face of the evil she had never even seen in person, a paralyzing fear washed over her, as if her very organs were melting.

“Hrrgh...!”

Yes, she had survived—but only because Havitz had destroyed everyone else. Her parents, her siblings, her cousins, their cousins...

It wasn’t a massacre. It was a bombardment.

There were times she wondered what kind of god would allow such a disaster.

But when she realized she was likely the only one in the world to escape Havitz’s grip—

Revenge? Someone like me?

Her trembling hands tried to steady themselves, but it only made them shake more.

I’m scared.

Havitz terrified her—beyond reason.

“Captain...” Eyepatch said, his voice filled with pity.

Jaestin pounded her own hand again and again.

“Damn it! Damn it!”

Finally calming herself, she fixed Shirone with a cold glare.

“You see this? This is my reality. I can't even shoot a gun like this, let alone kill anyone.”

“You won’t need a gun.”

Jaestin’s brow twitched. “I thought I was the one killing Havitz?”

“You are. But with a dagger.”

“What the hell are you talking about? I’m a sniper. I don’t do close combat, and with a dagger?”

“No, it’s still a long-range shot.”

Shirone cut her off.

“Havitz will be at least 120 kilometers away. You won’t be in danger.”

“...There’s no gun in the world that can shoot that far.”

“That’s why I said we need your hatred. If it’s you, you can do it.”

Nothing Shirone said made sense—at least not to anyone from this world.

‘No, he’s probably right. That’s what makes it worse.’

Eyepatch spoke up.

“Do it, Captain. This is your shot. I mean, us? Whatever. But your skills? They’re too good to rot here.”

It would’ve been impossible for Jaestin to grow her influence, not with her name so well hidden.

“Let’s end this once and for all. Then we can step into the real world. And hey…”

Eyepatch flushed and added awkwardly, “You’ve got to settle down sometime, right?”

The absurd comment made everyone stare at him, and the counter woman asked in disbelief:

“Wait, don’t tell me you’re into the captain...?”

“What are you talking about?! I may only have one eye, but I can still see just fine, okay?”

Jaestin turned toward him.

“Yeah? Well, I’ve thought about you.”

Eyepatch’s one eye widened.

“F-For real?”

“Of course not, you dumbass.”

“...”

With Eyepatch shut down, Jaestin turned back to Shirone.

“Okay, say I do kill Havitz. Then what? Who’s going to protect us after we assassinate the emperor of the Gustav Empire?”

“Oh…”

It was a good question.

“Well, obviously, Kashan will—”

Thud! The table shook as Jaestin slammed it.

“Don’t give me that crap. So we risk our lives and go right back to hiding and kissing nobles’ asses? I’m done living like that.”

Shirone nodded slowly, understanding.

“Then what do you want?”

“Hmph. Before I answer that, you should answer me first. What’s your real position? Don’t downplay it. I know you’re behind this. Not a single guard came running during that whole mess.”

“I’m a mage from the Ivory Tower.”

“The Ivory Tower?”

The black-haired man frowned. “I thought that was just a research institution.”

“What a coincidence. That’s what they told me in kindergarten too,” Jaestin scoffed.

The man shrugged. “They say you’re higher than kings.”

“Good.”

Realizing Shirone’s social power, Jaestin leaned back and got serious.

“Listen up. The Jaestin Mercenary Corps may be small, but we’re elite. It’s embarrassing to say this after getting wrecked, but…”

“No, you’re all incredibly skilled,” Shirone said.

“Glad you get it. I want you to get us into Guardian King—the top mercenary guild in Arachne. And not just as grunts. I want to be made a squad leader at minimum.”

“That might be difficult...”

“This isn’t nepotism. It’s compensation for hiding and surviving all these years.”

Yahweh might be omniscient, but even he couldn’t demand others sacrifice themselves for someone else's gain.

“I’ve done a little guild work myself. Even if you’re skilled, guilds value contribution. You’ll cause a stir.”

Not just Jaestin’s crew—other guilds were just as tightly knit.

“What? You can’t even give us that, and you’re making big promises?”

Jaestin’s temper flared.

“Let me be clear: If it’s not Guardian King, there’s no deal. This isn’t a mission for me. It’s a fight for my life.”

“Hm...”

Silver Ring had likely disbanded in Radum, and the only guild Shirone could personally ask for help from was the Parrot Mercenary Corps.

“Sister Marsha…”

“What?”

When Shirone snapped out of his thoughts and looked up, Jaestin’s crew was staring at him wide-eyed.

“What did you just say? Marsha? Clay Marsha?”

“Yes. Do you know the Parrot Mercenary Corps?”

It was Jaestin who was taken aback instead.

“Of course I do. The Parrot’s one of the world’s top 10 mercenary guilds. The Fallen Madonna. Who in this business doesn’t know Marsha?”

‘One of the top 10 guilds in the world?’

A feat she’d pulled off just four years after leaving Galliant.

‘Well, she is smart.’

It seemed she'd put to good use the substantial support she received from Uorin as a reward for helping in the Underquarter’s maze.

‘Yeah, if it’s the Parrot, this might work.’

The Fallen Madonna, Clay Marsha.

If anyone could open a path for the Jaestin Mercenary Corps, it would be the matron of the abandoned wolves.

“Alright. I’ll introduce you to the Parrot Mercenary Corps. I’ll even recommend you for a squad leader position. That settles our deal, right?”

“Nope. What kind of deal ends like that? Not until I’ve confirmed Marsha’s acceptance with my own eyes.”

Spinning her finger in the air, Jaestin added,

“Last I heard, she was stationed in the Iron Kingdom, but she’s so elusive… The sooner you track her down, the better. I might change my mind.”

Confident that the advantage was hers, she watched Shirone’s reaction with a smirk.

“There’s no need to track her down.”

Shirone asked calmly,

“By any chance… is there a Temple of Theraphos nearby?”

As a famous refuge nation, Arachne housed countless coexisting religions.

The Theraphos sect also had a small temple on the outskirts of Bengol, where it recruited followers.

“Excuse me.”

Shirone approached a priest and greeted him.

“Welcome. May the grace of Theraphos be with you. How may I help?”

“Do you truly believe in Theraphos?”

Without being in a trance state, channeling was difficult.

“Of course. The great god Theraphos guides all humanity toward good. Those deemed evil—”

“Then could I speak to that god?”

“Pardon? What did you just—?”

The priest frowned in offense, but Shirone sighed and said,

“Judge, I know you’re listening.”

At that moment, the priest trembled, rolled back his eyes, and looked up at the ceiling.

“Hexa, you got lucky with this apostasy case. If I hadn’t filibustered in the council…”

It seemed he had a lot to say.

“Let’s talk about that later. Right now, I have a message for someone named Clay Marsha. It’s urgent.”

“…What’s the message?”

The voice of the Judge, channeled through the priest, sounded disappointed, but Shirone didn’t pay it any mind and relayed the message.

Two hours later.

At a tavern in the Iron Kingdom.

The entire building had been rented for three days, and the Parrot Mercenary Corps’ officers were already drinking in broad daylight.

Their leader, Marsha, was in a corner trimming her nails when the door creaked open.

“Boss. Boss Marsha.”

The vice-captain of the 10th squad dragged in a wide-eyed boy with shock-white hair.

One of the officers asked,

“What is it? If you're here to bum booze, get lost.”

“No, it’s not that. This kid must’ve had too much day-drinking—he took a nap and started spouting nonsense.”

The boy with white hair said,

“It’s not nonsense. I saw it. A revelation—God gave me a revelation!”

“Come here, you adorable little thing.”

Marsha beckoned the boy over and lowered her gaze to assess his mental state.

“No drugs… What kind of dream was it?”

“My great god told me to deliver this message to you. You must go to Arachne immediately.”

Marsha grinned with a cigarette between her lips.

“What? Even God’s fallen for my beauty? Is He telling me to enter the beauty pageant?”

“You’re over thirty—they won’t even take your application.”

Smirking at Freeman’s jab, Marsha wrapped her arm around the boy’s neck.

“So, kiddo. What exactly did your great god say about me heading to Arachne?”

“H-He said this.”

The boy repeated the words exactly as spoken.

“There, Hexa—no, Yahweh—no, Shirone is waiting for you.”

“…Hmph.”

She pushed the boy back and leaned into her thoughts, exhaling a long trail of smoke.

“What kind of god stutters?”
