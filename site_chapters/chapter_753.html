Chapter 753:

Shirone looked up at the sky.

Law.

In the end, the mechanism of Lawslayer, which must kill, resembled the fate of Minerva, who was destined to become a witch.

Can one ever escape it?

If he had chosen bread for lunch today, would that have been an act of human will—or the result of an unknowable combination of infinite variables?

Even if I thought I wanted bread but suddenly changed the menu…

If that too was the result of law—

Then it’s too vast to analyze, and Uorin’s use of the word “curse” may be the most accurate way to describe it.

At least, until Beron’s experiment ends.

Soothed by the sound of crickets, Shirone read the final lines of the letter.

The dagger Lawslayer is believed to have come into Minerva’s hands around the age of twenty.

That fearsome weapon wandered the world, taking countless lives.

Its destructive power was beyond belief.

At some point, high-ranking officials in various nations began dying in strange incidents.

By the time their leaders attempted to investigate, it was already too late. The revenge had been perfectly carried out.

As many murderers emerged as there were victims, but the true killer was Minerva.

That’s the nature of revenge, isn’t it?

Minerva, beyond redemption, chose to become a true witch. She unleashed a catastrophe recorded in history as the Walking Dead.

An unspeakable number of people died, and humanity nearly faced extinction.

That’s when the Ivory Tower intervened.

Was that too part of the wheel of law?

The Ivory Tower, having stopped the Walking Dead, presented Minerva with two options:

To receive humanity’s judgment—or to become a resident of the Ivory Tower and work for the good of humankind.

Minerva became one of the Five Stars of the Department of Human Safety.

At first, Minerva supposedly chose the first option.

She didn’t want to fight for the humans she so utterly despised.

But apparently, there was some kind of deal. If I had to guess…

It was an order from the Great Star.

Probably promising that even those who became killers under Lawslayer’s law would not be punished.

What came to Shirone’s mind the moment he read this part of Uorin’s letter was his conversation with the Great Star.

The Five Stars of the Ivory Tower, in exchange for commanding satellites, are permitted to register one item.

The example she gave back then wasn’t just hypothetical—it was a direct reference to Minerva’s case.

Minerva did not destroy Lawslayer.

The Ivory Tower must have demanded it, of course, but she apparently stood her ground to the very end.

It caused an enormous controversy across the world.

These were the words of someone who had lived through that era.

Minerva was transported to the Ivory Tower under the protection of the stars, while imprisoned in a wooden cell.

The crowd was immense—over twenty thousand. They hurled curses and stones at her.

Their rage was understandable.

But the Minerva Shirone saw in the records looked… calm.

Even under a storm of reporters’ questions, she stared straight ahead in silence.

Until one journalist broke through the crowd and reached her.

The place fell silent in an instant, and the question they asked was—

The core question.

Why didn’t you destroy Lawslayer?

And Minerva, for the first and last time, turned to the reporter and is said to have replied:

—Because if there is someone who truly must be killed, then they must die.

It was the desperate wish etched into the heart of a ten-year-old girl, powerless and without protection, who had been humiliated by countless adults.

And so Lawslayer still wanders the world, taking lives.

Now you understand what it means to strike Extreme Evil with Evil.

If you find Lawslayer and pour your desperate wish into it, the law will move to eliminate Extreme Evil.

But... this story isn’t meant to make you feel sympathy for Minerva. Though if it’s you, I’m sure you’ve already forgiven her.

What I really want to say is this: the core of Lawslayer lies not in the weapon, but in the one who truly must be killed—the desperation behind it.

Without desperation, Lawslayer will not activate.

That narrows things down.

Powerful people and the wealthy only want to locate Lawslayer—they don’t actually try to possess it.

Because if it ends up in their hands, they’ll suspect someone used it to target them.

It’s a weapon that stands as the natural enemy of power.

Almost every known case of death by Lawslayer was when the weak killed the strong.

If you trace the routes of those incidents and follow the paths, you’ll be able to determine where it is now—and who holds it.

And the place I reached, after rapidly tracking its path around the world, was here: the Republic of North Aymond.

If Extreme Good exists in the real world, then naturally, Extreme Evil must dwell in the under world. Most likely, it’s Satan.

And like you, he exists outside the boundaries of law. That’s why even Anke Ra couldn’t touch him.

If Nane and Miro follow the law, then Shirone and Satan are the counterbalance—those who deny it.

However, according to Heaven’s report, even Satan must inhabit a living body in order to manifest.

Back then, that body was someone named Frankwine.

So it’s a race against time.

Satan is probably narrowing down his candidates as we speak. There are too many for history-searching abilities to isolate.

That’s why we need Lawslayer.

Uorin plans to use Lawslayer to eliminate every one of Satan’s potential vessels.

I know it’s a difficult task—but I hope you’ll help.

If you help me this time, you won’t owe me anything anymore.

A very Uorin-like closing line—calculating, but sincere in her own way.

I miss you.

The letter ended with the seal of a Teraje, and Shirone tossed the paper into the campfire.

As he watched the paper crumble into ash, Shirone’s eyes filled with firm resolve.

I’ve been tracing the past records of deaths to track it down. If my guess is correct…

It should be here, in Military Facility No. 48, located just two kilometers from the capital of the Republic of North Aymond.

Tomorrow, I’ll find them.

The unfortunate person who holds Lawslayer.

The second morning of the Holy War had dawned.

The previous day’s conference had ended in chaos due to Gustav’s outburst, and an additional session was scheduled by majority vote.

Gustav was intent on starting a world war, and the representatives of each nation had spent a sleepless night trying to prevent it.

“Then, though it’s unprecedented, let us begin the second session of the conference.”

At Uorin’s declaration, the representatives seated themselves around the round table, and the royal guards moved behind them with noticeably sharper killing intent than before.

“My apologies for yesterday. Being in such a distinguished place must’ve stirred up my youthful passions.”

Smōdo, the Internal Affairs King of Gustav and one of the Four Great Arts, bowed toward the round table on his way to his seat.

It was unexpected, but surely another calculated move conjured in Smōdo’s cunning mind.

“We were all a bit heated yesterday. Let’s put that behind us and try to have a more productive discussion today.”

“As expected of the empress of the Empire—so gracious.”

With a sycophantic smile, Smōdo sat down where the other Four Great Arts were already in place.

Kneeling with her eyes wide open was Natasha, and next to her sat a middle-aged man cross-legged, resting his chin on his hand.

This time, Uorin openly examined them.

Balkan, the God of War of Gustav’s Four Great Arts.

Bearded like a bandit, he was in his fifties but still boasted a rugged, muscular body.

But what was truly terrifying was his intellect.

A military genius of the continent who combined brains and brawn. It’s no exaggeration to say Havitz can wage war because of him.

Balkan met Uorin’s gaze and grinned, baring his teeth without even lifting his chin from his hand.

He has no intention of backing down.

Today’s conference would not be easy either. Now it was up to the rest of the world to decide how to confront Gustav.

A world war must be avoided if possible. But if they cross the line, there’s no need to grovel either.

Uorin had already established discreet guidelines with Jincheon and the leaders of the Seven Kings.

“Then I’ll announce the first item. While we were discussing the Valkyrie budget yesterday, there’s a more urgent issue that needs addressing.”

As the others nodded silently, Uorin looked Havitz square in the eye and said:

“So… are you really planning to go to war?”

It was a preemptive strike to seize the initiative—but Havitz was still Havitz.

“I already said I would, didn’t I? Didn’t we talk about it last night? Or what, did you beg for help or something?”

But Uorin now fully understood the kind of person she was dealing with.

“You do realize how many people will die, don’t you? Gustav won’t walk away unscathed either.”

“That’s fine. I’m not the one dying.”

Before Havitz’s overwhelming narcissism, Uorin decided to focus on results.

“In that case, the Gustav-affiliated forces currently assigned to the Valkyries will be absorbed by the remaining nations.”

“Hmm.”

For the first time, Smōdo rubbed his chin, and Balkan gave a meaningful nod and smirked.

Havitz XVII would never have made this decision himself, but his predecessor, Havitz XVI, had heavily invested in the Valkyries to prevent war with Heaven.

Abandoning the Valkyries would weaken the Empire’s power by one-seventeenth. Waging world war in this state… The tax rate required to maintain stable time, manpower, and material supply would be…

As Smōdo’s thoughts raced with terrifying speed, Havitz muttered disinterestedly:

“Then take them. I’ll manage without the Valkyries.”

Shock swept through the Holy War chamber.

Even Blitz and Enox, who had been observing human conflict with interest, turned their heads in confusion.

“Are you serious?”

Analyzing it objectively, if pushed this far, Gustav would usually be forced to retreat.

According to Kashan’s analysts, Gustav’s chances of victory were below ten percent.

“When you’ve gathered in a plaza like this…”

Havitz spoke irritably.

“You gotta do something, right? If you just sit around all day deciding things, the sun’ll go down. I gotta get home. I just want to do anything—fast. So stop dragging it out and let’s just go to war.”

No calculations, no strategies—just pure, chaotic desire to mess around.

Balkan shrugged his shoulders.

“Kehaha, as expected of Your Majesty. A brilliant decision. After all, when it comes to killing people, you gotta be ready to run out in the middle of a meal.”

Uorin’s gaze turned cold and still.

He said plaza, didn’t he?

Uorin was one of the people who had stayed longest in this vast plaza.

People waiting for appointments. People reading books. People watching the crowd.

But sometimes, people like this appear—

People who, for the sake of their own amusement, plunge everything into chaos without the slightest regard for others.

Like a child, Havitz said:

“Let’s have some fun.”

“……”

As the representatives of each nation sat in stunned silence, he stood up and grabbed the round table.

“Haven’t decided yet? Then let me settle it cleanly. From now on, the Gustav Empire will… hupp!”

Havitz tried with all his strength, but the heavy round table didn’t budge.

“Hahaha! Your Majesty, how could you leave me out of such fun?”

Balkan stepped up beside Havitz and grabbed the table.

“From this moment forth!”

The two exchanged a glance and nodded, then raised their arms high in unison.

“It’s war!”

With Balkan’s immense strength, the round table spun ten meters into the air. Natasha threw back her head and cackled.

As the table spun and crashed back down, the horrified faces of the other representatives peeked through.

“Let’s kill with a bang!”

As the table of the Holy War shattered with a resounding thud, for the first time, a deadly light flickered in Uorin’s eyes.

Extreme Evil.
