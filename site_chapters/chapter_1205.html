Chapter 1205:

"Hmm."

Armand shot him a skeptical look, making Yorahan hurriedly avert his gaze.

"Right, let’s eat. We need to eat."

He had no idea what kind of fish this was.

Of course, it was a familiar species to the Fireborn, but Armand had never bothered to name them.

She was likely experiencing the magic of fire’s flavor, yet she never acknowledged it.

After their humble meal, the two sat in silence, staring into the flickering campfire.

"What do you want to do?"

Armand asked.

"You said you wanted to change the world. Do you plan to reform the Fireborn and make them fight?"

"Fight…?"

Yorahan rested his chin on his knee again.

"I don’t believe violence changes anything. Many philosophers try to achieve peace through war. In some ways, they might be right. But in the end, that logic only applies to their own kingdom, their own city, their own families. On a larger scale, their reasoning is no different from sacrificing half to save the other half."

"And what’s wrong with that?"

Now that she trusted Yorahan, Armand finally voiced what she had kept inside.

"That’s just how life works. How are we any different? To eat, we have to kill something else."

Yorahan asked.

"Do you want to fight?"

"The Fireborn have been oppressed. Just because we’re passive, humans treated us like livestock. To be honest… maybe I’m more angry at the Fireborn than I am at humans. Strength that can’t protect anything—it’s meaningless."

"That’s not true."

Armand looked up and saw the certainty in Yorahan’s eyes.

"The ability to share each other’s hearts is a gift. I believe humanity’s future depends on the Fireborn. That’s why I chose to stay in the village."

Was that really true?

As Armand fell into thought, Yorahan continued.

"The logic of strength won’t change anything. Humans are just afraid—afraid of being devoured by something stronger. But if they could understand each other the way the Fireborn do, that fear would disappear, and true peace would come. The strong ones are you."

Armand smiled.

"Maybe you’re right."

For a moment, their eyes met. Yorahan took a deep breath and tried to speak.

"Hey, listen—"

"But."

Her sudden interruption made Yorahan choke on his words.

"Cough! Cough!"

Oblivious to his inner turmoil, Armand’s gaze sharpened.

"Even so, we can’t just sit back and accept it. You don’t understand, but the Fireborn have suffered terrible persecution from humans. If you knew what happened, you wouldn’t be able to forgive them either."

"…Yeah. I don’t know."

The pain of others.

"But that’s where I’ll start. I’ll create a world where Fireborn and humans can live together."

The fire had died down by the time Yorahan stretched and spoke again with a lighthearted expression.

"Let’s get some rest. We’ll head back early in the morning—the village will be worried."

Avoiding the water’s edge, Yorahan laid out his blanket near the cliff, giving Armand plenty of space.

The rough pebbles dug into his back, but the thrill he felt today made his bed feel as light as a cloud.

"I should’ve brought my notebook. I need to write this down first thing tomorrow."

As he replayed his thoughts in his head to avoid forgetting them, he sensed a presence.

"…What?"

Startled, Yorahan tried to sit up, but Armand pressed a hand against his chest.

"I know your heart."

His lips wouldn’t move, but his heartbeat pounded violently.

"This time, I’ll show you mine."

Yorahan remained silent, but his racing pulse said everything.

Small-world resonance.

As their minds merged once again, a shudder ran through Yorahan’s body.

"Ah…"

Maybe he was born for this moment alone.

It was something beyond love—an overwhelming emotion that made him think he wouldn’t mind vanishing into eternity like this.

"Let’s become one."

Having already shared everything, there was no need for pretense. The two transcended mere concepts and united completely.

A heart greater than the universe.

That realization became the most essential data that would one day lead Yorahan to the realm of the divine.

The artists from various nations were in the midst of their final rehearsals for the grand performance scheduled at 10 p.m.

El Kiana and Maya, who would be delivering the finale song, finished their duet with an impressive harmony.

Although Maya had claimed the climactic part, El Kiana, too, was a seasoned professional.

Pannier spoke up.

"You pass, for now."

The musicians let out a silent breath of relief, knowing how strict of a producer he was.

Reina, a member of the Ogent family and the pianist for the performance, practically collapsed onto the keys.

‘This is hell. Absolute hell.’

Pannier added,

"Of course, I'm only passing you under the assumption that the actual performance will be better than the rehearsal. Don't let your guard down."

"Yes, sir!"

Reina straightened up in a hurry.

"Excuse me."

The door opened, and someone entered.

The performers turned in shock, their expressions filled with disbelief.

Maya and Kayden spoke at the same time.

"Fermi?"

Now the King of Kesia, Fermi’s presence prompted Pannier to rush forward immediately.

"Your Majesty!"

"My apologies for arriving unannounced."

In truth, if a king was going somewhere, his arrival would have normally been announced through his aides.

"What brings you here..."

"I was just passing by and heard the music. It sounded pleasant, so I thought I'd take a look."

Fermi gave a casual excuse.

‘Cell Buster cannot be stopped. At least, not yet.’

The latest information from the Apocalypse still pointed to the destruction of humanity.

'Maya, Kayden, and...’

There were three people in this room alone who were key to understanding that great calamity.

"It's been a while. How have you been?"

Fermi raised a hand in greeting as he approached.

Maya hurriedly bowed her head in response.

"I'm doing well, Your Majesty."

"No need to be so formal. We're old classmates, aren't we? By the way, how have things been for you? Has anyone... dropped by?"

Kayden stepped in front of Maya, blocking his path.

"What do you want with Maya? Say it to me instead."

Having once been part of the Golden Wheel, Kayden knew Fermi never approached anyone without a reason.

And in most cases, that reason was—

‘For his own gain.’

Fermi studied Kayden and Maya for a moment before smiling.

"So, are you her manager now? Impressive. You've probably become her most successful fan."

"Just say what you came to say and leave."

"I'd like to have a private chat with Maya. If she accepts my proposal, she'll become even more famous."

Given the recent Arachne incident, the artists in the room immediately became wary at the mention of sponsorships.

"What do you think, Maya? Do you have some time?"

"Uh, well..."

Maya glanced toward Pannier for guidance.

Fermi, sensing her hesitation, spoke bluntly.

"Consider my position, won't you? I'm the king of a nation participating in the Holy War. I can't exactly be turned away."

At that moment, everyone understood the situation.

'No matter the country, a king is still a king. If we refuse him here, our lives could be ruined.'

Kayden bit his lip.

'Still as underhanded as ever. But I can't step in right now. If I interfere here...'

He didn’t know how Maya's life would be affected.

Even though he told himself to hold back, the moment Fermi reached out toward Maya, Kayden's right hand moved on its own.

‘Damn it! No!’

The performers in the room turned pale as they saw his fist flying toward the king’s jaw.

"Oh dear, that was close."

However, as if he had been expecting it, Fermi simply tilted his upper body back, effortlessly dodging the punch.

Kayden finally managed to stop himself, grabbing hold of his right hand.

"I... I..."

Fermi observed his panicked expression, his eyes turning cold.

‘So, this is a prison break.’

Even when Maya's entire career was on the line, Kayden had been unable to control his right hand.

‘For now, this is enough.’

Fermi stepped back with a grin.

"I'm just messing with you. You're so sensitive. I can't even put my arm around a fellow celebrity's shoulder?"

He turned to Maya.

"Sorry for the scare. I'm a fan of yours too, you know. I'll be looking forward to your finale performance."

Maya, who had been terrified at the possibility of a sponsorship proposal, still didn't seem completely at ease.

"Wasn't there something else you wanted to say?"

"It was just a joke. No one's going to touch you."

He considered mentioning that Shirone wouldn’t stand idly by, but Kayden was already tense enough.

Fermi turned away, his amused expression vanishing as he walked up to Pannier.

"Can we talk?"

"Ah... Yes, Your Majesty."

After granting the performers a break, Pannier led Fermi into a private room.

"Shall I bring you some tea?"

It had been a while since he had entertained someone of such high status, and he found himself awkwardly shuffling.

"No need. Please, take a seat."

At that moment, Maya’s voice drifted in from the rehearsal hall.

"I see the roles have changed. El Kiana must be upset."

Since this was a detail one wouldn’t know unless they had investigated beforehand, Pannier narrowed his eyes.

‘As expected, he’s no ordinary man. Even before he became king, they said he was the true power behind Kesia.’

Fermi asked,

"Was it a matter of skill, or was there another reason?"

If Maya was a key figure to the future, then any changes in her circumstances needed to be understood.

‘After all, outcomes are simply the sum of their causes.’

Pannier responded.

"As a producer, I’ve evaluated many artists. But what I’ve come to realize is that there is no perfect standard by which to judge a person."

"Oh?"

"Humans struggle to manage even their own lives. I’m no different. When I judge an artist, I try to see how much they put into their craft."

"That’s not exactly a precise standard, though, is it?"

"Especially not in the arts. But after all these years, I’ve come to a realization…"

Pannier let out a dry chuckle.

"In the end, that is the closest thing to the truth."

The truth.

Maya would be the one standing under the spotlight.

‘Perhaps this is the key hint to Cell Buster. But something still isn’t adding up.’

Fermi gave a nod.

"Apologies for coming unannounced. But I had to ask you something directly."

Since information regarding future events could not be shared with anyone, he had no choice but to investigate this way.

"Go ahead, Your Majesty."

"Are you familiar with a man named Rove Lanstein?"

"...Excuse me?"

It was an unexpected question.

"How do you know Lanstein?"

Why wouldn’t he know?

After all, the Guitar Man of the Seven Magical Sins was not known as an artist, but as a mage.

"I don’t know him personally. But I heard he was once part of your agency. Are you still in contact with him?"

"I see..."

It had been many years now, but Rove Lanstein was still a name Pannier could never forget.

"He was a huge part of my life. But I haven’t heard from him in a long time. I don’t even know if he’s alive or dead."

Fermi cut to the point.

"He’s alive."

So he was still out there.

‘So you’re still alive? Have you still not let go of that path? Are you still living that way?’

Pannier asked,

"Your Majesty, what do you think an artist is?"

Fermi hesitated.

"Well..."

It wasn’t a question that could be answered by someone unfamiliar with the field.

"Is there even a definition? It’s just another profession. Nothing special or grand about it. No matter how successful I become, I don’t call myself an artist. I call myself a musician—someone who makes a living through music."

Sensing a subtle distinction in his words, Fermi asked,

"Then what is an artist?"

Pannier answered.

"Life itself."

"Life?"

"People who live as art itself. No matter what the world says, they live exactly as they desire. Sometimes, a great artist is born. But nine times out of ten, they die as vagabonds, forgotten by the world."

A distant look crossed Pannier’s eyes.

"Rove Lanstein... lived closer to art than anyone else I’ve ever known."
