Chapter 1198:

Gustav Sector.

Balkan looked at Zetaro’s expression and immediately understood.

"He’s already dead."

Balkan himself was wounded and far from fine, but Zetaro’s face was as lifeless as a corpse.

"What about Natasha?"

"I sent her after Uorin. No point in letting her sit around. Besides, Havitz is out of our hands now."

"He walked right past me."

That was just like Havitz.

"You’re the game maker. Even if you can’t kill Havitz yourself, dragging Wizard into it was more than enough."

Zetaro let out a sigh and lowered his head. At that moment, Natasha entered the room.

She stuck out her tongue.

"Lost her."

"That woman isn’t ordinary. She won’t go down easily. But keep pressing her."

"Why? What happens to the Holy War doesn’t matter to us. We just need to kill Havitz."

"Exactly."

Zetaro raised his head as Balkan continued.

"Tag is just a small game. Sure, Wizard is strong, but if you asked me to bet on this, I wouldn’t put my money anywhere. My gambler’s instinct says it’s a fifty-fifty shot."

It was razor-close.

"But there’s no such thing as a draw in gambling. You either split the pot, or the stakes double. And tell me, is there anyone here who came just to break even? The stakes will rise. Havitz will throw the Holy War into complete chaos. That means we—"

Just then, an aide entered.

"Your Majesty, there’s a summons from the conference hall. An emergency gathering of all national representatives in twenty minutes."

"Understood."

Balkan looked at his friends.

"See? Even the other nations know it. If this continues, we’re all doomed."

As Balkan stood, Natasha moved to support him.

"You’re going?"

"Of course. Get your heads straight. Nothing is over yet. A gambler’s time comes when everyone else starts to tire. Natasha, go after Uorin."

His eyes gleamed with madness.

"Make sure, no matter what, she doesn’t attend the conference."

"Got it."

As Natasha left, Balkan changed his clothes and placed a firm hand on Zetaro’s shoulder.

"You’re not giving up already, are you?"

"Balkan, I…"

"You are the game maker. And not just any game maker—a brilliant one. Do you know why?"

Zetaro looked up.

"Because your rules are always fair. Even if someone loses, they have no room for excuses. That’s why Havitz loved your games."

"You created a game that even I, a gambler, can’t predict the outcome of. Understand? The one who truly excites him isn’t Wizard—it’s you."

Zetaro’s eyes regained a spark of life.

'Havitz.'

Was it just his imagination?

Four children from the neighborhood gathered, singing as they gazed up at a tall wall.

Havitz, Havitz…?

The sky was painted in twilight.

Havitz glared at Wizard.

"Huuhhh…"

The Stars of the Ivory Tower detected a faint trace of confusion on Havitz’s twisted face.

'What’s going on?'

Leader of the Ten Mages Bartok, had witnessed many warriors in battle, including Wizard,

'A technique that breaks Vanishing, trained by the Five Star. But Satan’s reaction is…'

Somehow, it looked sad.

"Havitz."

Wizard stepped closer.

"I love you. Stop running away now."

"Hmph."

He couldn’t believe it.

'You love me? Nonsense. It’s a lie. A trick to kill me.'

And yet, he couldn’t reject it outright.

'But… what if it’s true?'

That moment of hesitation—Wizard caught it and struck past a single frame.

"Urgh!"

Havitz tumbled across the floor. The Stars of the Ivory Tower furrowed their brows.

'What was that just now…? It felt like every human on Earth had shut their eyes at once.'

Armin understood.

'The pinnacle of spatial-temporal abilities. If Havitz has no fixed time, then that child erases space.'

Two beings, similar yet completely different.

"Huuh… huuh…"

Blood poured from Havitz’s nose and mouth as he glared at Wizard.

"You say you love me?"

"I do."

Havitz’s lips curled into a grin.

"Good."

The shadow of Hell Prison flickered—then, the next instant, both of them vanished from the room.

"… Are they gone?"

Even the Stars of the Ivory Tower couldn’t interfere with events happening beyond the laws of the world.

"Yes, most likely."

Armin pointed at the rattling door.

"A crack in space-time."

Havitz wasn’t fast.

But every time he moved, the action occurred outside of the laws of the world—outside the records of reality.

'Try and follow me.'

It wasn’t about speed or slowness.

"Kuuh…!"

Just then, Wizard surpassed another frame and landed a blow on Havitz’s ribs.

"Are you just going to keep running?"

She wanted to snap his neck right there. But too much killing intent would break the rules of the game.

"Kehehe…"

Havitz chuckled.

"Aren’t you being a little harsh? Honestly, I’m starting to have doubts. Do you really love me?"

'What’s your move, then?'

"I do. As long as I don’t break your fingers, it remains true."

That was the rule.

"Alright, in that case…"

Closing the distance through Hell Prison, Havitz grabbed Wizard and pulled her to the ground.

Wizard clenched her fists, filled with a murderous urge, but she couldn’t bring herself to strike back.

'No, I can’t.'

If she attacked Havitz now, her declaration of love might turn into a lie.

'Or… since I haven’t broken his fingers, it’s fine. Havitz will believe it to the end.'

Will he, though?

She wasn’t sure. If she miscalculated, the Satan she had so carefully trapped within the system would slip away once more.

'How much does Havitz really love me?'

She had no way of knowing.

In the end, neither of them truly knew the other. Their odds remained a perfect fifty-fifty.

Havitz stood up.

"Alright, so your love for me is a ‘fact.’"

That meant he had to allow Wizard even stronger strikes in the future. But he had no choice but to trust in the game.

As he stepped back, his face looked slightly exhausted, slightly unsettled.

'What do I even want to do with this girl? Something inside me has been filled, but…'

At the same time, the void inside him only felt larger.

"Come find me anytime."

Leaving those words behind, Havitz sprinted down the corridor. Wizard bit her lip.

She barely managed to hold back the sob threatening to escape and replayed the situation in her mind.

'I couldn’t fight back.'

She had earned his trust for now, but as their encounters continued, the intensity would only grow.

Love and violence in an equal exchange.

'How long…'

Perhaps forever.

Because no one can ever truly know another person’s truth.

"What is truth?" Great Star asked.

"Xing, the truth is simply fact. The fact that I have protected humanity until now—that is the truth. You must believe in me."

"Shut up."

Even as the armies of Hell ravaged their way up the Ivory Tower, Xing remained unfazed.

"The demons are ascending, Xing. This is your last chance. If I die, so does intelligence itself."

"No, it’s quite the opposite. It’s you who’s preventing human intelligence from expanding beyond its limits."

The barrier of the outer world.

"Don’t be so stubborn. Some things don’t need to be proven. Humanity has loved each other all this time, and we have flourished because of it. Do you deny even that?"

"Yes."

Xing squeezed her soul dry as she answered.

"The idea that physical connection is ‘beautiful’—that too is just part of the system you created. No matter how much flesh intertwines, no matter how many fluids are exchanged, we can never truly know the truth."

"Why does it even matter? We’ve lived in harmony for so long without issue."

"It matters now. This is a question of humanity’s survival. If you want to live, then convince me. Prove it."

"But there’s mental magic. Humans can directly perceive each other’s minds."

"That’s just another system. Drimo, the Under World—it’s all under the Administrator’s control. There is no way to directly verify what you call the ‘mind.’ Therefore, logically speaking, the only being in this world who truly possesses a mind… is me."

"That argument is weak."

"But you can’t refute it, can you? That’s the problem. If there’s no contradiction, someone has to uphold it. At the very least, in humanity’s final hour—Omega Year 999."

"And just how do you expect me to prove it? There’s no way to physically show a person’s mind!"

"That’s exactly why I can’t believe in it. No one can ever truly know another’s truth. It’s better to believe in myself than in some administrator from beyond the outer world. That applies to all of humanity."

Even as she spoke, a small doubt crept in.

'Is this truly the right path?'

Even for someone with the strongest willpower in human history, her conviction was beginning to crack.

She gritted her teeth.

'Don’t waver. Believe in yourself. If we can’t share our minds, then I am everything.'

"Sharing… minds?"

Yorahan’s expression went blank.

"Yes. It’s called Small World Integration. It connects us to everything in existence. When minds are united, a new world unfolds."

"Even with something like a rock?"

The Fireborn laughed.

"We told you, everything in the world can be assimilated. Grass, trees, wind, earth, birds, squirrels… Of course, each bloodline has its own natural affinities."

"What does the earth feel like?"

"Hmm… how should I put it? It feels like absolute safety. Like being protected without needing to offer anything in return."

The Fireborn closed their eyes.

Watching their lips slowly curl into smiles, Yorahan thought to himself.

'Sharing minds?'

The reason people struggle to be considerate is because of distrust and fear toward each other.

'But they’re different. They don’t doubt, because they’re all connected.'

A sudden thought struck him.

'If the human heart is a hell…'

Then maybe the Fireborn were the only hope of pulling humanity out of the abyss.

"Oh my, why that face? Yorahan, you’re quite an unusual man."

"This is incredible."

"Huh?"

"It might take time, but someday, all humans will welcome you."

The Fireborn blushed.

"Do you really… think so?"

"Of course! I guarantee it. Oh, and—could I try Small World Integration too?"

"Of course. Just a moment."

Right then, Armand cut in.

"Stop."

"Why? What’s wrong?"

After three days together, they had grown more familiar, but he still found it difficult to deal with her.

"I warned you. If you try anything foolish, I won’t stand by."

"But it’s not dangerous."

"It depends on who you are. If you harbor even the slightest ill intent, you’ll hurt her."

"I don’t have any bad intentions."

"You can’t be sure."

"That’s why we should test it."

When Yorahan stubbornly clammed up, Armand let out a sigh and stood up.

"Fine, I’ll let it go."

Armand turned to leave as well.

"Where are you going?"

"To my room. I want to jot down what I’ve been thinking about."

"Not now. We’re heading to the mountains."

"Huh? Me?"

"We’re low on supplies. If I go hunting, I won’t be able to keep an eye on you, so you’re coming with me."

"Wait, that’s not—"

Just then, a sharp gust of wind whistled through the air.

"Uwah! What was that?"

Yorahan stumbled backward as a longsword suddenly flew into Armand’s grasp.

"H-How did you do that?"

Fastening the blade to her waist, she answered.

"Precision Control."
