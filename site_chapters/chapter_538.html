Chapter 538:

“Ra’s avatar! Grant us eternal life! A body that does not perish!”

As the roar of voices spread beyond the city, the officers scanned the ground for any signs of movement from the Undergrounders.

Not wanting to sour the mood, the High Priest stepped forward and smoothly continued the ceremony.

“We will now appoint the Arbiter of this settlement.”

“This cannot be allowed!”

The previous Arbiter shouted, his eyes filled with a mix of fear and rage.

“That boy is an Ancient! A slave of ours! How can the great Children of the Sun be ruled by a slave?!”

The people responded with murmurs of unrest.

To the Children of the Sun, light was their sole sustenance. But the idea of being ruled overnight by a slave was understandably hard to swallow.

“Think about it! How can one not born of the Sun wield its light? It’s a trick—this isn’t the language of the gods! And if his power vanishes tomorrow, what will you rely on to survive the darkness?”

As the crowd began to sway, a gaunt man with bat-like wings stepped up to the High Priest.

It was Exer, one of the Guardians.

“High Priest. Did you see the divine language in that boy?”

While the High Priest responded, Shirone approached the former Arbiter, attempting to de-escalate the situation.

“Look, I don’t want anything from this duel. As long as no one means harm, I’ll stay here in the settlement…”

Before he could finish, the Arbiter’s head was severed from his body.

Shirone looked up to see Exer floating upside-down in the air, arms crossed in an X with twin blades.

Landing lightly as if he had always been walking on the ground, Exer strode to the edge of the ceiling and declared to the people:

“The Arbiter of this settlement has now been decided. If there are any objections, bring them to me.”

“Wooooooaaaaaahhh!”

The crowd erupted in unified cheers.

The Sun must always exist, and for them, there was no real choice.

Shirone, however, was far from pleased.

Just an hour ago, the very same man had been revered like the sun—only to be beheaded in an instant. That was the fate of an Arbiter.

‘I’ll accept it for now. If I want to survive here, I’ll need to use whatever I can.’

Back in the temple, Shirone sat on the throne.

The four Guardians stood in pairs on either side of him, while the High Priest bowed and began explaining the Arbiter’s responsibilities.

Not that it was difficult work.

The slaves handled all the menial tasks; Shirone only needed to keep the settlement lit with his light magic.

What he gained in return was absolute authority.

Like ants who devoted their lives to a queen with no other ability than reproduction, as long as light bathed the settlement, Shirone would reign in untouchable divinity.

‘I’ve gained the power to make things happen. But I can’t live out my life as a glorified lightbulb. If I want to survive—’

He would have to rebuild human civilization.

And that, in this ruined world, was perhaps the greatest goal one man could pursue.

Shirone turned to the High Priest and asked,

“What’s your name?”

“I am Bebeto, born with the ability to see the divine language.”

Bebeto explained how Guardians were born.

‘Hmm. Mutations, from the Tree of Life…’

Following the High Priest, the Guardians each introduced themselves. The last was a massive man who thumped his chest.

“I am Targan, a Guardian born with the power of detonation.”

“Detonation?”

Targan rolled up his sleeve and demonstrated.

As he clenched his fist, his muscles hardened like stone, opening small crater-like vents.

“Hngh!”

With a puff, gas erupted from the vents, and flames burst around his arm.

“This is a weaker demonstration, out of respect for the Arbiter. But against Undergrounders, it’s much more potent. Leave the fighting to me.”

Satisfied with the Guardian’s capabilities, Shirone nodded and descended the steps.

“Exer, was it?”

Shirone’s eyes gleamed sharply.

“Yes. Do you have a command for me?”

“From now on, no matter the situation, do not act without my orders.”

He was referring to Exer’s assassination of the Arbiter.

“But there cannot be two Suns in one settlement…”

“I know that. I’m saying that before making irreversible decisions, you must think carefully. For example, he could’ve been used as a bargaining chip in negotiations with hostile factions.”

There were no such things as negotiations in the settlement’s system.

But since the Arbiter’s word was law, Exer accepted the order without protest.

“I understand.”

Shirone said nothing more—for better or worse, it had handed him power.

“The night is deep. Go and rest. I’ll begin official duties in the morning.”

Just as they had appeared, the four Guardians vanished like the wind, and Bebeto faded after linking his code.

Left alone on the throne, Shirone rested his chin on his hand, deep in thought.

‘I’ve got a lot to do. If I want to achieve my goal, I’ll have to tighten control. Time to steel myself.’

The Arbiter’s position, revered by all the Children of the Sun, meant only one thing to Shirone—survival.

In the depths of a dark underground facility, dozens of monitors flickered on simultaneously.

Not even an insect could survive in this place—the source of the Mucus. Yet a shadow shaped like a human passed between the monitors.

“Energy mass gauge confirmed. Current reserves: 428%.”

A deep voice and the sound of keystrokes filled the silence.

In response, the computer called up a wall of program code across the screens.

“Activating purification system. Code number 387.”

With a click of the Enter key, thousands of lines of code scrolled endlessly upward.

Fan motors filled the room with sound, and the monitors pulsed with electric light.

“Complete. Program terminated.”

All the monitors shut off at once, and the silhouette vanished into the darkness.

Ten minutes later, hundreds of projectiles launched from every corner of the city, jetting upward into the sky.

Ssssshhhhhhh!

As if the sky itself had been pierced, rain poured down early in the morning.

Thick black clouds blotted out the sun, but the people of the settlement poured outside in joyous cheers.

“Rain! It’s raining! Finally, it rains!”

“Oh, great Ra! Bless us!”

The Children of the Sun opened their mouths to catch the life-giving water, while the slaves rushed outside with large containers.

“Mmm mmm! Hm hm hm~”

Yoa was in a fantastic mood from the start of the day.

Not only because it was the first rain in sixty days—but also because today marked the rise of a new Arbiter.

Humming a tune with no discernible melody, Yoa finished getting ready and checked her face in the mirror-like palm of her hand, which had transformed into silver. Then, a heavy knock boomed on the door.

“Hey! What are you doing in there? Didn’t you hear the Arbiter’s orders?”

“I heard. I’m coming out now.”

Outside the door, all three Guardians were gathered.

Seeing Yoa’s fresh clothes and lightly painted eyes, Targan frowned.

“What’s this? Been photosynthesizing too long? Why do you look like that?”

“Mind your business. Let’s go—we’re going to be late for the morning assembly.”

“You’re the one who’s late.”

As Yoa stepped out, Targan grumbled behind her back.

On the way up to the temple, they saw the High Priest Bebeto hurrying up the path. Karof called out to him.

“It’s raining.”

“Haha, surely Ra welcomes the new Arbiter. But… are you only just now coming up?”

“Yes, we were summoned in a rush.”

Bebeto clicked his tongue.

“So was I. There’s not much to do in the settlement, but the Arbiter wants assemblies every morning. Between that and what he told Exer yesterday, this Arbiter is certainly… unique.”

No one dared mention Shirone’s origins, and Karof smoothly changed the subject.

“Still, the rain is late this time. On average, it used to come once every thirty days, but lately it’s been more delayed.”

There were no seas in Apocalypse—as anyone could guess from the skyfish. No one really understood why it rained.

Much like how humans bloomed from the Tree of Life, they simply accepted it as another miracle of Ra.

“The Sun’s Guardians answer the Arbiter’s summons.”

The Guardians entered the temple and bowed before the throne.

The slaves who typically served the Arbiter were nowhere to be seen. Shirone alone greeted them with a stern gaze.

“He seems like a different person than yesterday. Even in the ancient era, he must not have been ordinary,” thought Bebeto.

Then Shirone spoke.

“From today onward, I will take an active role in governing the settlement. All Guardians will attend the morning assembly and report results.”

Karof spoke with an apologetic expression.

“But the settlement is peaceful. There's no need for the great avatar of Ra to burden himself…”

Shirone cut him off.

“How many settlements are in this city?”

The Guardians glanced at one another before the High Priest stepped in.

“It’s not exact, but we estimate three.”

“Estimate? You live in the same city and don’t know how many settlements there are?”

“The Children of the Sun follow only Ra’s will. As long as there is another avatar of Ra in those settlements, we have no reason to interfere.”

‘It really is like an ant colony,’ Shirone thought.

Even among ants of the same species, their focus was not the species but the queen.

“Then from now on, we’ll make contact. Guardians, find the nearest settlement and inform them that the Arbiter of this one seeks negotiation.”

Targan lifted his head.

“I have no intention of disobeying your order… but why must we do that?”

In a world where control is everything, the meeting of two Suns was bound to be awkward.

But Shirone intended to change this world.

“I’m proposing an alliance. We can’t stay trapped in this settlement forever. Out there, there are devices capable of generating light. But trying anything now would be too risky. I plan to join forces with the other settlements and accomplish that goal.”

Exer spoke.

“You alone are our light, Arbiter. We need no other.”

That may be true for the Children of the Sun.

But what about the Ancients?

Including those who had been brainwashed and the countless others still asleep in cryogenic capsules—none of them could truly be happy in this world.

“Are you saying you won’t follow my order?”

Realizing their mistake, the Guardians quickly bowed their heads.

Before the future of their race—the Sun—personal doubts were a luxury they could not afford.

“Our apologies. Then, who shall we send? Please issue the command and we will risk our lives to fulfill it.”

“It’s dangerous outside, so we should send as many as possible. Three Guardians will handle the negotiations, and the remaining one will accompany me.”

The first part of the order made sense. The second did not.

“Accompany you? Where to, my lord?”

“Starting today, I will investigate the city. The sun is up during the day, so there’s no harm in leaving the settlement.”

The High Priest cried out in alarm.

“You mustn’t! It’s too dangerous! If something were to happen to you, the settlement would—urk!”

He flinched at the fierce gleam in Shirone’s eyes.

What he had forgotten was that this Arbiter not only wielded light but possessed formidable combat strength.

‘Right… he’s someone who wouldn’t lose even to the Guardians. What do we do now…?’

The Guardians also looked troubled.

No matter how strong he was, it was unprecedented for the most protected figure in the settlement to leave on his own.

“I’ll escort the Arbiter!”

Breaking the tension, Yoa stood up with determination.

“Leave it to me! Whether it’s Undergrounders or Mucus, I’ll protect the Arbiter no matter what!”

“Hm.”

At last, Shirone gave a satisfied nod, and Yoa smiled faintly to herself.
