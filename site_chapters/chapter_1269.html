Chapter 1269:

A shabby underground room.

Iruki was carefully filling precious paper with rows of numbers when Dorothy entered with tea.

“Drink this.”

Though the other survivors couldn’t even dream of tasting it, it was nothing more than a simple brew made from common herbs.

Soothed by the warmth of the tea, Iruki let out a sigh and set down his pen.

“It’s a dire situation.”

“I don’t know. Calling it dire almost sounds like a joke now.”

Humanity had been annihilated—literally.

One might expect to feel a strange peace in such times, but Iruki couldn’t allow himself to relax.

“Isn’t there a way to contact the ground forces?”

Jin Seoug Eum had done everything she could to evacuate everyone, but some had chosen to stay behind.

“You know it—going up means instant death. They stayed knowing that.”

Iruki looked down at the documents.

‘Those left on the surface.’

The data had been compiled by mobilizing every available information system, but accuracy wasn’t guaranteed.

Still, the number was substantial, and Iruki quickly filtered out the key individuals.

‘Ikael. Ashur. Wizard. Son Yujeong. Kira. Eden. And…’

Adrias Miro.

‘A battle of good and evil. If Miro is fleeing, that means evil has won…’

Iruki asked, “How’s Gaold?”

“From what I’ve heard, he’s finally calmed down a bit. Though, of course, no one dares get close.”

“I see.”

Iruki buried his face in his hands and thought.

‘Can they survive this?’

According to the intel, the surface was no longer a place any living being could inhabit.

Dust storms created from the collapse of the world’s structures had brought visibility to zero.

Havitz’s Death Field was rapidly consuming the land, and demons would be hunting for any survivors.

“This place isn’t safe either.”

“Even if it’s underground, it’s still a part of the real world. If the core gets completely burned, passageways will open down here too.”

Iruki nodded.

“Above all else… Cell Buster.”

According to Xing, the power of the Taiji would only last, at most, twelve more hours.

Dorothy asked, “Can it be stopped? It’s one of the most extreme codes that can be used from the Outer World.”

“We have to stop it.”

Iruki rose to his feet.

“We can’t rely on Shirone. For the first time in his life, he’ll be fighting for himself.”

As his friend, Iruki wanted to help however he could.

“The only ones we can count on now are…”

The door opened.

“Guitar Man.”

Rove Lanstin and Taira Lyn were waiting.

In the area where the survivors were gathered, the artists had arranged a small concert.

Reina’s piano melodies and the vocals of Maya and El Kiana harmonized beautifully.

Even Rai, Kayden, and the other soldiers could forget reality, if only for a moment.

When the performance ended, someone spoke.

“Bravo.”

“Master Panier.”

Once the most famous musician in the world, he now stood among the people, clapping.

El Kiana asked, “Where have you been? They said a famous artist arrived. Did you know?”

“Of course I did. That guitarist I told you about before—it’s him. Robe Ranstin.”

Maya grew curious.

“The one who stuck to his music until the end, right? Then why isn’t he here? I’d love to hear him.”

“He didn’t come here to play music.”

“Then why?”

Panier hesitated.

Fermi had given him a rough explanation, but he still found it hard to believe.

The Seven Magical Sin.

As he sat down, the artists gathered around.

“Artists are sensitive to being called lazy. Truth is, people like us look like bums if we’re not pretending to be doing something every day.”

The survivors chuckled softly and listened.

“In my view, that kid always sat there in a daze, lost in thought. He’d pluck a few strings, then fall back into silence. I got frustrated and pushed him hard. Told him to bring me a new piece every day. If he failed, I’d cut off our contract and support.”

El Kiana asked, “So did he bring them?”

“Of course. And they were quite good, too.”

“I was pleased. I’d proven my point. If I could turn that lazy musician into a workhorse, it’d be a success. So I kept pushing. More, more, more. Then one day, he just stopped bringing pieces. Didn’t even show up. I wasn’t disappointed… if I’m honest…”

I felt guilty.

“It was a forced march. The moment he stepped into my studio, he threw hundreds of pages of sheet music in my face and screamed like a madman.”

Panier looked around at everyone.

“I’m not here to say artists should or shouldn’t be lazy. What I want to say is: when we apply the ‘standards’ we’ve agreed upon to individuals, it can become so cruel it wounds their very soul.”

He should’ve respected him for who he was.

“That kid must’ve been fighting alone. Against me, the world, the gaze of others. He fulfilled what I demanded every day, just to prove he was right. But thinking about it now… why did he have to? Couldn’t we have allowed that guitarist, just one person, the freedom to fail? Couldn’t we have let him protect at least his own soul?”

Panier admitted:

“And now, the Robe Ranstin I once spoke of is dead. The one here now is Guitar Man, the killer of Seven Magical Sins. His crimes are so absurd, they make even his tragic past seem like a joke. There’s no room for sympathy. But…”

Did he really have to fight this way?

“I doubt he pities this wreck of a world either…”

His voice trailed off.

“That… is the part I find saddest.”

Shirone asked, “A fight for me?”

“Yes.”

Having lost everything, was there truly anything left for him to fight for?

Fermi spoke.

“When we first extracted future intel from the Apocalypse, we only planned to use it. You did well. In the end, the world ended according to the god’s outcome. Omega concluded.”

Shirone stayed silent.

“And thus, the world perished. That’s fine. But in truth, we knew the world would end. We preserved the future, even restricting your access to the intel.”

“Why?”

It was time to ask the question again—the one he’d first asked back in the room.

“Because that’s the only way it could be changed.”

“I felt something was off. We mined future ruin from the Apocalypse. That’s someone’s belonging—someone who must exist in the future, not the present. That means… for any outcome to occur, every cause must align. And so…”

Fermi raised his index finger.

“In that final future world you visited in the Apocalypse—who do you think recorded the data stored in the Core?”

“Well…”

Shirone blinked.

“Exactly. It wasn’t God. God is only the result itself. God doesn’t record the past. That future, where the past was stored, exists only because someone recorded that past.”

“Who?”

Shirone asked again.

“Who was the recorder?”

The one who documented everything up until the conclusion of Omega still exists in this world.

“The Freemason.”

Fermi turned around.

“Let’s go. You’ll probably know the guy well.”

At some point, Jin Seoug Eum had gotten down from the bed and was preparing to cast an Ether Wave again.

Seriel spoke.

“Go ahead. Just make sure Seoug Eum’s condition stays stable. I’ll be preparing things here.”

Seeing her so frail, Shirone asked,

“Can’t we go to the surface? With my magic…”

“No. There’s no way. The place we’re going to is a completely sealed space—no entrance, no exit.”

“No door?”

What Shirone couldn’t immediately grasp was how anyone had survived there in the first place.

“You’ll see when we get there.”

As Fermi finished speaking, Seoug Eum activated her magic.

When the Ether Wave pulled the space into view, a pitch-black wall of darkness loomed before them.

“Be careful. It looks straight ahead, but it’s actually a vertical structure,” said Nade.

He flew swiftly into the darkness, and Shirone followed behind.

Hup.

Falling with his limbs outstretched, he spotted a faint sparkle far below.

It’s incredibly deep.

Using a Fly spell to land, Shirone looked around. Fermi and Seoug Eum approached.

“This way.”

As they walked, Shirone felt a strong electromagnetic field—stronger than the air itself.

Woooooong...

As they got closer to the light source, the visibility expanded to a roughly 20-meter radius.

“It’s… been a while… Shi… rone…”

“Richard.”

The face of Ainka Richard, who had once been a member of the Golden Wheel during their academy days, floated in the air.

Shirone’s face twisted into a grimace.

Below the neck, there was nothing. Only bundles of wires stretched and spread out in every direction.

“You… how did you end up like this…”

Richard adjusted his voice module.

“It’s fine. It was my choice. Honestly, even this face is artificial. Only my mind has been transmitted here. You could say the real me is already dead. Then again… maybe saying I’m ‘dead’ is weird since I’m still here, right?”

It felt like a prototype of the Outer World.

“You were the one who recorded Omega?”

The lack of humanity in the final record made sense—because the one who recorded it was no longer fully human.

“Yeah. The final version you received was my data. But that’s an old achievement.”

Fermi explained.

“Richard’s family, the Ainka, runs the second-largest alchemy foundation in the world. It was established by the descendants of those called the Freemasons. Their goal is to ensure humanity’s eternal existence through science—Scientology.”

The sum of all those causes had led to the Outer World’s result.

Richard spoke.

“Who knows. Maybe someday I’ll become a god and create a world for the Illuminati. Who can say? But what’s clear is that since you’ve met me, Shirone, it means you still have a chance.”

“What do you mean…?”

Someone stepped out from the surrounding darkness.

Mirak Minerva, one of the Five Stars of the Ivory Tower, and the Four-Star-ranked “Holy Brain” appeared.

“Minerva…”

Shirone was relieved she was safe, but she looked at him with apologetic eyes.

“You’ve suffered a lot, haven’t you?”

“Well, you know…”

The Holy Brain, Adam, appeared in the form of a Gaia being and approached.

“Yahweh. Descendant of Goffin. All of this is my fault. Please, give me a chance to undo it.”

Again… for humanity?

“We can bring Amy back.”

Shirone turned at Minerva’s words.

“What?”

Richard repeated it.

“We can bring Amy back, Shirone. If you’re willing to fight again, we’ll make it possible.”

His heart pounded, but his head was full of doubt.

“How is that possible?”

“By reversing history.”

Fermi said.

“Use tachyons to create a cause where Amy doesn’t die. It’s a fight for you.”

“That’s a contradiction.”

Shirone’s thoughts quickened.

“No matter what illusion you believe, it’s still just an illusion. If I go back and save Amy, what happens to this place? Will she suddenly pop up here? Will Nade suddenly change?”

No.

“The world where Amy survives would be a completely different universe. Just one of the many multiverses. Sure, if I abandon my heart, I could live there. But if I’ve already let go of my heart—then why does it have to be Amy? This is the same thing the gods tried to do to us.”

All that mattered was producing a result.

“This is different from protecting the heart with Ouroboros. This is asking me to change my heart for the sake of a law. I can’t do that. I’d never be able to come back to this place.”

“What if you could return?”

At Richard’s question, Shirone turned.

“If you could return to this place—where your heart belongs—Shirone, would you fight again?”

“In a multiverse of infinite worlds…”

Where even a single finger could spawn infinite patterns—

“How could I find this one?”

Richard answered.

“Philosophy’s End.”

A fixed and immutable coordinate in this universe, planted here by Amitabha Vanya and Veron of the Council of Ten.
