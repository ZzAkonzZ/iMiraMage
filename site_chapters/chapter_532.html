Chapter 532:

Rian, Fermi, and Marsha followed behind Miro, stepping through the veil of sunset.

Lines of blue light streaked vertically across a space as dark as the night sky, and glimmers of starlike brilliance cascaded down along those lines.

“I don’t feel like we’re falling,” Rian said.

“To be precise,” Fermi replied, “we're not falling—we're being transmitted as information. Gravity doesn’t apply here.”

Rian walked over to where Miro was standing.

“So this is the Under Coder?”

“You could call it the entrance. Since we’ve left Dreamo, our information is now classified as Minority Conception. Once the effects of Dream Star wear off, we’ll begin to disintegrate.”

“Isn’t that dangerous?”

“No. As long as our Avatars remain intact, we won’t merge with other information. We’ll descend in our original state.”

“What about Arius?”

Come to think of it, the blue-furred dog was nowhere to be seen.

“He’s not an Avatar. He was just a projection manifested through our mental channel. Looks like his consciousness couldn’t reach this far.”

Miro shrugged it off.

“Doesn’t matter. He’ll find a way to contact us. And if he can’t—well, that’s life.”

As the effects of Dream Star wore off, the four of them began to disintegrate into sparkling particles.

Their limbs dissolved first, followed by their torsos and heads—until only their faces remained. At that moment, Fermi said,

“See you at the mid-layer.”

“What does th—”

Before Marsha could finish her sentence, all four of them dissolved completely into light and descended along the stream of the Under Coder.

“Ow, my head…”

When Miro groaned and came to, Rian, Fermi, and Marsha all wore similarly pained expressions.

“What do you mean by mid-layer?”

Fermi looked around and answered, “This is it.”

They stood in a square room about 100 pyeong wide, filled with hundreds of mirrors.

“This is the mid-layer of the Under Coder, a virtual space created by humans. It’s made from recycled information discarded from Dreamo—a compact world of its own. Any human whose Avatar remains intact is filtered through here.”

Standing among the footprints of humanity stamped into the Under Coder, Marsha found herself freshly reminded of how obsessed humans were with exploration.

“So what are all these mirrors for?” she asked.

“They’re links—gateways to the virtual worlds built by different designers. The mirrors follow a communication protocol they created.”

“So each mirror connects to a different virtual world? What kind of worlds are there?”

Fermi activated his Depreciation Contract.

A chip shaped like a honeycomb pattern of four horizontal and four vertical lines passed down his throat.

“Information Magic: Information. It decodes the mid-layer’s protocol into real-world language.”

When he expanded his Spirit Zone, the names of the virtual worlds and the designer code names appeared on the mirror surfaces in plain text.

“The mirrors here at the entrance link to the most popular virtual worlds at the moment. I’ll read a few out—see if any catch your interest.”

Fermi listed the names and creator aliases:

The Taste of Humans — ID: Pig Who Poops Love Story Between Dog and Human — ID: Animal Lover Missing a Few Limbs? No Problem — ID: Just a Head Left

“Stop!” Marsha held up her hand, face full of disgust.

It seemed absurd at first glance, but the names clearly evoked things that would never be allowed in the real world.

‘Cannibalism, bestiality, mutilation... that’s what these worlds are about.’

What made it worse were the designers’ code names—clear signs they felt no guilt whatsoever.

“It’s a circus of Minority Conception,” she said. “If you go in, can you actually do those things?”

“Some people just watch. Others actually play. It’s about fifty-fifty.”

“And I assume it’s not free?”

Fermi chuckled at her question.

The very fact she assumed they were doing it for profit just proved Marsha was still sane.

“Unfortunately, 80% of the content is free. Most of the designers are lunatics who’ve completely given up on reality. For paid content, you can use Dream Star. That’s all they care about, and it can be converted into currency back in the real world.”

“I get the gist,” Miro said. “So which one do we go into?”

“No matter which link you take, the information will continue to fall. Whatever path you choose, the final destination is the Abyss.”

“Then let’s go with that one,” Miro said, pointing to a large mirror.

Fermi used Information magic to read the world.

The Dead Don’t Speak — ID: Squishy Corpse

It was a world built around playing with dead bodies. Fermi judged it suitable for beginners.

“Entering through a link is simple. It’s a reflection-world mechanism. Just place your hand on the mirror like this…”

Fermi vanished the moment his hand touched the surface.

“Huh, that is easy.”

When Miro placed her palm on the mirror, her reflection swapped places with her real self, and the scenery flipped. It was like the reflection had always been the real her.

She realized then what the principle of a reflection-world really meant.

“Convenient. This way, you can transmit entire information sets.”

Fermi, waiting on the other side, shrugged.

“Exactly.”

Rian and Marsha joined them shortly after, and the group began descending through the virtual worlds of the mid-layer.

Each world was wildly different in size, structure, and theme—but all of them shared one trait: they were horrific.

Screams, revolting acts, the twisted faces of people driven mad by pleasure or pain—none of it seemed human anymore.

By the fortieth world, even Marsha, a Regulation Exception user, was mentally exhausted.

Miro, with her training in non-attachment, could endure it. But even she was caught off guard by how casually Fermi hummed to himself, clearly enjoying it.

Miro glanced at him and asked, “You seem used to this place. How often do you visit the Under Coder?”

“Not anymore. I practically lived here when I was twelve. Haven’t felt much interest lately, though.”

‘Twelve, huh…’

Though she hadn’t seen her nephew in twenty years, Miro was now certain of one thing—Fermi was not a normal person.

Completely unfazed by the screaming around them, Fermi continued, “I was actually a designer here once. My world was called Vengeance is Mine. It let people create their enemies and enact any revenge they wanted. You were one of my favorite targets.”

Even if it was virtual, walking beside the person who once programmed your virtual twin and tortured it would be unsettling for anyone.

But Miro just burst out laughing.

“Hohoho! Bet that got you a lot of attention?”

“Not really. At first, it made it into the top 100 mid-layer worlds, but the guest count dropped. Since there weren’t any updates, the world eventually sank into the Abyss.”

“Too bad.”

“At the time, I was pissed. I thought, ‘How do I make something popular?’ But then I realized something. Was it actually fun torturing and killing you? And I realized... it wasn’t. You know why?”

Miro tapped her chin in mock thought. “Hmm… because it wasn’t real?”

“No.”

Fermi stopped walking and turned to her.

“Because I knew it wasn’t real.”

Only the trapped is truly real.

Once the lid is lifted, all concepts spill out and mutate into something else entirely.

“The moment you think, ‘I can leave anytime,’ all sensation becomes fake. That’s when I stopped coming here. Places like this don’t suit a realist like me.”

‘You can leave anytime.’

Miro rolled the phrase over in her mind.

In these manmade virtual worlds, everyone knows that truth. But in this reality—no one, not even herself, could truly believe it.

“We’re here. This is the last virtual world.”

The link led to a world where people surgically removed body parts and swapped them with others.

Though no one could quite understand why, the people missing their limbs were enraptured with bliss, and a man with multiple arms wore a satisfied smile, completely absorbed in self-admiration.

After leaving that world behind, the group finally arrived at a dimly lit room with a single mirror installed in its center.

Fermi cast Information magic and began decoding the inscription.

-Warning. This is the exit of the Mid-Layer. If you do not wish to go to the Abyss, wake up from the dream now.

“…That’s what it says.”

“If we want to wake up, do we just wait around?” Marsha asked.

“There’s a specific method,” Fermi explained. “Anyone who frequents the Under Coder knows it. But that’s only possible because this is a human-made world. The Abyss is an automatic convergence of Minority Conception. No information there will accommodate us.”

Fermi offered a final word of caution.

“Once we enter the Abyss… I don’t know how to come back. In fact, there might not be a way back at all.”

“There is,” Miro said.

“My friend came back. So there must be a way.”

Of course, she didn’t bother to mention that said friend had returned to that very place once again.

Marsha asked, “Before we go, I want to be clear—what exactly is Shirone’s state in the Abyss? If it’s an entirely different world, adaptation might be an issue. I doubt there’s a magic school there.”

“Information bonding patterns are unpredictable,” Fermi said. “But the Abyss would combine her into the form most suited to that world. If there’s a magic school, she’d most likely be a student there.”

“Hm… So basically, she exists in a configuration of the most familiar information to her.”

“Exactly. That’s why we need the two of you.”

The two he meant were none other than Marsha and Rian.

“Because we shared strong connections with Shirone in the real world, our informational compatibility will be high, right?” Marsha asked.

“Yes. The deeper the penetration of your information into hers, the higher the chance of restoring Shirone’s damaged information. In short, you’ll sever the bond with the Abyss and replace it with your own.”

“Alright. Got it. Let’s go, then.”

The four of them lined up before the final mirror leading to the Abyss.

The way back was still completely unknown. And the world they were about to enter—compared to the Mid-Layer of the Under Coder—was sure to be far worse, a concentration of the most corrupted, discarded, and twisted information in existence.

The terminus of information—this world’s end.

Even Fermi, who frequented the Under Coder like a playground, had never ventured into this unknown. It was natural for him to feel a sense of tension.

Miro took a deep breath and pointed to the mirror.

“So, who wants to go first? Who’ll be the first tourist on our hell-bound journey?”

Before anyone could volunteer, Rian spoke up.

“I’ll go ahead and wait for you all down there.”

He reached out, placing his hand on the mirror. As he did, the two versions of himself—real and reflected—met palms at the surface. The moment the reflection became the real Rian, the physical Rian in the room vanished as if he’d been an illusion all along.

“Wow, now that’s a man,” Marsha muttered.

“Tch. Let’s go. What’s the worst that could happen—worse than death?”

Marsha followed Rian into the link, vanishing through the mirror.

Miro turned to Fermi with a sly grin.

“You coming? Don’t you want to get paid? Or were you planning to ditch me and sneak off?”

“Please. As if,” Fermi said with a bitter smile, placing his hand on the mirror.

The thought had crossed his mind, but he knew better than to think pushing Miro into the Abyss would be enough to exact true revenge.

One step at a time.

As Fermi vanished, Miro looked at her reflection in the mirror and smiled.

“It’s okay. Because you’re there.”

Leaving behind a cryptic phrase no one else would understand, Miro stepped forward—into the endless descent that awaited in hell.
