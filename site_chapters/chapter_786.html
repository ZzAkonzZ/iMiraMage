Chapter 786:

Mitochondrial Eve is powerful in that she can transmit an individual’s experiences to the next generation with one hundred percent accuracy.

Even before they gained the name Terraje, they had been dividing like cells, absorbing the histories of the world as they spread.

They built a vast database by integrating all that experience and passing it on to the next generation.

History Search.

Through this, Uorin was able to locate the first encounter between Havitz and Abella.

“Havitz meeting her is what changed Abella’s fate.”

As Uorin explained, a gentle smile formed on Abella’s lips, lost in the memory.

“Playing with Mister Havitz was really fun. He always knew what I liked—unlike the other adults.”

He was now known as the most evil man in the world, but no one could outright deny her words.

“I know about the witch’s fate. People told me about it ever since I was born.”

The Yus tribe was a gypsy people who understood the Law.

“If Mister Havitz hadn’t broken my cursed fate, I’d probably be living in pain right now as a witch.”

The story returned to fourteen years ago.

“Let’s dig through here and make an underground canal. Big enough for a monster frog to pass through.”

On the sandy shore of the fishing spot, Havitz and Abella were focused on building a sandcastle.

His baitless fishing rod had been cast into the sea for over ten days now, but the shark he wanted still hadn’t shown.

“You know a lot about castles, mister.”

“Of course I do. It’s my house.”

Abella, flattening the ground with her hands, looked up with a face full of sand.

“Mister, are you the emperor?”

“Hm? The emperor?”

“You said your name is Havitz. The adults say the emperor of this country is named Havitz.”

“Oh, that’s Havitz the Sixteenth. That’s not me.”

“Is it okay to have the same name as the emperor?”

“Hmm, I guess so? I know hundreds of people named Havitz.”

Abella muttered, disappointed.

“It’d be nice if you were the emperor.”

Having delicately finished the underground canal, Havitz asked a bit later,

“Why the emperor? Doesn’t sound very fun.”

“Because you can do anything. My mom says if I want to change my fate, I’d have to be someone like the emperor.”

“Fate? What’s that?”

It was after ten days of playing that Abella finally brought up the darkness in her heart.

“People in my tribe say I’ll become a witch. My mom’s a witch too. That’s why she always has to give her body to men.”

The Yus tribe was in decline.

“Back then, the Yus were respected for their Law, and people came from far away to get their fortunes read. That’s how we used to overcome the witch’s fate. But now they say that time has passed.”

The tribespeople couldn’t let the name of Yus, which had thousands of years of history, die out with their generation.

“Now we can’t tell fortunes anymore. So the girls with the witch’s fate go around selling their bodies.”

In Bardol, the village where the Yus tribe now lived, there were both fishermen and pirates.

There wasn’t much difference between them.

“I don’t want to go back. The adults keep coming and touching me. My mom got mad once, and the men—way bigger than you—beat her nearly to death.”

“So being a witch isn’t fun, huh?”

“Of course not. What’s good about it? Just thinking about it is disgusting. I want to grow flowers.”

“Then don’t be a witch.”

Abella shook her head.

“They say you can’t change the Law. Every night, my mom holds me and cries. She says she’s sorry. But she still says I’ll have no choice but to become a witch.”

“I don’t get it.”

“Well, of course you don’t. Only gypsies understand.”

“If you don’t want to be a witch, just don’t be one. That’s all.”

Perfect chaos.

To Havitz, who lived entirely outside the rules of “how things should be,” the Law was nothing but an empty concept.

“Just do what you want. Life’s more fun that way. Why do boring things?”

“...Can I really?”

Deep in thought, Abella smiled brightly.

“But I’m having fun now that I play with you. Before, I was always alone in the forest.”

They completed a beautiful sandcastle.

“I’m having fun too. No one in the palace ever wants to play. They just cry at everything.”

Havitz mimicked the frightened faces of the people around him, and Abella laughed until her stomach hurt.

“Haah, I wish I could play with you every day.”

“Then let’s play every day.”

Abella hesitated.

“But you’re an adult. Mom said adults are busy, so I shouldn’t bother them.”

“It’s fine. Playing with you is the most fun. I’ll just not do the other stuff. Who cares?”

Abella’s eyes blinked sweetly.

“You’re really different from the adults I know.”

“How so?”

“You don’t tell me what to do or not to do. You don’t act like I’m annoying.”

“Other adults do that?”

“More than you can imagine. ‘You have to be a good person,’ ‘Hold your spoon like this’... they never stop.”

Havitz twisted his mustache.

“Hmm, I wouldn’t know. I’ve never heard things like that in my life…”

And so he came to a conclusion.

“People who say things like that are scared. Adults are always scared, as far as I can tell.”

“Scared of what?”

Havitz shrugged.

“Pfft, who knows? What’s there to be afraid of in this world? It’s like they think they’re walking on a tightrope over an invisible cliff.”

He made walking motions with two fingers.

“But when I tried walking around, there was no rope at all. You don’t fall no matter where you go. It’s obvious. But people keep pretending they don’t know.”

“Hmm. So, you don’t fall.”

“If you want to play with others, you should give them what they want sometimes. But if you don’t want to play, there’s no need to listen. It just gets a little boring, that’s all…”

Havitz comforted her.

“As long as you’re not afraid, you can do anything. You should grow flowers. Don’t listen to all that boring stuff about witches.”

She didn’t understand all of his words, but it was the most comforting thing anyone had ever told her.

“Okay! I’m going to grow flowers starting today! Thank you, Mister!”

Havitz grinned, baring his teeth.

From that day forward, Havitz and Abella would go into the forest and collect flowers every time they met.

One flower a day.

They didn’t know the names or species, but just doing what they wanted was enough to make them happy.

Still, the shark showed no sign of appearing.

“Maybe I should go farther out.”

Havitz sat by the fishing spot, yawning as he looked out over the endless sea.

“Why’s she so late today?”

Abella had never been late before—but today, she hadn’t shown up.

“Maybe she doesn’t want to play with me anymore?”

Missing her, Havitz stood up and headed toward her village.

From the moment he reached the fishy-smelling entrance of the fishing town, it was clear that something had happened.

“Damn gypsies! We fed you, sheltered you, and this is how you repay us?!”

Hexter, the village leader, kicked a frail woman to the ground as Abella ran forward.

“Waaaah! Mom!”

“Abella! Why did you come?! I told you to run!”

Abella shook her head.

“No! I’m not leaving you behind!”

It had been two months since the gypsies settled in the village. The pirates, long fed up, had started laying hands on Abella.

Her mother resisted desperately, leaving claw marks on Hexter’s face—and that’s when it all exploded.

“People who couldn’t even find a place to rot anywhere else dare lay a hand on me? You all want to die here?!”

The chief of the Yus tribe stepped in front of the mother and daughter.

“You offered us work, and we did exactly as you asked!”

“Work? You think trash like you belongs out at sea? You think the house, food, and chickens we gave you were free?!”

“We’ll leave! Just let us go!”

Hexter laughed scornfully.

“Sure, leave. But first, pay back everything you’ve mooched off of us. That’s only fair, right?”

He shoved the tribal chief to the ground, grabbed Abella by the hair, and tore her from her mother’s arms.

“Mom! Mom!”

The flower vase Abella had molded from clay shattered, and the nameless flowers she had gathered were crushed underfoot.

“No! Please, not Abella! Take me instead!”

“Shut up! This is barely even payback! Take her away!”

One of Hexter’s men slung Abella over his shoulder and headed for the barn. The tribe’s chief crawled forward, screaming.

“You bastards! What crime has a child committed?!”

“Crime? Of course she has one. Being poor.”

As the men laughed mockingly, a voice called out from beyond the crowd.

“Abellaaa~. Let’s play~.”

A deathly silence fell. Everyone turned to see Havitz standing there.

“Who the hell are you?”

The pirates instinctively drew their swords, but the moment they got a look at Havitz, their faces turned ghost-white.

“...The King of Desire…?”

Clang. The pirates’ weapons fell to the ground.

“H-hrrk!”

No one had ever seen him in person, but there wasn’t a single soul in the village who didn’t know Havitz.

God above, why?! Why now?!

Why is he here?

It was over. No one knew why he had come to such a remote fishing village, but one thing was certain:

We’re all going to die.

There is no mercy from the King of Desire.

“Waaaah! Mister!”

Breaking free from the stunned pirate’s grasp, Abella ran crying into Havitz’s arms.

“There, there. It’s okay.”

Havitz lifted her up and walked toward the pirates, gently patting her back.

“They crushed my flowers! They beat my mom so badly!”

“There, there. It’s okay.”

Realizing Havitz and Abella knew each other, Hexter’s fingertips began to tremble.

What have I done?

You’d have to be brain-dead to think stabbing a sleeping demon was smarter than this.

“Your Majesty, we—”

“Die.”

Havitz cut him off.

“Abella cried. I don’t want to play anymore. Die.”

“Aaaaghhhh!”

Screaming like his mind had snapped, Hexter drew his sword and slashed his own throat.

“Khkk!”

The clean, fatal cut made the other pirates panic. One after another, they drew their weapons and followed their leader’s example.

This is the only salvation!

Because if Havitz actually decided to "play," death would not be the end.

Most of the pirates chose suicide, but the villagers lacked the courage.

“Captain of the Guard.”

At Havitz’s words, a shadow slipped forward like a ghost, revealing its form on the ground.

“Your command.”

Wearing a white mask beneath a black cloak, the figure crossed two curved swords over his chest, mustache twitching.

“Seize the villagers. Sentence them all to the dark punishment.”

“Yes, sir.”

Gouging out the eyes, slicing off the nose, tearing the eardrums, ripping out the tongue, cutting off the limbs, and peeling off the skin.

Then they would be injected with nutrient fluids and forced to live out their lives in endless agony—that was the dark punishment.

“Nooo! Not that!”

As the villagers ran to grab the pirates’ fallen swords, the guard captain’s shadow moved faster than the wind.

“Kill me! Please! Just kill me instead!”

One after another, people began to faint.
