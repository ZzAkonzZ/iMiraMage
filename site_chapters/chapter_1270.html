Chapter 1270:

“Veron.”

Shirone knew.

The story of the toad who, just before being devoured by a snake, achieved enlightenment and was reborn as a human incarnation.

The final question he held in the moment before immortality ended was…

“Can humanity truly change the future with its will?”

The Veron Problem.

If a philosophical spike was driven deep enough into one's mind, perhaps even in an infinite multiverse, one could find their spiritual home.

“Thank you,” Shirone said. “But I can’t do it. No, it’s impossible. I’m not Yahweh anymore.”

Tachyon was the inverse of Hexa.

“Right. Amy died, and I lost my compassion. But Amy isn’t the only one who died. Ninety-nine percent of humanity is gone. My parents, too.”

He hadn’t said it out loud, because everyone here had lost someone they loved.

“If it's the same universe—if the space is the same—then sure, I might meet the Amy I love. But even so, nothing changes. I wouldn’t be able to return here.”

Adam spoke.

“Because the timelines are different.”

“Exactly. From the moment I save Amy, that universe diverges again—it becomes a different world. So in this time, in this space, the outcome of humanity’s extinction doesn’t change. That means I still can’t regain compassion. And ultimately…”

Shirone said, bitterly,

“Because I’m not Yahweh, I can’t even reverse causality in the first place.”

Silence fell.

“You’re all misunderstanding something. The multiverse isn’t just infinite space—it’s an infinite number of time coordinates. There’s a ‘me’ who makes countless choices in the same timeline across different universes, and countless versions of me moving from past to future in the same space. Veron’s ‘Philosophy’s End’ only solves the space problem. Even if I go back and save Amy, this now doesn’t change.”

Shirone should have been the one hurting most as he said it—but surprisingly, the others didn’t look troubled.

As Shirone blinked, Neid grinned slyly.

“Of course you’d say that. Did you really think we didn’t consider time paradoxes? We know you can’t change the future of the same universe just by going to the past.”

“…What?”

Fermi spoke.

“First, we fix the spatial coordinates using Philosophy’s End.”

Shirone slowly turned his head.

“So the only problem left is all the different timelines in the same space. If we can’t change the future by going to the past—then what if we merge all of past and future into the same present?”

“Wait, how could that possibly…” Shirone trailed off.

“…Ah.”

“Right. Time Vibration.”

Fermi said.

“We blow up every barrier in the timeline between when you save Amy and now. Then you and we are no longer past and future—we’re all just one synchronized present.”

The time paradox is resolved.

“But…”

Shirone’s voice became urgent.

“Time Vibration is integrated with the Eleventh Sense, right? Even if I tried, without Tachyon, I can’t evade the divine law. I can’t handle both states at once.”

“Depreciation Contract.”

Fermi pulled out a document.

“The reason I like this ability is because it lets me adjust the terms of the contract. Sometimes that’s more important than what magic I’m leasing. And here—your signature agreeing to transfer Time Vibration is clearly written. Go ahead and check it.”

Shirone didn’t remember signing it.

“A depreciation deal only lets you rent magic using profits gained from other abilities. Just matching the price almost killed me. I’ll let you imagine how much this deal cost.”

It didn’t sound like a joke.

“Anyway, you can trade it for a chip anytime. Or more precisely—I can pull you back into the present.”

“Fermi…”

“At first, it was just a theory.” Fermi hated getting sentimental.

“Honestly, I didn’t think you’d agree. I didn’t even have a clear plan—just the concept of deceiving God. Amy could have truly died. In a state beyond resurrection.”

“Then why?”

Why did Shirone agree?

“You said you’d trust me. That’s what you said.” “Honestly, I didn’t expect that. I couldn’t find a solution even after wrestling with you for days. I don’t know if it was desperation or if you really believed in me…”

Fermi gave a dry smile.

“It stressed me out so badly, I felt like my blood would boil. First time in my life I’ve taken a loss in a deal.”

“You…”

Sensing the tremble in Shirone’s voice, Fermi clapped his hands, shifting the mood.

“Well then, it’s my turn to answer now. If you save Amy in the same space—I’ll pull you into the same time. So what happens?”

Fermi snapped his fingers and mimicked Shirone’s earlier words.

“She’ll just pop into existence.”

This is real.

“…Hrk.”

A sob escaped.

“Then you’ll be Yahweh again. And you’ll watch civilization—blown to dust—rise again like magic. Even the 99 percent of humanity who were sacrificed will be reborn in our reality, swept along the accelerated flow of time. So, is that good enough for you?”

The world’s pickiest customer.

“Really…?”

Tears poured from Shirone’s eyes.

“You can really… save Amy? I can… really see her again?”

Neid smiled.

“We’re changing the future, Shirone. Let’s give that arrogant god a punch to the face.”

After sobbing with his head bowed, a light burst from Shirone’s body.

Hexa—Miracle Stream.

And when he raised his head again, there was no trace of defeat in his eyes.

“I’ll do it. I’ll save Amy, no matter what.”

A fight only for himself.

But in the end, Shirone’s victory would be humanity’s salvation—so Fermi had been right all along.

Neid smirked.

How far ahead do you even see? He still found him obnoxious when he thought back to their school days—but now he finally understood why Seriel had fallen for him.

Still, I seriously had no idea. Those two? I’ll have to ask Amy later.

Fermi said,

“Then let’s begin the briefing.”

Iruki sipped his tea as he looked at Lanstin and Lynn sitting across the table.

Not as arrogant as I expected.

If anything, their eyes were cast downward, their posture slightly timid.

Indifference.

A signal that said: I don’t care about power plays—just stay out of my life.

This won’t be easy.

To Iruki, artists were like strangers living in the same building.

You think you know them—but in truth, you start from knowing absolutely nothing.

“A long time ago, I heard you said goodbye to your daughter.”

Killing intent.

An aura as intense as Gaold’s swirled through the air—but at least the door was now open.

“It was a cold,” Lanstin finally spoke.

“A petty illness. She was always a bit sickly. Well, probably because of me. Everything bad that happened was always my fault.”

“Couldn’t you take her to a hospital?”

“…I think I was in the studio. Probably playing guitar. I liked playing the guitar.”

He sharpened his words at the end, like it was the one thing he would never let anyone take from him.

“Does he really not remember?”

According to reports, at the time, he had gotten beaten up by loan sharks while trying to borrow money.

“I’m not doing it.”

Lanstin continued.

“I suppose I should thank you for letting me live. But it doesn’t matter. I’m not going to do anything.”

“Mr. Lanstin.”

“I’m not an assassin. Sure, I’ve killed people—but I’ve never taken money for it. I don’t know. I don’t want to think about it. Just let me go back.”

“We’re not asking you to kill anyone.”

Lynn raised her head.

She assumed the leader of humanity would be asking him to eliminate someone.

“This is much harder. You’re the only one who can do it. If you fail, everyone dies.”

Lanstin didn’t seem particularly upset.

Whether that was because everyone would die, or because only he could do it, was hard to say.

“So what, I just play the guitar?”

“Your ability—Resonance. It alters bio-waves and induces death. The more organisms present, the greater the effect.”

“And?”

“In eight hours, a disaster called Cell Buster will occur at the North Pole. It merges with human cells through a species called Anti-Cell and reduces all cellular function to zero.”

Lynn’s eyes widened.

“Wow.”

Iruki spoke.

“We’re asking you to delay it for seven days.”

“Delay it?”

Not destroy it?

“You probably can’t stop it with Resonance alone. It’s an unsolvable code. Kill one, and it splits into two. Destroy two, and it becomes four.”

So… seven days.

“You drag me here just to tell me to die?”

“This is humanity’s final chance. The fate of the planet is in your hands.”

“That doesn’t matter.”

Lanstin continued.

“What matters is that I’m the last guitarist. Whether the world ends or not—it’s none of my business.”

“But maybe it is. If you save all of humanity, wouldn’t your daughter be proud?”

The laugh he let out for the first time sounded more like a scoff, and Iruki chose not to say more.

“I don’t know.”

He was still a stranger from next door.

Fermi spoke.

“Even if we’ve solved the time paradox, it actually makes things more complicated. Events in the past can now affect the future. If you touch anything besides Amy, the present we live in could be altered.”

“That’s tricky.”

He had no right to complain—but the one he was about to fight over causality was none other than Nane.

“History must be preserved. There are three things we need to solve this. First, a blueprint that holds the entirety of human history. Second, the ability to process and compute that history. Third, a vessel to enforce that history once it’s calculated.”

More than just tricky.

“And,” Fermi said, “we have all three.”

Shirone listened.

“First, the blueprint of human history—Uorin has it. Second, the computational ability—Richard and the cerebral star together can handle it. And third, maintaining the existing timeline regardless of your path—that’s the job of the Twelve Apostles.”

“Messiah.”

From the darkness, the Twelve Apostles emerged.

“You guys…” Shirone asked.

“What were you doing hiding there all this time?”

Asraiker stepped forward.

“The Messiah we serve is the Yahweh of the era. There was no reason to address one who had lost their realm.”

When sadness flickered in Shirone’s eyes, Asraiker glanced around nervously at her comrades.

“I-it was a joke.”

Venom Dragon Poine said,

“In any case, now that you’ve regained your realm—congratulations. Fight with all your strength. We’ll protect history.”

Yes, these were…

The Apostles of Time.

The ones who had perfectly preserved humanity’s history in the face of Anke Ra’s three resets.
