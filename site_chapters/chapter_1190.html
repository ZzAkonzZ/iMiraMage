Chapter 1190:

On the 1.5th floor of Ymir’s mind, Shirone had a dream.

A Transcendent Dream.

Time had no meaning in dreams, but the amount of data he received was immense.

A story of colossal scale.

It was the enlightenment of a man known as the first Yora, gained amidst a horrifying tragedy.

It was a time when humanity had moved past tribal life, and centralized governance was beginning to take shape.

“Phew, it’s hot.”

Tyrants were rampant, and the lives of the oppressed were far more miserable than in Shirone’s world.

It was an era when many philosophers and seekers questioned the nature of existence and preached their thoughts.

“Is it supposed to be this hard to meet people?”

Yorahan, a twenty-year-old seeker, also carried the spirit of enlightenment in his heart as he wandered the world.

He opened the lid of his waterskin and tilted his head back, but only a single drop of water fell.

“Smack, smack.”

His thirst had worsened from climbing the mountain path, yet he could find no source of water.

“This… if I’m not careful, I might actually die.”

Saying that, he burst into laughter.

“Well, I suppose I have it better than most. There are so many suffering under tyranny.”

War, and more war.

The ambitions of rulers were endless, and conflicts with neighboring countries erupted almost daily.

‘The war must end.’

Some argued that the political system had to change, while others believed the economic system needed reform.

‘A world built on the sacrifices of a few can only last so long. If everyone just made a small effort to care, war would end.’

He believed in the goodness of the human heart.

“Alright! Let’s go!”

After all, he had to meet someone to spread his beliefs.

It was two days later, during his bold journey, that he first sensed an ominous sign.

“Huff… Huff…”

Though he had traveled through countless remote areas, he had never encountered a place as isolated as this.

‘Where… is this?’

Not even common animals were in sight—only trees and grass as far as he could see.

He had studied herbalism to some extent, but the plants here were unfamiliar. Yesterday, he had eaten the wrong one and ended up with severe diarrhea.

‘The dehydration is getting worse.’

Had he lost consciousness?

In his intermittent moments of awareness, it seemed that another day had passed.

‘Is this the end?’

He regretted being unable to spread his beliefs, but he did not resent his fate.

‘There must be others, people who think like me. It’s not like I was ever popular anyway.’

He had no way of knowing just how impossible his dream was in that era.

‘… I feel drowsy.’

Just as he was about to embrace eternal sleep, he heard the sound of footsteps rustling the grass and a voice calling out.

“What are you doing here?”

Yorahan’s eyes snapped open, and in his blurry vision, he saw the faces of a beautiful man and woman.

“A… person…”

He cursed his parched throat.

“Help me…”

The two exchanged glances and shrugged before asking him again.

“How should we help you?”

“W-Water… Something to drink… Please…”

“Water? There’s water in the village.”

As Yorahan, on the verge of death, was about to fall into despair, the woman asked,

“Can’t you hold on?”

He briefly wondered what she would do if he said no, but he had already nodded.

“Alright. Just wait a moment.”

The woman closed her eyes.

At first, he thought she was praying for his soul, but then something astonishing happened.

The surrounding trees began to shake, and countless droplets of water started to emerge from their leaves.

‘What is this? A dream?’

Since magic was a privilege known only to a select few in that era, he could think of no other explanation.

But when a droplet landed on his tongue, his brain released an overwhelming surge of pleasure.

“Haa… More… More…”

“Let’s collect it first.”

At the man’s suggestion, the woman gathered leaves and shaped them with clay to create a makeshift bowl.

“Here.”

As she poured the water in, Yorahan gulped desperately, his Adam’s apple bobbing furiously.

“Haa…”

How could he possibly describe that sensation in words?

‘I’m alive.’

And with that, he drifted into sleep.

The sound of laughter from outside gradually roused him.

Birds were chirping.

He tried to recall the last time he had heard birdsong, then suddenly sat up.

“What… What is this?”

Looking around, he found himself inside a small hut, lying on a bed woven from grass.

“It wasn’t a dream?”

As he swung his feet over the side of the bed, the tent flap opened, and two people entered.

A boy and a girl, both appearing around seventeen. They were beautiful, but what caught Yorahan’s attention the most was their scent.

‘Flowers…?’

Humans whose bodies smelled like flowers?

Carrying a bowl of water, the two were startled to see him awake.

“Oh? You’re up? We should tell the chief.”

“Wait, wait!”

As he hastily called out, they stopped.

“Sorry, but… where am I? What happened? Did you two save me?”

“No. It was my brother and sister who saved you. I need to tell the chief, though. May I go?”

Their overly polite tone seemed suspicious, but Yorahan simply assumed they were naturally gentle.

“Go ahead. I hate to ask for more after you saved my life, but… could I get something to eat?”

Now that his thirst was quenched, he was starving.

“Food…”

The girl hesitated for a moment before speaking.

“Oh! Armand has food. Should I bring you some?”

“Yes, thank you. I don’t have any money right now, but once I recover, I’ll repay you somehow.”

Not long after, they returned with a plate containing red chunks of meat.

“Here, eat.”

Unlike what he had imagined, the food was raw, merely washed of blood.

“Well, I guess different regions have different customs. Interesting. It would taste better if it were cooked, though.”

“Eek!”

The children flinched.

“The Fire Tribe doesn’t use fire. Fire destroys everything… nature… and us.”

“The Fire Tribe?”

It was a name he had never heard before.

Still, sensing no hostility from them, Yorahan bowed his head.

“Thank you. I’ll eat well.”

“Um… Can I go tell the chief now? He told me to report back.”

“Hm?”

Yorahan blinked.

“Didn’t you already go?”

“You asked for food. How could I have gone yet?”

And at last, he realized something was off.

‘Something about this place feels subtly different. Is it a culture where prioritizing others is the most important value?’

If that were the case...

‘Then the gods have guided me here. This is my chance to test my beliefs.’

Chewing on the raw meat, Yorahan spoke.

“Alright, go ahead.”

Once he confirmed that the tent flap had closed, he swallowed the raw meat and put on his shoes.

“They say hunger is the best seasoning.”

Stepping quietly out of the hut, he saw the Fire Tribe basking in the sunlight with gentle smiles.

‘Hmm, I don’t sense hostility.’

The atmosphere was completely different from the villages he had visited before, easing his tension.

As he wandered through the village, feeling the gazes of the Fire Tribe on him, he heard a fierce female voice.

“Why did you let a human in?!”

A raised voice was common in human society, but hearing it here made it feel strangely out of place.

‘Human. She must be talking about me. Then…’

Were the Fire Tribe not human?

Following the voice, Yorahan approached a hut and peered inside through a window with no glass.

‘Wow.’

All the Fire Tribe were beautiful, but the woman sitting in the chair radiated an extraordinary dignity beyond that.

‘She smells nice. Is it lotus?’

The woman, Chief Lutia, spoke.

“Armand, don’t get so worked up. The Fire Tribe must help humans. That is the will of our ancestors.”

Yorahan turned to look at the woman called Armand.

‘She’s terrifying.’

Her fierce eyes and wild, waist-length hair set her apart from the rest of the Fire Tribe.

“Humans?”

Armand stomped her foot hard.

“You know how much humans have persecuted us! The reason we live deep in these mountains, where no human sets foot, is because of them!”

“I understand the dangers of humans. But we cannot simply turn a blind eye. If we understand and embrace them, one day, they will cherish us in return.”

“Before that happens, we’ll all be dead.”

“Armand.”

“What will you do when that human wakes up? Aren’t you afraid of what he might demand from us? Will we run away again? Are we going to abandon the village we’ve cultivated for hundreds of years?”

The Fire Tribe’s extreme passivity was just as intense as humanity’s desire for free will.

“One man should be fine. And besides, we have you, Armand.”

A carnivorous plant—an extreme rarity in nature.

To eat meat, one had to hunt, and hunting meant possessing an active will.

“… It’s frustrating. You don’t understand, Lutia. There are strong humans out there.”

Armand, who had long fought to protect the Fire Tribe, never underestimated humans.

“Ordinary ones can be stopped with our Dimensional Barrier. But if that man is…”

Pointing outside the village, Armand finally noticed Yorahan standing by the window.

“You!”

Startled, Yorahan quickly ducked under the window, but Armand immediately burst outside.

“I-I’m sorry! I’m sorry!”

Grabbing him by the collar, she dragged him into the hut.

“Ugh!”

“You were eavesdropping like a thief?! I’ll kill you!”

Armand reached for the only piece of metal in the chief’s possession—a longsword.

Though it was meant only for hunting and combat, she had no time to hesitate.

‘He’s heard everything about the Fire Tribe. If he exploits our nature…’

It would lead to a disaster.

As the gleaming sword rose above her head, Yorahan quickly raised his hands.

“Wait! You don’t have to kill me!”

“Shut up.”

Just as she was about to swing the blade, she felt her body freeze, starting from the foot planted on the ground.

“Armand.”

Lutia spoke.

“You dare handle metal without my permission? It seems humans aren’t the only danger to the Fire Tribe.”

The reason the lineage of the lotus was the chief’s was because their Dimensional Barrier was the strongest.

It was a force where neither action nor inaction existed—a perfect balance of persuasion and compromise.

Armand lowered her sword in defeat.

“… I apologize.”

If humans were dangerous because of their potential for violence, then Armand was no different.

“Leave.”

Placing the sword back on the shelf, Armand left the hut, and Lutia approached Yorahan.

“I’m glad you’re safe. Please, have a seat.”

“Ah, thank you.”

Having already brushed past death multiple times, Yorahan sat down in a daze.

“As chief, I must ask—how did you get here? This place has almost no animals, and many poisonous plants grow here. People don’t come.”

They had cultivated the village to be this way.

“Oh, I am a seeker. I travel the world to spread my thoughts. But that doesn’t mean I’m unemployed! I have studied a few disciplines…”

Lutia tilted her head.

“I don’t quite understand. Don’t all humans share their thoughts?”

“Well, I mean special thoughts. Like the direction of humanity—how we should live.”

“Oh?”

Lutia showed interest.

“What a fascinating profession. Could you share your thoughts with me as well?”

It was the first time anyone had asked him that, leaving Yorahan flustered.

“Uh, well… The foundation is love. Of course, material things influence people’s hearts, but…”

Even as he rambled, Lutia sat still, listening without the slightest movement.

“… So, that’s about it. Haha! I don’t usually ramble like this, but my mind is all over the place.”

Yorahan chuckled awkwardly, but then his expression stiffened.

“Uh… Excuse me?”

Lutia, who had been sitting gracefully, suddenly began trembling, and tears streamed down her face.

“I see.”

She smiled like a flower in full bloom.

“What a truly beautiful world.”
