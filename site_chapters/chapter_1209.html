Chapter 1209:

Far from the Green Ocean where the species war was taking place, a temporary barrack had been set up.

Inside the rundown shelter, low-ranking angels had gathered, and an equal number of fairies were tending to them.

“Sniff, sniff.”

“Can you please stop crying? It’s getting on my nerves.”

As one angel began sobbing over their situation, the rest burst into tears as if on cue—like newborn infants.

“Haaah, seriously.”

At first, the fairies had shown some degree of respect, but now they no longer regarded them as angels.

“If you're just going to cry all day, go to sleep already. Try thinking about those of us who have to take care of you.”

“S-sorry…”

As one angel curled up and lay down, the others followed suit.

Once, they were beings who enforced their will upon the world through the divine concepts given by God.

“Hey. Everyone gather around.”

As the angels finally quieted down, the fairy squad leader gathered the others.

“This setup is way too inefficient. Let’s just group the angels all in one place, and we’ll take turns caring for them in shifts. Maybe twenty per group.”

“That’d be better. Honestly, it’s not even about the work—it’s the whining. If it weren’t for this species war, I’d have smacked one of them upside the head—”

Suddenly—

“Oooooohhhhhh!”

The angels’ eyes shot open, and their innate power, Vibration, flared to life.

“W-what’s happening?!”

The fairies turned their heads in a panic—and saw hundreds of pillars of light rising into the sky.

“…They’re back.”

The Judicial Halos reappearing above each angel’s head made it impossible to deny the truth.

Did they really pull it off?

Had just three archangels gone to the sun and actually changed the divine Law set by God?

Like corpses rising from their graves, the angels slowly began to stand.

“Kesker.”

The fairy squad leader shot off like an arrow.

“Y-yes! You called for me!”

Knowing what she’d done, her mind blanked and her small body trembled.

Of course, the angels knew too.

That’s why their gazes toward the fairies were anything but kind—but even for them, those times had been horrifying.

Losing one’s moral compass… is terrifying.

It was an experience they never wanted to relive, so scolding the fairies would have to wait.

“Where is Uriel?”

“H-He’s in the Green Ocean…”

Through Oversight, the angels scanned the battlefield, then spread their wings of light and took off.

Just hold on a little longer.

As countless flashes of light shot off in succession, the fairies asked Kesker,

“What do we do now?”

“What do you mean?”

Kesker’s face twisted into a grimace.

“We follow them!”

Lollipop Mark anxiously scratched his nails against the floor.

“Hurry. Please hurry.”

The data being transferred at that very moment was pure foresight—something impossible to obtain in reality.

Even one more tick… could change the future of humanity.

Operator pointed ahead.

“Hey, this is rebooting, isn’t it?”

When Lollipop Mark looked up, the lights on the Zets’ faces—those that had been offline—were slowly coming back on.

“Dammit!”

He quickly ducked his head and checked the progress.

64%.

If the main system finished rebooting at this rate, it would counterattack, and all the files would be lost.

I have to cut the connection. Even if the files are damaged, some recovery should be possible.

Should he be satisfied with this much?

The response time between man and machine wasn’t even comparable—he had to rely on intuition.

Operator said,

“You decide. I won’t blame you no matter what happens.”

“Hrrrrghhh…”

Lollipop Mark’s eyes sharpened.

Just one more second…

A single second of data could save tens of thousands of lives.

Still okay. I can push it another three seconds—

In that instant, without warning, everything went black, and his hand reached for the disconnect button.

Cut.

At the same time, the central pillar of the main system lit up with a low hum.

“Haah! Haaah!”

The number on the hologram read 83%.

“Yes! I was faster!”

He rapidly typed in recovery commands.

“Good, the corrupted files are restored. Now… let’s see what kind of secrets you’ve got hidden in here.”

Just as he was about to open the files, Freeman rushed in and snatched the equipment from him.

“What are you doing?!”

Lollipop Mark’s eyes widened.

The Parrot Mercenary Corps officers had silently surrounded them, weapons drawn.

“W-why are you doing this?”

“Sorry.”

Marsha spoke as she took the equipment.

“No one can view this data. I’ll take it. You’ll be compensated.”

“You can’t do this! Give it back! That’s mine! There’s an important program—”

“Shh.”

Team 6 leader Snyder appeared behind Lollipop Mark and held a dagger to his neck.

“Cut it out. This is about the future of humanity. It's better for you to back off.”

The threat was chilling, but what hurt more was the coldness from someone who’d treated him like a little brother until now.

“Hah! Now you pull this crap? Fine, go ahead and stab me! Let’s see you do it, you old hag!”

“…What? Old hag?!”

Marsha’s eyes flared with fury.

“I’ll kill you for real! You think this is a joke? We’re terrifying people!”

“Then do it! You don’t even have the guts! If not, give me back my gear!”

Snyder asked,

“…Should I eliminate him?”

Marsha groaned.

“Is that how you talk to a kid? Just calm him down or give him candy or something!”

As she turned and stormed off, Snyder sighed and lowered his dagger.

“Why’s she mad at me? Did I call her an old hag? Huh? Did I?”

Operator spoke up.

“I understand you can’t share the information. You don’t need to threaten anyone.”

“…Right. Sorry.”

Freed from restraint, Lollipop Mark turned away.

“Ugh, screw this!”

He kicked a low kick—but it was like hitting a log. Only his leg hurt.

“Ha! Don’t be so angry. I’ll introduce you to a really pretty lady. She’s only… fifteen years older than you.”

“No thanks!”

“Anyway…”

As Snyder ruffled Lollipop Mark’s hair and walked ahead, the other officers followed.

“What do we do with these guys?”

The Zets had formed a wall ahead, silently standing in formation.

Shirone looked out from the rooftop, then returned to the room where Zet had collapsed.

"The scenery hasn’t changed."

Even though the main system had gone down, the world was still governed by Law.

Why? Even though Ikael had succeeded…

It was a frustrating feeling, as though no matter what changes occurred in reality, the future had already been predetermined.

That can’t be. I must be missing something. God’s thought process is broader than mine.

But he didn’t have long to reflect—Shirone turned his head at the sound of an engine starting.

“Zet!”

Sanskrit script appeared on Zet’s facial screen, and his limbs began to move.

“You’re awake? All of a sudden, the system just…”

Shirone stopped dead.

The form was the same, but something felt undeniably different.

“Form is emptiness. Emptiness is form.”

Rising fully to his feet, Zet raised a Tan of Law into the air—just like the others outside.

“It’s me! You remember me, don’t you?!”

“Remove your worldly desires.”

Dodging the explosions erupting all around him, Shirone hurled himself toward the door.

The error has been corrected.

The reboot of the main system had restored quantum-level discrepancies to their default state.

“Damn it!”

Because it could never be undone, Zet was now just a machine to Shirone.

Don’t desecrate what he was.

“O ye beings, follow the Law.”

“Yaaah!!”

Casting Hand of God, Shirone slammed Zet into the wall with tremendous force.

With a loud crash, Zet’s body collapsed to the floor, half-crushed.

“O ye beings… the… Law…”

The light faded from Zet’s screen.

Shirone wiped his tears.

The broken teacup, the scorched carpet, even the fish flopping outside the shattered aquarium…

They were all part of Zet’s heart.

Shirone gently returned the fish to the aquarium made from Materialization and carried the body back to the center of the room.

“…I’m sorry.”

Hoping that the heart passed on today would one day spread across the whole world—

“Huah!”

He shot up through the ceiling.

What about the excavation team?

At the tallest building in the city, countless Zets climbing the outer walls sparkled like shattered glass.

In the underground chamber of the main system, the excavation team was locked in battle with a unit of Zets.

“Damn it! There’s no end to them!”

With the building indestructible, their only way out was to break through the Zets.

Operator shouted, “We’re almost out of power! Drive the Zets into the corner! This is the final collapse!”

“Got it!”

As the mercenaries charged the enemy lines, the formation of the Zets began to warp.

Meanwhile, Marsha stood behind a pillar, holding the Omega 999 file.

What do I do with this?

It was for Shirone’s sake, but ultimately, the employer of the mercenary group was Fermi.

His special condition had been: “Any information that doesn’t need to be viewed must be delivered as-is.”

“At the time,” Marsha had asked, “What kind of situation is that? How am I supposed to hand it over without even knowing what it is?”

“There are many ways to handle classified information. Coded books, metaphors in paintings, non-melodic scores that shatter classical forms. I'd prefer those kinds of relics not be examined at all.”

“Hmph, alright.”

Marsha had thought to herself,

As long as I’m the only one who knows, what’s the harm? Who would even know?

“And one more thing,” Fermi had added.

“If you obtain data created with technology from the future world, under no circumstances are you to view it. No one—not even High Gear’s administrators—can be allowed to know.”

Looking back now, it seemed like Fermi had foreseen everything.

“Man, what a tight-ass. How are we supposed to maintain any kind of teamwork without at least a little humanity?”

“If it’s the Fallen Saint, that’s more than enough. The team doesn’t just follow her out of fear. Besides, this directly affects profits. When it comes to information, who knows it is more important than what is known. Information that isn’t monopolized has no value.”

Information that everyone knows has the same value as information that could never happen.

“They call that information entropy. But actually, this clause has a deeper meaning.”

“A deeper meaning?”

“Shirone.”

For the first time, Marsha’s face turned serious.

“There’s some information Shirone must never know. The moment he finds out, everything is over. It’s not just about monopolizing it—it needs to remain at a level of entropy so high it’s as if it doesn’t exist at all. Shirone agreed to that, which is why he had his memory wiped.”

“What kind of information could it be…? And what if I accidentally mention it without knowing?”

“You’ll know. The moment you encounter it, you’ll be certain. That’s why it’s not even written in the contract. There can’t be any channel through which the information moves.”

Even Marsha’s mouth was considered a channel.

“…Alright. I promise. If it comes down to revealing this to Shirone, I’d rather take my own life.”

Only then did Fermi present the contract.

“…Please sign.”
