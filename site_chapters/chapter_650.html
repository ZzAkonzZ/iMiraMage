Chapter 650:

Rian didn’t fully understand what Shirone was saying—but that was precisely the point Shirone wanted to make.

“Coming to the guild was the right move. I think I was taking things too lightly.”

“Hmph. Maybe that’s true for Ra Enemy, but I still don’t agree about the guild. I mean, sure, I respect you, but if they get all jittery just hearing your name, they’re not worth worrying about.”

Rian voiced the frustrations he’d felt back on the first floor.

“Same with the info cards. They say nobody’s read them, but who really knows? Once we leave tomorrow, they’ll probably just resell them for 2 gold again.”

“Haha! Honestly, with a system this big, it's not easy to pull tricks like that. If we're suspicious, then everyone is.”

“Still, dropping 50 silver just because someone’s read it once—that’s kind of a scam. What if you’re buying hundreds of cards?”

“I’d still buy them.”

Shirone held one of the information cards in his hand.

“People usually think of long periods of time in vague terms. Say you have a task that takes a year. But really, a year is just a pile of seconds.”

“Well, yeah, that’s true.”

“Now, imagine it takes us one year and thirty minutes to find Ra Enemy. But what if someone else finds him in one year and twenty-nine minutes? What then?”

Rian blinked.

“It might sound like a distant future, but things like that happen all the time. And the world we live in? It’s already happening right now.”

Shirone stood the card up.

“That one-minute difference… whether or not you read the intel even a day earlier can decide success or failure. If a year’s worth of effort can collapse over a single minute, then paying 50 silver more isn't a loss.”

That was the mindset of people living in this world.

No one pointed you in the right direction. From start to finish, you had to do it all yourself.

‘I was too naïve.’

Only now did Shirone realize just how many capable people had always been by his side—and how much easier things had been because of them.

He tossed the card onto the bed.

“Of course, rest is important too. No one can go an entire year without eating or sleeping. We’ll have to make countless right decisions.”

“If you get obsessed with the little stuff, you might miss something truly important. That’s why you bought all the info cards.”

“Yeah. You never know what could help us. I think other mages from different guilds probably made similar choices.”

Just then, Estera knocked.

“Shirone, are you in there?”

“Yes, come in.”

Waiting until he finished speaking, she entered with a guild membership form and a pen.

“I already filled out everything you need. Just check it over and sign here.”

Shirone reviewed the terms.

Guild ranks were divided into Rookie, Senior, Veteran A, and Veteran B—with fees and benefits differing drastically by rank.

“Hm.”

As Shirone looked over the Rookie rank, Estera, quick to catch on, offered an explanation.

“The guild promises top treatment, of course. But still, we can’t just raise someone’s rank arbitrarily. For guild members, this ranking is basically their way of life.”

The higher your rank, the easier it became to earn money—and it was tied directly to reputation and honor.

“That’s fine. I expected that.”

Honestly, if they had offered to boost his rank right away, it would have hurt the guild’s credibility.

“In return, we’ll cover all expenses related to the jobs you take. And if you decide to deposit money with the guild, we can offer premium interest rates.”

Shirone responded honestly.

“Estera, the truth is, I don’t plan to work with the Silver Ring Guild long-term.”

“I know. That’s usually the case with world-class mages. But as long as you don’t formally leave the guild, the contract conditions we’re offering will remain valid for five years. You can even join other guilds abroad.”

All Estera wanted was a document proving that Arian Shirone had joined the Silver Ring Guild.

That alone would give them a leg up over War Wagon and Blood Rose, the other two of the kingdom’s Big Three guilds.

Shirone felt the same way, so he didn’t feel particularly pressured.

‘At least within the kingdom, using the guild will make things easier.’

As he accepted the pen, Shirone asked,

“Just to be sure—leaving the guild really is free, right?”

“Of course. I’m a mage too, you know. I’m not that dumb.”

Of course, even if the chances of keeping Shirone were slim, she had no intention of giving up easily.

‘Well, I’ve done everything a manager can do. The real game begins when he arrives at the main branch in Bashka.’

“Oh, and about that job…”

Estera pulled a letter from her coat.

“Guild work isn’t your main goal, right? Then this should be the easiest for you. The reward’s pretty high too.”

“Is it just delivering a letter?”

“Yup. Just take it to the address listed and hand it directly to a man named Brooks. There’s a receipt enclosed inside, so get his signature and bring it to the Silver Ring Guild in the capital. Then you’ll be paid 87 gold. That’s the amount after fees.”

“Eighty-seven gold? That’s a lot for just a letter.”

It definitely seemed high for a delivery.

“Fees aren’t always based on risk. Sometimes, finding a noble’s lost cat pays better than risking your life in battle.”

“I see.”

“And we don’t give jobs like this to just anyone. Consider it a sign that the guild trusts you.”

“Can I open it?”

He wasn’t the type to snoop, but since the letter wasn’t sealed, there could be misunderstandings later.

“No problem. The client didn’t ask for it to be sealed.”

“Because people get more suspicious when you do?”

The more you try to hide something, the more others want to dig it up—it was human nature.

“It depends on the situation. Maybe the sender really didn’t care. But this time, I think you’re right. The letter is written in code. Some private cipher—I can’t read it either.”

“Encrypted, huh…”

Even if the job seemed easy, Shirone got the sense that 87 gold came with its risks.

“Got it. I’ll deliver it once I reach the capital.”

As Estera stood, she asked,

“What time are you planning to leave? We’ll prep a private carriage for you.”

“Huh? That’s a bit much…”

As far as Shirone knew, even the lowest tier private carriage cost over 10 gold a day to rent.

“It’s fine. Like I said earlier, the guild is covering all associated costs.”

Sure, Shirone’s name alone was worth the money—but more than that, Estera was clearly trying to guide him naturally toward the Silver Ring Guild’s main office.

Since Shirone had joined the guild to travel more efficiently, he didn’t sweat the minor details.

“We’ll leave at sunrise tomorrow.”

“Alright. I’ll make sure everything’s ready. If you need anything, come find me anytime.”

After Estera left, Shirone and Rian exchanged glances and shrugged.

Everything really was just… easier.

Before sunrise, Shirone and Rian woke up, washed, and went down to the first floor.

The guild members had yet to arrive for the day, and a small private carriage was waiting outside in the rear courtyard.

Estera approached, accompanied by a man in his fifties with deep wrinkles etched into his brow.

“Let me introduce you. This is Mr. Austin, a guild member who’ll be escorting you to the capital. He’s 53 years old, specializes in fire magic, and holds a Veteran A rank.”

“Nice to meet you. I’m Shirone.”

“Austin. I look forward to working with you.”

His lean frame and hardened expression spoke of a life shaped by stubborn resolve.

“We’ll be heading out right away. Get in the carriage.”

As Shirone and Rian climbed in, Austin shot a sharp glare at Estera.

“What the hell is this? You’re seriously making me chauffeur a Rookie who joined just yesterday?”

“He’s not just any Rookie.”

Austin knew full well who Shirone was, but after more than 30 years as a mage, his pride wouldn’t be so easily bent.

“Still, why me? There are other guys around.”

“Oh, come on, you know why. I’m not asking you to play coachman—I’m asking you to bring Shirone to the main branch. That helps the guild grow, and it’s good for you, too. I’ll make sure you’re well compensated.”

“You think I’ve stuck with Silver Ring this long for the money? I don’t care if he’s an Arch Mage—no one gets to order my life around.”

As he held his ground, Estera sighed.

“Austin, have you ever seen an Arch Mage?”

“No.”

“Exactly. Neither have I. So how about…”

Clenching her jaw, Estera spoke coldly.

“How about we both shut up and just do our jobs? I’m hanging by a thread here!”

She had no intention of being cold to someone who’d dedicated 30 years to the guild—but this was Shirone. That changed things.

If Austin didn’t want to do it, he could leave.

Austin bit his lip at the unexpected scolding but eventually turned away with a bitter expression.

“...Fine.”

A Veteran A rank mage knew better than anyone that this was a world where everything revolved around money.

“Sorry. I guess I’m a little on edge from not sleeping at all last night.”

Estera wasn’t feeling great about it either, but business was business.

“I know. Being a manager isn’t for the faint of heart.”

To survive, one had to work.

Climbing into the coachman’s seat, Austin adjusted the reins, then opened the passenger window.

“Well then, let’s go. I’ll take the fastest route—just relax and rest.”

“Yes, thank you.”

Shirone wasn’t oblivious, but he knew any comment would only make things worse, so it was better to quietly accept the ride.

The carriage left the city of Kentra, passed through the wide plains of Sadia—Nade’s hometown—and entered the edge of the underground mountain range.

That meant three hours remained until the capital.

“Hm?”

For the first time since setting off, an expression appeared on Austin’s face.

“What’s this?”

A woman, staggering as if injured, was walking straight toward the carriage.

Having seen it all, Austin stopped the carriage a safe distance away.

‘We’re pretty far from the city… What’s someone doing out here?’

“Please help me! Help!”

The woman, clutching her torn clothes with one hand, came running toward the carriage.

“Stop right there!”

A fireball flared in Austin’s hand.

When the woman kept approaching, he hurled the flame to the ground between them.

“Kyah!”

Then, with fireballs now in both hands, Austin shouted,

“Don’t try anything stupid! My flame magic shows no mercy!”

Shirone and Rian stepped down from opposite doors of the carriage.

“What’s going on?”

“Stay inside. It’s nothing serious.”

But to Shirone, it didn’t seem minor at all.

“She looks hurt.”

“Sure she does. But we’re not the ones who are hurt.”

Shirone understood exactly what the veteran mage meant, so rather than approach, he cupped his hands around his mouth and called out,

“Miss! What happened?”

Kneeling before the wall of fire, the woman shouted back,

“I was kidnapped by bandits! Please help me!”

“Bandits?”

That meant the area wasn’t safe either.

“I’ll go check.”

While Austin stood guard with fireballs at the ready, Rian approached the woman.

She was wearing only a torn top and had scratches all over her, as if she’d rolled down a mountain.

“What happened?”

Clutching Rian’s pants, she said,

“I barely escaped! We have to get out of here—they’ll be coming after me!”

Rian glanced back at the carriage as if to leave the decision to Shirone. Shirone then asked Austin,

“Can we give her a ride?”

“You two rented the carriage. It’s your call. But I wouldn’t recommend it.”

“You think she’s lying?”

“Maybe. But that doesn’t matter. What I’m saying is—whether she’s telling the truth or not, if you don’t care, then nothing happens.”

Perhaps that was the philosophy of a guild-trained mage.

“But if there really are bandits, don’t we need to avoid them too?”

Austin had no counterargument for that.

“Rian!”

At Shirone’s signal, Rian helped the woman to her feet.

“Get in the carriage for now. Let’s hear your story.”

“Thank you! Thank you so much!”

Shivering from the cold, the woman burst into tears.
