Chapter 530: Under Coder (1)

The carriage arrived in the capital of the Kingdom of Tormia, Bashka, and quickly turned toward the Jijeo Mountains.

Inside were Miro and Rian, seated across from each other, deep in conversation.

Though they’d ridden nonstop from Baiden, Rian felt no fatigue as he listened to Miro recount everything Shirone had been through.

At times, he wondered if he’d simply fallen for an even greater con artist than Alf. But Miro's strength—evident when she shattered Alf’s sword—wasn’t something born of mere technique. It was beyond human.

And so was his own ability.

‘Divine Transcendence… Yaksha.’

Staring out the window, lost in thought, Rian suddenly asked, “Do you know what the word Smille means, by any chance?”

“Hm? Smille?”

That’s when Rian finally began to share his story.

From the first time he reached Divine Transcendence in Heaven, to the time he ate Ymir’s arm, and the story behind receiving his <Idea> from his grandfather.

“Every time I hear that word, Smille, my wounds seem to regenerate quickly. I’ve decided not to overthink it, but… I’d still like to know what it means.”

“Hmm… I see…”

Intrigued, Miro searched her memory, but the word Smille rang no bells.

‘Maybe Teraje would know…’

But since Teraje wasn’t around, she could only speculate based on what she’d heard.

“Ymir was the pinnacle of beings who’d reached the final stage of the Elixir of Life. If you ate the arm of such a giant, it’s possible you inherited some regenerative ability. But my guess is a bit different.”

Rian looked up.

“I think it’s related to your <Idea>.”

“You mean the sword’s power?”

“No, something deeper. I suspect your grandfather extracted the object called <Idea> from a dream. But as far as I know, extracting an object like that isn’t so simple. According to someone I know who’s experienced it firsthand, the process is extremely complex and difficult.”

“Then how did my grandfather do it…?”

“The village your grandfather stayed in might be the key. Also, the fact that he paid eighty million gold—it’s probably connected. In other words, the <Idea> had already been extracted by someone else. Your grandfather just paid the price and brought it back to reality.”

“But I doubt he’d be carrying that kind of money while hunting bandits.”

“There’s no way for the person to know exactly when the dream began. Likely, the logic of the dream itself played a role. Especially if your grandfather didn’t even know about the world of Drimo—then he’d have no choice but to accept it.”

“I see…”

Miro held up her index finger for emphasis.

“What matters is—if your grandfather wasn’t the one who extracted it, then who did? The odds of someone else meddling in his dream are slim. Which leaves us with one possibility.”

Rian swallowed, waiting for the answer.

“Bloodline. Or lineage. The accumulated thoughts of your ancestors formed the <Idea>, someone from a previous generation extracted it, and your grandfather simply paid the price to retrieve it. That’s probably the closest theory to the truth.”

“Bloodline…”

“I heard the Ogent family passes down both blue and black hair traits, right? The secret might lie there.”

Hearing this, it was as if a light had appeared in the dark tunnel of Rian’s mind.

“And that’s why I believe your regeneration is also connected to <Idea>. Think about it—doesn’t it seem strange? A sword that can’t be broken?”

“Strange how?”

“It’s clearly not just about durability. It belongs to the realm of concepts. More precisely, the power of <Idea> is this: the information that composes the sword cannot be destroyed.”

Miro pointed at Rian.

“If perfection was the will of your ancestors, then that same trait must live on in their descendants. When your body’s information is damaged, it restores itself. That explains why your grandfather heard that voice, Smille, too.”

Rian nodded in genuine awe.

To have deduced so much from so little—she really was a magician.

“I think I understand now. Thank you.”

Miro’s expression remained serious.

“Be careful, Rian.”

“Hmm?”

“Even if your <Idea> is indestructible, your body isn’t. If what happened during the extraction occurred just once for your grandfather, but is happening continuously in you… then maybe Ymir’s arm triggered something. But Ymir was still a living being—and all life has limits.”

It was the part Rian had feared the most.

“Deny is an axing that negates natural law. That’s why you can move supernaturally without reaching your physical limits. But if you push it too far, your body will break. And if that keeps happening…”

Eventually, your body won't recover at all.

It was a terrible fate, one no outsider could truly understand—and so Miro stopped herself there.

“Sorry. I probably just made things worse.”

“It’s alright.”

Rian smiled.

“There was a time I was terrified, but not anymore. Even if the next limit is my last, I’ll still swing with everything I’ve got. Because this is the path I chose.”

Miro nodded solemnly.

‘The Greatblade style, huh…’

That tenacity—the will to cut through anything in his path—was the true spirit of the Ogent family, and it lived on in Rian.

“We’ve arrived at the destination.”

The coachman called out from midway up a mountain path where the road abruptly ended.

They continued on foot, and by the time they reached the Ardino estate, it was already midday.

Miro knocked, but no one came to greet them.

Raising an eyebrow at the voices coming from inside, she turned the doorknob. The door creaked open—it hadn’t even been locked.

“Pwahaha! So anyway, back in my day, I was very popular. One time a woman was staring at me so hard she got hit by a carriage!”

“Oh my, the face that even the gods would envy?”

“Huh? Oh! Y-Yeah! That’s me! Hahaha!”

Miro stared blankly at the sight of Enrique and Marsha laughing like old friends.

Enrique finally noticed her and awkwardly cleared his throat.

“Ahem. You’re here. Thought you’d be later than this.”

Ignoring him, Miro walked up to Marsha.

“When did you get here? You’re a lot earlier than I expected.”

“Hmm? Oh no, I got here about an hour ago.”

“…”

Miro had assumed they’d spent at least three days eating and sleeping under the same roof.

Still, she had to admit—Marsha was freakishly good at winning people over.

“When you want to make a good impression, start with the parents. It’s socializing 101.”

Marsha whispered, then waved to Rian.

“Long time no see. You’ve gotten way taller.”

“Thank you for helping Shirone.”

Rian said the words he’d prepared beforehand.

Seeing how deeply he cared about Shirone, Marsha thought—he hasn’t changed at all, aside from his size.

“No need to thank me. I’m not doing it for free.”

When they entered the room on the second floor, they found Fermi in the middle of a conversation with Arius. He stood up when he saw them.

“You’re fast. You really are my aunt.”

There weren’t many people in the world who could locate two unknowns so quickly.

“Well, I’m a busy woman. Now—here’s the contract.”

Miro placed the Depreciation Trade Contract on the table.

In the buyer's signature field was written the name of the great mage, Milward.

While sold chips could be transferred to others, Teraje was not so careless as to sign someone else’s Regulation Exception herself.

“This is great. Now we just need Shirone’s signature.”

As Fermi picked up the contract, Marsha cut in.

“Wait, let me see too.”

It was only natural she’d be curious—she had once used a Regulation Exception that extracted others’ magic.

After carefully examining the Depreciation Trade Contract, Marsha nodded.

“Well-made. You activate omniscience and omnipotence by digesting the chip. So you’ve twisted the concept of consumption into acquisition.”

Since a situation might arise where the Depreciation Trade would be useful, Fermi crafted a chip himself and tossed it to Marsha.

“It’s a clay-type chip, made to official Tormia standard. The image engraved on the central inlay represents omniscience, and the outer mold pattern contains omnipotence. Once the chip is removed, it has a shelf life of three days before it starts to decay.”

“Ooh.”

If the timer only starts once the chip is removed, then technically, Fermi himself has no expiration to worry about.

He can simply take them out as needed.

However, the buyer and seller must meet with Fermi within that three-day window.

“So you just swallow it?”

“Yes. The omniscience and omnipotence in the chip are digested in the stomach over the contract period and eventually disappear. However, as digestion progresses, the balance between omniscience and omnipotence subtly shifts.”

“You mean the efficiency drops.”

“Correct. At 30% digestion, you need about 1.03 times the original concentration. At 60%, it’s 1.07, and at 90%, it rises to 1.12 times.”

“That’s not a huge drop in performance.”

“It’s hardly noticeable with a single casting. But over time, especially with prolonged use, you’ll definitely feel a bit more fatigue.”

Considering you could learn magic just by eating a chip, that trade-off was barely a drawback.

“Well, well. That’s all fine. Now it’s your turn, Fermi. Since everything’s ready, tell us how we get into the Under Coder.”

“Ah, right. That was the deal.”

Fermi picked up a glass bottle from the lab table.

Inside were dozens of red pills that looked like beans.

“The Under Coder is a space that exists outside the dream. The reason you can’t go there normally is that, the moment you try to leave, you wake up. So you need a special agent that affects the brain.”

“That’s what’s in the bottle?”

“Yes. It’s a stimulant called Dream Star. When it kicks in, it awakens the brain just enough so you don’t wake from the dream, but your senses remain connected to reality. That’s how you leave the dream without waking.”

Miro snatched the bottle and gave it a shake.

“Unbelievable. This kind of drug exists?”

“It’s extremely hard to synthesize. You have to balance on the razor’s edge between dream and reality. It’s very expensive—and even if you have the money, it’s not something you can just buy.”

“Any side effects?”

“There’s no way around it. You’re forcefully awakening your brain. If you get addicted, it’ll be rough. But for a few uses, there shouldn’t be any problems.”

“Alright. So what’s the detailed plan?”

Fermi looked over at Arius.

“For four people to operate together, they need to share the same dream. That’s called Address Sync. We’ve agreed that Arius will handle the tuning of the mental frequencies.”

Given Arius’s deep understanding of the human mind, there was no better person for the task.

And Miro trusted that he would never put her in danger.

“Good. Then we’re ready.”

“One last warning: the final destination is The Abyss. Under Coder is just the gateway. If the Avatar is destroyed, they may never wake up again.”

But they’d come this far fully prepared for that risk. Not one of them wavered at the threat to their lives.

“It doesn’t matter—if it means we can save Shirone.”

Hearing Rian’s resolute conviction, Fermi smiled.

‘This is going to be a fun trip.’
