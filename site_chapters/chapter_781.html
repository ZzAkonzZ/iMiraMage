Chapter 781:

War had broken out.

The Gustav Empire had swiftly advanced on the capital of the Cotria Republic, and now the fall of the enemy nation was imminent.

Gustav Havitz.

Perched atop the highest spire of the imperial palace, Shirone looked out at the night sky with sorrowful eyes.

He’s Extreme Evil.

When Shirone heard that the Gustav Empire had buried 70,000 citizens of the city of Pasifa alive, the shock nearly knocked the breath from him.

And not even a single commander had been present. The captured women were reportedly sold as slaves to Gustav’s own citizens.

How can someone wear the mask of a human and…

Once Cotria fell, the Gustav Empire—its loose ends tied up—would set its sights on the rest of the world.

“I need to find a solution quickly.”

Just as Shirone murmured to himself, Minerva soared up to the spire on her jet.

“When did you get back?”

While the card players were working to figure out how to kill Havitz, Shirone had been focusing on sealing the altars.

“About an hour ago. The area around Aganos is nearly secure. How’s the design of the Lawslayer going? Any progress?”

Minerva shook her head.

“We’ve got a rough outline, but there’s still a lot of fine-tuning to do. The magnitude of change in the laws is just too massive, and we need to handle both Alpha Phi and Beta Phi with extreme care.”

Shirone replied gloomily, “I see.”

“It’s becoming clearer bit by bit. Once we resolve a few key issues, we can put it into motion right away.”

“Yes. Please take care of it.”

Shirone began preparing to leave Aganos.

“You haven’t forgotten, right?” Minerva asked.

“You’re handling this altar only because you're staying in Kashan. The Holy War is a human institution, and you should never forget that Uorin is also using you to advance her own nation’s interests.”

“I’m aware of that much.”

Uorin was not pure.

Even with Mitochondria Eve’s powers allowing her to observe the world from its peak, she was still human.

Her desire to kill Havitz stemmed not from justice, but because the nature of Extreme Evil threatened Kashan’s future.

No matter what she says…

She lived for herself. That’s why Shirone couldn’t approach her with the same honesty he gave to Nane or Miro.

“Nothing will change. I don’t want Havitz to destroy the world either. Same goes for the altars.”

“Good. I’m glad to hear it.”

Just as Minerva exhaled sharply, Gando shot up from below the imperial palace, riding a reverse lightning bolt.

“Her Majesty summons you.”

Shirone raised his hand, channeling Yahweh’s light. In an instant, they vanished.

“There you are. I’m glad.”

When they arrived in Uorin’s chambers, a man Shirone didn’t recognize was sitting across from the card players.

“Who is that?”

“A delegate from the Cotria Republic. He came through the Holy War’s highest-level secure channel. Gustav doesn’t know he’s here.”

Shirone could guess the reason, given that Cotria was already on the brink of collapse.

But isn’t it too late to turn back?

The Cotria Republic’s attempt to ally with Gustav had shattered completely after the mass burial of seventy thousand.

Are they just clinging to whatever hope remains?

With their collaboration exposed, even the Holy War had turned its back on them.

“Sit. We don’t have much time, so I’ll explain quickly.”

Uorin spoke on behalf of the envoy.

“To get to the point, the Cotria Republic will be reintegrated into the Holy War.”

Minerva asked, “And in return, what are we receiving?”

Even if Uorin led the Holy War, she couldn’t unilaterally approve a nation that had colluded with the enemy.

“They’ll give us the ancient weapon—Ex Machina.”

“That’s… sufficient.”

Minerva accepted it. But Shirone, who didn’t know what Exmachina was, still had questions.

“Is it powerful enough to turn the tide of war?”

“It might be. Or it might not.”

Uorin deferred to the experts, and Ness from the card group spoke up.

“Ex Machina is an anti-law weapon. It alters laws within its range. Some say it was created by the archangel of creation, Kariel.”

Kariel had perished.

“Though it’s called a weapon, it’s closer to a computational device. It detects every factor that influences a given variable, analyzes them, and runs simulations until it produces the desired outcome. We can then use those results to revise and reapply the laws.”

Mice added, “Say I want to eat pasta tonight. Someone nearby has to make pasta. For that to happen, maybe his wife has to go to the market and buy ingredients. And for her to buy those ingredients—”

Agaya continued, “—We keep tracing it back like that until we find the originating variable necessary to produce the outcome we want. It might be ridiculous. Like some guy four kilometers away tripping this morning.”

Gudio said, “Of course, Ex Machina only gives simulations. But what if we use its results and make that guy trip four kilometers away in the morning?”

Shirone swallowed hard.

“We could imprint the exact result we want into a specific space-time coordinate. Out of countless possibilities, we could draw the image—one in which Mice is having pasta for dinner, and the entire city’s situation reflects that picture.”

That’s why it’s called a weapon.

“It exceeds human cognitive capacity,” Shirone said.

Gudio nodded. “Hard to call an angel a genius, but only someone like Kariel could’ve created this. Either way, with Ex Machina, our design becomes flawless.”

Uorin chimed in, “It’s believed Ex Machina originated during the war with the ancient Gaia. The weapon was likely intended to control their defiance against Heaven.”

Minerva added, “No other Ex Machina has ever been found. Either they were lost… or maybe they were never mass-produced to begin with.”

“Production was… stopped?”

A possibility occurred to Shirone.

“Exactly. If it’s the latter, it means the Gaia solved the Veron problem. They freed themselves from the laws and became able to alter the future through their will alone. Maybe they were always capable… or maybe they achieved it.”

“They achieved it.”

If it had always been possible, Kariel wouldn’t have created Ex Machina in the first place.

“Sense Eleven: Ultima.”

A race that resisted Anke Ra and broke through the laws of Ex Machina to establish a unified mental system.

Gaia.

Though Shirone had never met them, he missed them deeply.

“By the way…”

Shirone snapped out of his thoughts.

“Even with such a weapon, how did you end up getting completely overwhelmed?”

The envoy answered.

“Ex Machina isn’t omnipotent. There are three main reasons why we lost to Gustav.”

The envoy folded one finger at a time.

“First, lack of personnel. As you might imagine from Ex Machina’s function, its performance varies drastically depending on the user. It was originally designed to be operated by angels. Without at least comparable brain function, a human would go insane the moment they enter the device.”

Understandably, no one would want to step inside under those conditions.

“Second, radius limitation. The maximum range of Ex Machina is 200 kilometers—broad enough to be considered a map weapon—but that doesn’t even cover a quarter of the Cotria Republic. It’s only thanks to this that we’ve managed to protect the capital. That’s why we’re here, seeking an alliance.”

The envoy clenched his fist.

“Third, the Supreme Commander—Balkan. We don’t know how, but every time Ex Machina modifies the laws, he reacts almost instantly.”

It was the crowd-sensing ability—gunki.

“When we change something, so do they. Because he constantly devises new countermeasures, it led to overload, and ultimately, all five primary users of Ex Machina died. We have a secondary team, but with the first team gone, we realistically have no means to keep fighting.”

Revealing this level of internal vulnerability meant they were staking everything on this alliance.

‘The same would apply to the Holy War. If Balkan can read crowd signals, then we’ll need our five strongest as well…’

Shirone raised his hand.

“Wait, did you say five?”

The envoy explained.

“It was originally a system designed for one angel, but the volume of data exceeds what a single human can handle. So the machine was modified—four brains linked in parallel to process the data, and one in series to make the final judgment.”

When Shirone looked to the card game players, Agaya shook his head.

“We’ll handle the parallel nodes. Not just because of our cognitive balance, but also because teamwork is essential.”

They had once lost to Balkan, but as a unit, they were still the undisputed strongest four-man team.

“Then who handles the final slot?”

“The Holy War will have to find someone suitable. The person in that role must make final decisions based on our calculations. A mere computing machine isn’t good enough—every country has those.”

“It requires value judgment,” Shirone said.

Ness nodded.

“Environmental factors are a given, but they must also be able to model human emotions. Someone whose brain function rivals ours, and who has the gambler’s instinct to stake everything when needed. Creativity is also key.”

The requirements were extremely demanding.

“We’ll leave it to the Holy War to decide, but the first person who came to our minds was Snake of the Black Line. He now goes by his real name, Sein, and has joined Sion. If anyone can do it, it’s him.”

Sein’s Iron Eye allowed him to process both emotion and logic simultaneously. But Uorin looked conflicted.

“I’ll send an official request for cooperation, but bringing him in will be difficult.”

“Why? Zion should be more eager than anyone to stop Havitz.”

“Theraphos’s recent decision to cease punishing Lawbreakers has stirred unrest in Heaven. Sein is currently deployed on the heavenly defense front with Miro. This operation might take months, and he likely won’t be available.”

Even the card game experts couldn’t argue. Preventing Havitz’s rampage was critical, but so was defending Heaven.

“That complicates things. Most Savants lack emotional nuance. What we need isn’t a top-tier calculator—we need the perfect gambler. But someone like that...”

“I know someone.”

Shirone’s eyes lit with excitement.

“Someone who can model human emotions with mathematical precision…”

He had once predicted the emotional behavior of every student at Alpheas Magic School and designed an entire holographic pattern accordingly.

“Someone with immense computational power.”

When electricity sparks in his eyes, he can calculate every event happening in a city within three seconds.

“And when necessary, a gambler willing to stake it all.”

He had led his team to victory in the Scramble Royale.

“Someone like that exists?”

Minerva tilted her head, while Uorin smiled knowingly.

“Hmm… Now that would be interesting.”

Encouraged, Shirone stood and turned to Uorin.

“May I make a recommendation?”

She nodded.

“Of course. If it comes from Yahweh himself, the Holy War won’t object.”

As Shirone smiled, the two of them pointed in unison and declared:

“Mercodine Iruki.”
