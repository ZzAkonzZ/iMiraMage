Chapter 1039: Another Belief (2)

Iron Kingdom, one of the Seven Holy Kingdoms, is a powerful nation that represents the eight kingdoms of the southern continent.

Its advanced political system and immense wealth more than make up for its geographical disadvantage of being located at the southernmost tip.

However, at some point, a subtle shift began to ripple through the power dynamics of the southern continent.

It all started with Tears of the Angel—an infamous hallucinogen that was openly distributed in the small kingdom of Kessia.

“It’s quiet.”

The capital of Kessia was eerily tranquil.

Gone were the rampant crimes, the terrorist attacks, and the sight of young girls crying in despair after losing their homes.

It was as if the city had become a ghost town, with only the occasional sound of groaning drifting from the tightly shut houses.

Fermi looked up at the blazing sun.

“This too is a form of peace.”

His unkempt beard covered his chin, and his once-dyed hair now hung long down his back.

“Parmu.”

At the sound of a voice calling out, Fermi turned and smiled with a hint of madness.

“Niacha.”

Though Fermi was originally from the central continent, he had now naturally adopted the southern accent.

“What’s the matter? Everyone should be asleep at this hour.”

A dark-skinned, medium-built man sniffled as he approached Fermi.

“I found you. I was about to head to the company. Do you have it? Tears of the Angel.”

Commonly known as Angel.

“Of course.”

Fermi glanced at Niacha’s dilated pupils as he searched his pockets.

“Stick to the recommended dosage. Too much ruins the fun. Here, take this and head back.”

The small plastic bag he handed over was filled with shimmering white powder, resembling sugar.

“Thanks. But I don’t have any money. I can’t work. Want to come over to my place? You can take my wife if you want.”

In Kessia, no one worked anymore.

“It’s fine. Money is no longer necessary.”

Excluding Iron Kingdom, the remaining seven kingdoms of the southern continent were already in Fermi’s hands.

“Here, take this extra for your wife. Good service is basic courtesy, after all.”

Fermi patted Niacha’s shoulder and handed him another packet of drugs.

“Sniff, sniff.”

Niacha pressed the bag to his nose, shuddering as his eyelids fluttered.

“Ahhh… Finally, I feel alive again.”

Still clutching the bag to his nose, Niacha mumbled, “Parmu, you don’t do Angel, do you?”

“What makes you think that?”

“I don’t know. Just a feeling. Why don’t you take something this good? Doesn’t it hurt?”

“If I indulge, who will make the drugs? Someone has to keep this city running, right?”

“True…”

Niacha sniffled a couple more times before waving as he walked away.

“Thanks, anyway.”

Fermi smiled as he saw him off.

“Stick to the dosage. That’s a warning.”

By tomorrow, Niacha would likely forget he had even met Fermi.

After lingering briefly, Fermi walked along the desolate street, littered only with dust and debris.

At the end of the block stood a modern building, its spire gleaming with the emblem of a golden wheel.

“Welcome, Chairman.”

As he entered the building, the cool air inside chased away the midday heat, brushing against his skin.

“Yes. How’s the excavation team?”

The assistant trailing behind him, her face devoid of makeup and her pupils slightly dilated, replied, “They woke up two hours ago. They’re waiting in the recovery room.”

Fermi’s gaze darkened.

“Three days and seventeen hours in real-world time. That was a tough excavation.”

Passing through the automatic doors, he entered a pristine white room equipped with state-of-the-art medical facilities.

Beds lined the walls—two hundred in total, all empty.

“Oh? You’re here?”

The bathroom door opened, and Marsha stepped out, drying her freshly washed hair.

“Where were you? I’ve been looking for you everywhere.”

The Parrot Mercenary Corps, which had allied with the Golden Wheel, had now risen to second place in the global guild rankings.

This remarkable rise included not just mercenary guilds but all existing guilds worldwide.

“I went for a walk. Just a little stroll.”

“…You’ve been going out more often lately. But the streets are empty—there’s nothing to see, right?”

“Isn’t that what makes it beautiful?”

Pondering his words, Marsha picked up a metal box from the bed.

“Here. This one was tough. The danger zone was deep, and it felt like digging down 200 years…”

“You’ve worked hard.”

Receiving the box, Fermi inspected the gem embedded in the center, confirming it hadn’t been tampered with.

“How much should I pay you?”

Marsha, pulling on a sweater, replied, “Still stingy, huh? You’ve raked in all the wealth of seven kingdoms, and you’re still tight-fisted.”

“That’s why I’m asking.”

“We’re in this for the long haul, so I’ll take a modest sum. Eight billion gold. Prepare it in large-denomination coins.”

Fermi smiled with narrowed eyes.

“Eight billion gold for four days of work.”

“We’ve got a lot of mouths to feed. Once it’s divided, there’s not much left. Just get it ready—I’m off to get a drink.”

Patting his shoulder as she passed, Marsha left, and Fermi stowed the box away.

“I’ll deliver it the usual way.”

“Oh, and…”

Just before stepping out, Marsha turned back.

“You should shave. Have you not visited the kids even once since then?”

“That’s… a personal matter.”

“Yeah, I figured. You’ll handle it your way, I’m sure. Just thought it might be better than wandering the empty streets.”

Once Marsha had left and the automatic door closed, Fermi turned to look in the mirror.

It felt as though a stranger was staring back at him.

“…Has it really been that long?”

When you spend your time ‘excavating’ future events unknown to the present, it’s easy to lose track of time.

Entering the bathroom, Fermi showered in warm water and lathered his face with soap.

Shhkk, shhkk.

The beard, which he couldn’t remember growing, was cleanly shaved off, and he began cutting his long hair with a razor.

Staring into the mirror afterward, he finally saw the clean-shaven face of the man once known as Fermi.

“Chairman.”

Emerging from the bathroom, still naked, he was greeted by his secretary, who lowered her head.

“Bring me my clothes. I’m going there.”

“…Yes.”

Momentarily sluggish due to the Angel, she brought him a fine suit.

After meticulously buttoning up, Fermi stood in front of the full-length mirror, inspecting himself for a long while.

“Hm.”

Feeling ready, he headed to a room where dozens of children lay asleep.

Each was hooked up to IV drips, with doctors in gowns checking their vitals and adjusting the dosage.

“Chairman.”

A masked doctor bowed as Fermi placed a hand on his shoulder and continued walking.

“F-Fermi.”

The only adult in the room, a woman with hazy eyes, sat up weakly.

She was Rachel, the director of the Aramones Orphanage and once a close companion of Fermi’s mother, Yolga.

“Teacher.”

And perhaps… the first woman Fermi had ever loved.

The doctor quickly flipped through the chart.

“The dosage of Angel hasn’t exceeded the recommended amount. The Emotion Disease is being suppressed, and consciousness is maintained for about four hours a day.”

Rachel spoke.

“Fermi, you can’t do this. Giving drugs to these children… This is a grave sin.”

Before Fermi started ‘excavating’ the timeline of the Emotion Disease outbreak, he had sworn never to see her again.

To Fermi, Rachel wasn’t just a caretaker but also a woman who had taken Yolga’s place.

“The drugs let them sleep peacefully. You know this. Without the drugs, they would suffer unbearable pain.”

Rachel knew that, but the prolonged use of Angel had weakened her rationality.

“I don’t know what to do… How can I help these children? Is this really the best solution?”

“You don’t need to think about it. I’ll do the thinking. Don’t do anything. I’ll take care of it. Just lie down and dream peacefully.”

As Fermi gently helped her lie back on the bed, Rachel’s eyes wavered as she looked up at him.

‘Fermi…’

It had been the same back then.

Even though he was just the son of a colleague and only nine years old, she had allowed a fleeting mistake—a kiss—because…

‘He was brilliant and incredibly gifted.’

She had been drawn to that overwhelming sense of possibility, relying on it without realizing she was giving in.

“Fermi, I… I actually…”

“I’ll come back again.”

As if knowing what she was about to say, Fermi cut her off before she could finish.

“Ah… ah…”

Rachel’s eyelids fluttered as the increased IV dosage lulled her into a deep sleep.

“Let her rest for a while. Any emotional fluctuations or heightened states could worsen the disease.”

“Understood.”

As the doctor bowed slightly, Fermi turned his gaze toward the children in the orphanage.

Though they occasionally twitched or convulsed, they all slept peacefully, their faces calm.

“Enjoy your dreams.”

Not in the hell of reality, but in the sweetness of slumber.

“Chairman.”

As Fermi left the room, the Golden Wheel’s attendant followed closely, the sound of her heels echoing softly behind him.

“The Holy War Assembly is approaching. Wouldn’t it be wise to secure Iron Kingdom beforehand, at least?”

“Leave it for now. They have the card we need.”

Fermi’s words required no further scrutiny.

“Understood. In that case, I’ll prepare a statement for the seven southern kingdoms, excluding Iron.”

“Hm.”

Fermi thought of Shirone.

“Hold off on that too. We’ll have a visitor soon. We should wait until the pieces are fully in place, don’t you think?”

“Yes, sir.”

The attendant noted his words and stopped walking. Fermi continued alone into his office.

He placed the box he had received from Marsha on a shelf, then sat in the chair facing the large glass window.

‘Mother.’

As he gazed at the desolate cityscape, Fermi recalled Yolga’s face from their meeting in Istas.

‘Don’t worry. I’ll handle it.’

Because I’m your son.

A woman who had left humanity with a choice for the future before she passed away.

She was often compared to the legendary Krixen Miro, but her nature leaned more toward philanthropy.

“Ugh…”

Veins bulged on Fermi’s neck.

‘It’s starting again.’

The reason he refrained from taking Angel was that it clouded his mind, making it difficult to ‘excavate’ history.

“Damn it.”

As the pain of the Emotion Disease spread throughout his body, his face twisted like that of a wild beast.

What else should he give up?

He had already discarded everything unnecessary to achieve his goal.

Suddenly, his eyes gleamed with a deadly resolve, and his expression turned fierce.

Gripping a military knife in reverse, he slammed it down hard into the back of his left hand.

Thud!

The blade pierced through his hand and the armrest of the chair, and blood began to drip down.

“Tsk.”

Clicking his tongue, Fermi pulled out a cigarette, tilted his head slightly, and lit it.

Fwooo…

Leaning back into the chair, he exhaled a long stream of smoke toward the window.

“…That’s better.”

The sun outside blazed with an almost unbearable intensity.
