Chapter 364: Worst Projects (2)

Shirone reached the 18th floor. Although any staff member could access it, stepping past the Association President's office door was another matter entirely.

Standing in front of the door, Shirone took a deep breath. Flu spoke beside him.

“Listen carefully. Beyond this door is the President of the Association.”

Shirone turned to look at Flu.

“Whatever you hear in there, it must never leak out. This is on a different level from what we know. From now on, I won’t ask you anything, and you shouldn’t reveal anything either.”

Shirone sensed Flu’s resolve and acknowledged that he, too, had risked everything to get here. He was curious about what Gaold would say but hoped it would be something he could handle.

Flu knocked on the door for him.

“Shirone has arrived.”

The door opened, and Gangnan peeked out. She scanned the hallway briefly before turning her gaze to Shirone.

“You’ve come. You recovered quickly.”

“Yes, thanks to your care.”

“Come in. Flu, you can head back now. Well done.”

Flu wasn’t disappointed; he knew this was where his involvement ended.

“I only did what I needed to. I’ll take my leave.”

Once Flu departed, Gangnan opened the door wide.

Inside, Gaold sat sunk into the old sofa, his newly dyed hair contrasting with the smoky scent of cigars clinging to the wallpaper.

“You’re here? Sit.”

Shirone took a seat across from Gaold as Gangnan served tea.

No one spoke. Everyone knew who needed to open the discussion.

“Miro…”

Gaold finally broke the silence.

“She was my classmate at school.”

“Yes, I know.”

“She was a strange one—no, more like eccentric. One day, she said she could create life. When I told her to prove it, she said she needed a cadaver.”

Gaold began his story about Miro. Shirone, curious about Gaold, was equally intrigued by Miro, so he listened intently.

“So, we snuck into the anatomy lab and retrieved a cadaver. Just a few samples—if we’d been caught, we’d have been expelled. But she didn’t care at all. Then we went to her lab, where she had this tank on a table, surrounded by bizarre equipment and tangled hoses…”

Gaold waved his hands dramatically, as if mimicking the chaos.

“Yes, I can picture it.”

“She stuffed all sorts of strange things into the tank. It was revolting. I was ready to leave, but she wouldn’t let me. Said she needed my help. I was stuck.”

It was hard to imagine the intimidating Gaold being helpless under someone else’s control, but Shirone couldn’t help but chuckle at the thought.

“Once everything was set up, Miro handed me a small cup and told me to provide a sample of my semen.”

“Cough, cough!”

Caught mid-sip, Shirone choked on his tea.

“D-did you…?”

“Have I lost my mind? Of course, I refused and stormed out. But the next day, she had obtained it somehow—said she bought it. She continued the experiment, boiling everything into a grotesque liquid. She called it ‘primordial soup.’ I made a bet with her: if anything came out of that mess, I’d run around the school in my underwear.”

Shirone smiled faintly. It seemed school life had hardly changed over the years.

“A month later, I went back to the lab. Miro was ecstatic, completely out of her mind. I didn’t understand. Did she really think something would come out of that? I’d just humored her because she was so eccentric.”

“Haha, I see.”

Then Gaold’s face lost all emotion.

“But there was something.”

“… What?”

“That grotesque liquid had dried up, leaving the tank coated in sticky mucus. Inside, there was a living organism I had never seen before.”

“Couldn’t she have brought it from somewhere else…?”

Gaold shook his head.

“No. That was my first thought too. But any living creature humans can imagine is based on pre-existing patterns of knowledge. This was entirely different—something born from a completely independent environment.”

Shirone found it hard to believe, but Gaold’s eyes were filled with conviction. Even as the foremost Grand Mage, his gaze trembled with the shock of that memory.

“I swear, if you had been there, you would’ve felt the same—a gut-deep recognition that this creature was utterly alien.”

“How did Miro create it?”

Could humans truly create life? The current pinnacle of bio-magic was artificial constructs like Harvesters, but no one would dare call those true lifeforms.

“Miro never explained. She swore me to secrecy, saying the world must never know. But now I have a theory: Miro was the greatest spatial-scale mage. In her created spaces, she could accelerate billions of years of evolution.”

“She… evolved a primordial organism?”

“Perhaps. But that’s not the point. The point is, I lost the bet and had to run around school in my underwear. I was branded a pervert for life.”

‘That hardly seems important…’

Shirone swallowed her unspoken thoughts.

Gaold chuckled, sensing her inner reaction, though he didn’t take back his words.

“Miro knew that lifeform wouldn’t last. It died within a week. She cried a lot that day. You might think, ‘Why create something just to lose it?’ But that was her—unpredictable and strange.”

Shirone nodded. Just from this anecdote, it was clear Miro was far from ordinary.

“It wasn’t just lifeforms. She was a genius of creation, inventing countless, unimaginable devices. I once asked her how she did it, and she said, ‘I just see it.’ When she hears the name of an object, the mechanics instantly come to her mind.”

“Hearing the name…?”

One thought immediately flashed through Shirone’s mind.

“Yes. Perhaps she had access to the Akashic Records. Regardless, Miro’s genius quickly drew attention in the magical community. She unlocked the Immortal Function early on and could twist spacetime with magnetic fields. But she didn’t care about fame; her mind was consumed by curiosity. That’s how the Paranormal Spiritual Science Society was formed.”

“I’m a member of that group too.”

Shirone felt proud of her affiliation.

“Ha, congratulations on ruining your life. There were three founding members: me, Miro, and some airheaded guy named Saine.”

“Isn’t Saine the Servant Syndrome guy? The one who made Istas’ Master Key?”

“Yeah… not exactly my favorite person.”

Gaold brushed off the topic with a single comment. Shirone was curious about Saine’s whereabouts, but it seemed that would remain a mystery.

“And now… this is the top-secret information you need to know.”

Gaold’s expression turned solemn, and Shirone gulped.

“Miro’s genius brought about disaster. The world’s twelve most powerful leaders—the Holy War—began to monitor her. They had long been watching the affairs of Heaven. This led to the creation of the Valkyries, the Holy War’s army.”

Shirone’s face darkened. She recalled King Orcamp of Kazra summoning her, intending to use Ataraxia to influence the Valkyries.

“Realizing the Final War was near, the Holy War sought a successor to Goffin. Miro was chosen. In an instant, a young girl became humanity’s bulwark. Can you imagine bearing the curse of immortality, trapped in an eternal solitude?”

Shirone couldn’t bring herself to respond.

“Eventually, a tribunal was convened, known as the Judgment of Twenty. Imperial envoys, the Pope of the Lami Church, paragons of virtue, and human rights leaders—all gathered to decide her fate. For balance, even her mentor, Alpheas, and Torhmia’s council auditor, Olivia, had voting rights. The result: 16 for, 1 against, 3 abstained. Miro was condemned to exile.”

“How could they…?”

To Miro, it was a choice between humanity’s survival and her own life. Even if she had agreed voluntarily, questioning the truth was inevitable. For twenty people to arbitrarily end her life was unimaginable.

“Indeed. The process felt overwhelmingly complex to me, yet the world seemed to move forward easily. No one opposed the premise of saving humanity through one person’s sacrifice. Days passed in a blur, and the day of her departure arrived. And Miro…”

Gaold’s voice choked, and then he fell silent. His gaze drifted blankly into space as if lost in thought.

His breath quickened, and veins pulsed in his reddening eyes. Tears welled in those unblinking eyes—tears that seemed entirely out of place on his rugged face.

Please, spare Miro! I’ve lived my life as a faithful servant of God, without a single shameful act. Take my soul if you must, but I beg you, spare Miro…!

You’re Gaold, aren’t you?

I beg you… Grant this humble servant your grace! Show mercy, oh divine one…!

Even God will forgive us.

Shirone’s shocked gaze darted around as the furniture in the room rattled violently, as if an earthquake had struck.

‘What… is this?’

Turning to Gaold, Shirone felt her breath catch in her throat. His tears had dried, but within his eyes swirled an uncontrollable storm of emotions.

The scene shifted, engulfed in roaring flames.

This was on a different level from the bunker’s terror. The only difference was that this time, Gaold was part of the inferno.

Gaold’s eyes rolled upward, whites showing, as his face twisted into a grotesque grimace. His lips curled back, baring teeth in a monstrous snarl.

‘This is… too much.’

Even with the strength of her awakened abilities, Shirone felt the sheer physical force pressing down on her. If this continued, the entire 18th floor might explode.

Through the chaos, Gangnan’s low, sharp voice pierced the fiery storm.

“Mr. President.”

Like a lie, the hellish scene disappeared. Gaold narrowed his eyes as if he had just come to his senses.

Only then could Shirone breathe. He hadn't even realized his entire body was drenched in cold sweat.

Gaold leaned back on the sofa as if nothing had happened. But there would be no more talk about the Judgment of the Twenty.

"Anyway, that's how Miro left this world. Since that day, I've been planning a project, and now it's almost ready to be realized. This is why I called you here."

Shirone calmed his racing heart and prepared to listen. Gaold, watching him for a moment, suddenly leaned forward.

"I will destroy Miro's spacetime."

"......"

Shirone took in Gaold's words and analyzed their meaning. The realization was even more chilling than the initial shock.

"Destroy Miro's spacetime...?"

It meant he intended to bring Miro back. From Gaold's perspective, it was a given.

But if that happened... this world would be on the path to destruction.

"If Miro's spacetime disappears..."

"Yes. The army of Heaven will invade. Eight archangels, dozens of ordinary angels, hundreds of fallen angels, thousands of demons, giants and fairies, ancient magic, schemas, and subjects armed with the firepower of mecha weapons. No matter who wins, our world will be devastated. This is what I'm telling you."

Gaold did not shy away from the truth. His resolve, unchanged for twenty years, would not waver now.

Shirone rubbed his face with both hands. The fact that his fears were becoming reality was suffocating. No, it was an even bigger problem than he had anticipated.

It was about rescuing one woman at the cost of all life in this world. He wasn't panicking or overreacting. Faced with an insurmountable problem, his mind felt coldly clear.

"Is there a way?"

If there had been a way to bring Miro back, Gaold would have done it long ago. There must be some nearly impossible condition to fulfill.

"Of course there is."

Gaold smiled pleasantly. He had even more faith in Shirone, who never lost sight of the core issue in any situation.
