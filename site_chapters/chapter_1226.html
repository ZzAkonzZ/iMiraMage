Chapter 1226:

While Yorahan wrestled with his inner turmoil, Areon stood proudly before the Fireborn, showing off his power.

“Behold! I conquered this forest with just a hundred cavalrymen. And I command an army of over ten thousand. Soon, this world will be beneath my feet.”

With eyes that knew no fear of the world, Armand found herself thinking:

He’s… attractive.

Young, beautiful, and born seemingly only to dominate—this proactive presence…

So this is what humans call royal blood.

It wasn’t strange that his very presence seemed to glow.

But I don’t like it.

That light made her uneasy.

A light that insists on shining alone is never truly beautiful. Yorahan…

He was someone who would become a light that embraced the whole world.

Chieftain Luthia asked:

“What do you intend to do with us?”

“I am merciful,” Areon said, though his cold blade pressed against Luthia’s throat told a different story.

“But only to my subjects.”

Withdrawing his sword, he looked over the Fireborn.

“You will soon become citizens of the glorious kingdom. You’ll be entrusted with an important task—serving your king. You’ll be given fine homes, food, and treasures beyond imagining.”

Luthia responded calmly.

“We desire nothing. We only wish to live peacefully in this forest.”

“A pity,” Areon said, his eyes turning cold.

“You don’t need to be good at anything. Because I am. Do you understand what that means?”

“I do not.”

“It means all you’re expected to do… is obey.”

As Areon raised his sword to strike Luthia down, Armand stepped forward and blocked the blow with her own.

A sharp clash rang out. The cavalry looked on in disgust, ready to charge.

Areon raised a hand.

“Stand down.”

As the cavalry backed off, Fisque stepped forward.

“She’s a carnivore-type. It may take a little time to tame her. Leave her to me.”

If someone had to kill the children, he preferred to do it himself rather than have soldiers do it messily.

“No need,” Areon said, stepping back with full confidence.

“Woman, what’s your name?”

“Armand.”

“I like it.”

That was all that mattered.

“You’re not a slave. You’re strong and bold. Come to me—I’ll rescue you from this hell.”

Having lived with Yorahan for over two years, Armand now understood the language of men.

“I’m married. I can’t.”

“Hah! Didn’t I say I was merciful? I don’t care what happened before we met. But if you reject me now, it’s a crime that justifies the death of all the Fireborn.”

Armand said nothing.

I’m sorry, everyone…

She couldn’t betray Yorahan. If the Fireborn were slaughtered, she would raise her sword at that moment.

“Hmph. I see…”

Areon glared at her with wild eyes as she stood silently in refusal.

“Kill them all.”

“Wait! Please, wait!”

Yorahan came rushing out of the storehouse. For the first time, emotion appeared on Armand’s face.

Relief flooded in so hard she forgot their dire situation—but it quickly turned to dismay.

Why did you come? You idiot…

Areon narrowed his eyes.

“Who are you? A traitor among the Fireborn?”

“I—I’m human. A wandering philosopher. I was captured by the Fireborn and subjected to humiliation for two years.”

“Captured? By this docile race?”

“This woman, Armand, is my wife. I spent two years here sharing knowledge and technology. I was constantly monitored to prevent any escape or harm.”

He stuck to the truth—any sloppy lie would ruin their only chance.

Areon glanced at Fisque, who said:

“It’s plausible. Ancient records describe carnivore-types as cruel and savage.”

The scholar likely recorded the facts—but ignored why those Fireborn had to become cruel and savage toward humans.

“So that’s how it is,” Areon mused, eyes flicking curiously between Yorahan and Armand.

“If you were held against your will, then you won’t mind if I take your wife, right?”

“T-that’s…”

“Why? Does that upset you?”

“N-no, Your Highness! I wouldn’t dare harbor such thoughts! I… I only have one request.”

“A request?”

Yorahan bowed deeply.

“Please take me to Mikhas. I’ll do anything you command, with all my strength!”

Areon, used to kneeling scholars and geniuses from across the continent, was pleased.

“Hah! A philosopher with an eye for the times. Very well. I’ll keep you at my side.”

“Thank you!”

Humans could never truly see one another’s truths. The Fireborn’s sense of betrayal was understandable.

Why…?

But Armand knew the truth of Yorahan.

There’s no way.

She knew he wasn’t the kind of man to abandon his wife—not even if it cost him his life. That only deepened her confusion.

“Then it’s settled. Even your husband kneels before me. So how about you make your choice?”

Because she trusted Yorahan, Armand lowered her sword. Areon took it as a gesture of submission.

“Yes, this is how the world works. Now stand. You’ll have much to do in service of me.”

From the lowest place, Yorahan looked up at the world.

And Areon, feet planted firmly, looked only skyward.

But did he know?

I can see it.

That place—right between the highest and lowest—is where humanity’s throat is most vulnerable.

Yorahan’s expression went blank.

And in one fluid motion, he rose—and swung the dagger hidden in his coat.

“Huh?”

Because it wasn’t hatred or resentment—because his motion felt more like a blade swung without an object, smooth and fluid…

“W-what…?”

A spray of blood erupted from Areon’s nape.

“Prince!”

Yorahan had slashed precisely at a vulnerable spot the armor couldn’t protect. As he turned back, he shouted:

“Run!”

The Fireborn were stunned.

“Teacher…?”

None of them had ever imagined he could harm someone.

“What are you all doing?! You wanna die here?!”

If the entire world issued commands, the only one they would follow was Yorahan.

“Everyone, into the forest! Now!”

The Fireborn snapped out of it and ran with everything they had.

Knowing they wouldn’t be able to catch anyone once they reached the forest, the cavalry commander mounted his horse.

“Chase them! Don’t let a single one escape!”

“Wait!”

Fisque’s shout made the cavalry rear back.

“The prince…”

Areon’s condition was dire.

They tried applying pressure to the wound, but hot blood kept pouring through their palms.

“He missed the spinal cord and cut only the artery… Just to buy time to escape? This guy… he knows anatomy.”

Back then, few did.

“Fi… Fisque…”

Areon’s face was swelling like a balloon. He clutched Fisque’s shoulder, forcing out words.

“Save… me… Save me…”

Damn it!

Fireborn or not, if the prince died, their lives were over too.

Fisque lifted his head and barked,

“We’re retreating to the base with the prince—now! Not a single second to waste!”

To make it through the forest in the shortest time, sacrifices from the cavalry would be necessary.

“Please hang on, Your Highness!”

Even as they transported Areon, Fisque focused all his knowledge on stopping the bleeding.

Soil can block wounds, but this blood loss is too much. Then maybe… water’s power…

Could he create blood?

In the distant future, humanity would understand all things—but only after many more wars.

“Hrrrrgh…”

Sweat poured down Fisque’s face. He was desperate.

“I don’t know how!”

No matter how he combined the four elements—air, earth, water, and fire—he couldn’t create blood.

“…Iron?”

Or maybe something else entirely.

“Fisque…”

Perhaps due to his heightened concentration, Areon’s voice grated on him.

“Your Highness…”

Areon was already deathly pale. Anyone could see he was at death’s door.

“I don’t… want to die…”

And with that, the prince of the Mikhas Kingdom passed away—without even closing his eyes.

“…It’s over.”

Even if he returned to the kingdom now, the king would surely execute him.

“Blood…”

But it no longer mattered.

“Blood, huh…”

If blood had destroyed his life, then perhaps it was also worth dedicating his life to.

“Hmph.”

Fisque jumped from the moving carriage and watched as the prince’s corpse was carried off toward Mikhas.

According to Omega’s records, Fisque spent the rest of his life trying, but ultimately failed to create blood.

He did manage to write a comprehensive volume titled The Composition and Components of Blood, but that had no bearing on the Dream of Inheritance.

Still deep in the dream, Shirone’s unconscious mind now turned to Yorahan’s anguish.

“Hah… hah…”

Running through the forest, leading the Fireborn, Yorahan felt the world go black.

Only when he collapsed to his knees did he realize how short of breath he was. Pain surged through him.

“Yorahan…”

Armand couldn’t speak.

“I… I…”

Hands trembling, Yorahan mumbled with hollow eyes:

“I cut someone. I… I cut a person…”

His ideals of love and peace, his rejection of violence—gone in an instant.

No—this wasn’t about reason.

“Hrrrngh…”

It was fear. Like a frog that had swallowed poison, something writhed inside his heart.

“Armand, I… I can’t…”

Yorahan’s eyes began to roll back, and Armand hurriedly linked their hearts through Small World Resonance.

The moment they became one, she wept.

How fragile…

He was the kind of man who couldn’t even kick a stone on the road without guilt.

Armand whispered:

“I’m sorry. It’s all my fault. I couldn’t protect you… I wanted to help you… make your dream come true.”

They say sharing sorrow halves it—and this time, it truly did. Yorahan slowly calmed down.

“No… it’s not your fault.”

Had he just been wandering through a dream this whole time?

“I was foolish. I never lost anything. I dreamed high ideals without knowing reality.”

“Yorahan…”

“Let’s hide. Let’s just disappear forever. Let’s live in peace with the Fireborn, in a place without violence or selfishness.”

“But your dream… You said you wanted to save humanity.”

“So many Fireborn have already died. I don’t want to lose anyone else. And without you, I…”

Their hearts were already one—she could feel it too. He had no regrets.

“…Okay. I understand.”

If that was what her husband wished for, that was enough.

“Let’s go. Deep into the forest, where no one can find us. Where no one can ever interfere with us.”

“…Yeah.”

And so, Yorahan gave up on his ideals.

Even so, the Dream of Inheritance continued, and Shirone found himself understanding.

So that’s how it is.

Because he knew better than anyone how hard it was to live by love, he also understood what it meant to give it up.

Now the only thing left for him is…

The first Yora.

After thesis, antithesis, and synthesis—his final trial was about to unfold.
