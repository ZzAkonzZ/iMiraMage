Chapter 1181:

Shirone and his party arrived at the Sun.

Though they were still far away, they could no longer approach due to the attacks from Red Flames and Corona.

Ashur spoke.

“There is nothing that can withstand solar energy. Only spiritual bodies can survive.”

The solar winds shook the space galleon.

“Ikael will be fine, and since Amy can wield the Idea of Fire, she should manage as well. But Shirone…”

Everyone turned to look at Shirone.

“The system will crash. Half of this world may be quantum, but the Sun itself embodies the most powerful force in the laws of the universe.”

Amy asked,

“Are you sure? If you can’t endure, Ikael and I can go alone. In simultaneous events, one mistake puts everything at risk.”

Shirone stared at the Sun.

‘Can I do it?’

Even if he reduced the light's transmittance to near zero, everything outside the window was still a blinding white.

“I’ll be fine. I’ll endure with everything I have.”

“Then, I will now disassemble the galleon. The moment it disappears, you’ll be exposed to extreme energy.”

The party swallowed hard.

“Please… stay safe.”

As the space galleon broke apart into panels and vanished, the Sun enveloped them at the speed of light.

“Huff…!”

Amy’s body ignited, and even Ikael struggled to maintain her existence.

Before the immense signal of the Sun, no concept could sustain itself.

Shirone gritted his teeth.

‘It’s just laws.’

As the Miracle Stream enveloped his body, it began pushing back the solar energy.

Even so, the space Shirone managed to secure was a mere seven-meter-wide sphere.

“Phew.”

Amy, now transformed into the Idea of Fire, and Ikael, whose body had begun to dissolve, turned to face him.

“You did it.”

Seven meters in radius was minuscule in the vastness of space, but the fact that a human could survive at all sent chills down Ikael’s spine.

Another solar wind swept over them.

“It must be Satiel. The laws of this world change from the Sun’s core.”

Amy asked,

“Even if something changes at the core, wouldn’t it take time for it to reach the surface?”

“Time doesn’t matter. The moment an event occurs, the result is already determined. Humans perceive cause and effect in sequence, but that’s an illusion.”

Shirone added,

“The moment the cause changes, the result is already rewritten. That’s why Omega’s records are measured in thousandths. You can’t calculate a percentage unless the end is predetermined. The ancient Gaians—or rather, Anke Ra—must have already known the final outcome.”

Amy sighed.

“It’s the year Omega 999 now, right?”

“Yeah. Probably 99.9%, nearly 100%. We're right at the threshold of entering the Outer World.”

Not much time was left.

“So this is how it ends. Both human life and this universe… the moment we open the Outer World…”

“It’s only the judgment of the laws.”

Shirone spoke.

“It’s just a computation. In the end, humans are the ones who decide this world’s fate.”

Though God might refuse to acknowledge that.

Even Ikael, who believed in Goffin, found herself conflicted as a creation of God.

‘Maybe human thought is also just a program. No one can truly know yet.’

Not until they reached the Outer World.

‘But in the end, humanity has come this far. We’ve truly reached the very doorstep of the Outer World.’

The arrival of Yahweh.

Even across infinite multiverses, such an event was unimaginably rare.

Shirone glared at the Sun.

‘To reach this place, the universe had to endure an eternity. But from the perspective of the Outer World, where imaginary time flows…’

All these events would be nothing more than a fleeting anomaly. To the gods, Shirone’s billions of years of journey had just been discovered.

‘To Yahweh, this is nothing more than an error. Just another world among the countless ones he has erased.’

But was that really the case?

McClain Goffin—the god who had sent out a single signal beyond the photon system.

‘Hexa.’

Humanity, the mind—he would prove them.

Through the light of Elikia, the Holy War had learned that Havitz had killed Smodo.

This meant there was now a one-hour grace period.

During that time, the leaders of each nation confirmed the death of King Kitra of Paras.

Under the official certification of Mito Shirano from the International Tribunal, Paras’ representatives opened the coffin.

“You may verify it yourself.”

Kitra lay peacefully with his eyes closed, and a serpent coiled around his neck, flicking its tongue.

“W-what is this snake?”

“It was His Majesty’s beloved pet during his lifetime. By national law, it will be entombed with him.”

Interfering with another nation’s customs was one thing, but the problem was that no one could touch the body.

‘Is he really dead?’

At a glance, his face was pale, and his chest showed no signs of movement.

Shirano stepped forward.

“I’ll verify it. It has to be certified anyway.”

Without hesitation, she reached out—only for the serpent to sink its fangs into her hand.

“Hiek!”

Officials stepped back, but Shirano’s expression didn’t change as she pressed her fingers against his carotid artery.

‘No heartbeat.’

The snake bit her multiple times.

Hiss! Hiss!

True to her nickname, the "Swamp-Colored Witch," the venom had no effect on her.

Licking the bite mark on her hand, she announced,

“Death confirmed. If anyone has doubts, check for yourself. Don’t complain about it later.”

“Hm.”

Since no one else stepped forward, the Paras officials closed the coffin and spoke.

“The acting authority will be decided soon. We will receive voting rights then, so handle the process accordingly.”

“Understood. It’s best to do this quickly. The Holy War emergency conference will be held in an hour.”

Because of Havitz.

“We’ll take care of it as soon as possible.”

As the workers carried the coffin back to their sector, Shirano watched uneasily.

‘Killed by a projectile?’

Aside from the elderly King of Kessia, even Balkan and Jin Kang wouldn’t have died instantly from such an attack.

‘Well, I verified it myself.’

At least officially, Kitra was dead.

Kessia Sector.

Fermi handed Seriel a neatly organized file in his office.

“Here, the Angel formula.”

Seriel silently accepted it, flipping through the pages as if verifying the contents.

‘This isn’t why I came.’

The expression on Fermi’s face that morning, and the belated news of the king’s death.

“Are you okay?”

When Seriel looked up again, Fermi was slumped in his chair.

“What?”

“So much has happened. People have died in just a few hours, and there’s been a terrorist attack.”

“Oh, the attack.”

Right, he had thought about that much.

“I don’t understand what’s happening anymore. It’s just… chaos.”

“Don’t overthink it,” Fermi said.

“There’s only one reason humans move—desire. Some seek money, some seek power, some seek pleasure.”

“And humanity?”

Seriel asked.

“The world turning into hell? The countless people suffering in it?”

“We weren’t born to think about that in the first place. We just live. If you start attaching meaning to humans, nothing can ever be predicted.”

“That’s not true. Even now, countless people are enraged. They’re protesting in front of the Holy War headquarters—”

“Of course they are. That’s exactly what politicians want.”

“What?”

“The fundamental strategy of financial circulation. There are only two times people spend money recklessly. First, when they have more money. Second, when they’re so furious their lids fly off.”

“Politicians aren’t the driving force of the economy. They leech off corporations, sucking money out of them. But corporations need to pull money from the people. So what do politicians do? They need to make the people as furious as possible.”

Fermi tapped his throat.

“Just tight enough that they suffocate right before passing out. Cross the line, and it’s a rebellion. But as long as they don’t, it’s far more effective than injecting cash into the economy. If you degrade the quality of life overall, unnecessary spending skyrockets.”

Seriel let out a weary sigh, sadness creeping onto her face.

“Just stay out of it. Focus on your work. Make medicine, save patients. And never get involved with politicians. As long as you think humans are special, you’ll never beat those beasts.”

“And you?”

Seriel asked.

“Do you really believe there’s nothing special about humans? That they’re just animals driven by desire, and that’s all?”

‘Master…’

To be competent meant being able to endure and endure until you achieved your desired outcome.

Fermi remained silent.

But to Seriel, that silence was already an answer. Her expression darkened with disappointment as she turned away.

“I’m leaving. Take care.”

She had likely come to comfort him.

Watching her retreating figure, Fermi impulsively rose from his chair.

“Wait.”

He pulled her into an embrace from behind, making Seriel’s shoulders tense.

“W-what are you doing?”

“Just… stay for a little while.”

Of course, she had come thinking she might comfort him, but she hadn’t braced herself for this.

“Don’t do this.”

Seriel clenched her fists.

“Once might be a mistake, but twice is something you can’t take back. You’re smart, aren’t you? You’ll ruin everything.”

“…You can always be stupid, but you can’t always be smart. You know that, right? Right now, I feel like an idiot.”

Just like back in their school days.

“I don’t want this. I’m not in the mood, and I don’t think I can handle you right now. Let me go.”

“Then leave. Just open my hand, walk out that door, and go. You’re smart, aren’t you?”

Seriel bit her lip.

A violent storm of emotions raged within her, but in the end, she lowered her head.

‘I don’t know anymore.’

She turned and kissed him.

Fermi pulled her closer, and they tumbled to the floor.

Their embrace was brief, but as they lay side by side, their faces were flushed.

Seriel caught her breath.

“You crazy… bastard.”

“Did you like it that much?”

She shot up and glared at him.

“This isn’t a joke! Do you think we’re still at the age where we can afford these kinds of mistakes?! This kind of reckless—”

“It wasn’t reckless.”

Fermi cut in.

“Not now, and not back then either.”

Seriel’s tense expression softened at the sincerity in his voice.

“Then tell me. What are you thinking? What do you really want?”

“Can’t you tell me?”

She reached for his hand.

“I’ll help you. I’ll never betray you.”

“I…”

But the moment he opened his mouth, a chilling reality pierced through his thoughts.

‘No.’

He would lose control.

“It’s time to go.”

Seriel stood and straightened her clothes.

“That doesn’t mean I’m giving up. I’ll see you later. And I will get you to tell me.”

‘Yeah, that’s the kind of woman you are.’

Someone who respected others’ emotions as much as her own.

Maybe humans weren’t just animals chasing desire, Fermi thought.

“Seriel.”

She turned back, and he said,

“The reason Amy failed the graduation exam… was because she wasn’t even at the level where you needed to be wary of her.”

“Hah.”

Seriel flipped him off but smiled as she left the room.

Now alone again, Fermi stared at the ceiling, lost in thought.

‘Mother…’

Yolga, the woman with the widest vision.

‘How did you endure it? Seeing what no one else could… how did you hold on?’

A path no one acknowledged.

“Let’s begin.”

Fermi stood abruptly, his eyes gleaming with purpose.

“Because I am Yolga’s son.”
