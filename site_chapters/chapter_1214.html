Chapter 1214:

Even as the minister of Kesia reported on state affairs, Fermi remained trapped within the solitude of his own mind.

“…So, after consultation between the departments, we sent the official document. What was it—oh, the International Tribunal.”

The ministers, their minds hazy from the effects of Angel, were unhurried despite the pressing urgency of every second.

“Handling that was a real pain. With the former king having passed, there’s a mountain of things to take care of.”

Fermi’s eyes narrowed.

‘Just how much of the drug did they take?’

When used properly, Angel could reduce pain to a manageable level for daily life.

Of course, it didn’t make it disappear entirely, so the discomfort was still intense—but…

‘Why do they keep ignoring the dosage?’

The fact that such weak-willed people were sitting in ministerial positions was exasperating.

Sigh.

Fermi masked his emotions.

“Thank you for your efforts. What about Lanstein’s whereabouts? We need information within the next six hours.”

There was no reply.

When Fermi raised his gaze, the ministers only returned it with indifferent expressions, remaining silent.

“Is there a problem?”

“Well, we haven’t had a single day of rest since the Holy War began. Everyone is exhausted.”

Exhausted?

‘You’ve got to be kidding me.’

Sure, human ability has its limits. But weren’t these people supposed to be the heads of their respective departments?

The chief secretary added, “Your Majesty, the workload is too much. Everyone’s worn out. At this rate, they won’t hold out much longer.”

‘Right.’

That’s what defeat looks like.

If they’d at least admitted it and swallowed their resentment, Fermi wouldn’t be this furious.

‘So what are they expecting?’

Wanting problems to be solved without backing it with actual work—wasn’t that a form of violence?

Because they’re hoping someone else will do it.

‘They’re using the fact that we’re “in this together” as leverage to guilt me. “If you don’t want us to sink, you have to sacrifice more.”’

The contradiction of an organization where the ones who work the hardest suffer the most.

‘Should I fire them?’

A single minister could be replaced. But Fermi also had to calculate the overall efficiency of the organization.

Above all, time was running short.

‘It’d be satisfying—like gaining a thousand more years of life—to beat them senseless and toss them out the door…’

But that wouldn’t solve the problem. So Fermi put on his usual polite smile.

“You’re right. I’ve been too demanding. I know how hard each of you is working for the kingdom.”

He wasn’t the least bit moved.

Knowing full well they didn’t actually believe those words made Fermi sick to his stomach.

Hold it in.

He had known since before he could even read that human beings were inherently self-centered.

‘It’s fine. I’ll do it. There’s no need to blame anyone. I’m doing this because it’s what I want to do.’

It’s not about whether something is possible or not—if it must be done, you find a way.

‘That’s what winning is.’

Fermi revised his plan.

‘I’ll have to leave finding Guitar Man to the field staff. The diplomatic connections of each department are crucial. I can handle the other tasks myself. Of the six assignments, I’ll prioritize the most critical…’

It would take 23 minutes.

‘No good. Cut it down to 18. Review the foreign affairs records of each nation and cross-reference them with the records from Omega 999…’

Suddenly, Fermi’s head began to spin.

‘How…’

How can they say they’re tired so easily?

‘Winning is supposed to be hard. Even a kid knows that. You’re fighting other people who have the exact same amount of time as you. And you’re telling me that you…’

Fermi clenched his fist tightly.

Stay calm. If you let emotion control you, you’ll ruin everything. You can’t sink now.

The former king, Manolka, once described Fermi with a single word:

Capable.

A man who could resist being swayed by emotion and endure anything until he achieved his goal.

“Let’s skip…”

Fermi began to speak slowly.

“Any non-essential tasks. They’re not particularly important. Please focus all your efforts on finding Lanstein. You may use your remaining time however you see fit. Thank you.”

“Understood.”

The ministers left the room with flat expressions, having gained nothing more.

Left alone, Fermi bowed his head.

“Ghhh…!”

His emotional sickness was worsening.

‘Damn it.’

He couldn’t take Angel—he needed his brain functioning at 100%.

Technically, Shirone’s Agape could suppress the symptoms, but that wasn’t an option for Fermi.

‘I can’t get entangled. Not even slightly.’

He had to guide the world with 0.1% lies and 99.9% truth.

As a desperate measure, Fermi clenched his fourth finger tightly and twisted his body.

Crack. The bone snapped, and pain shot through his brain—but it would keep him stable for a while.

“Hoo…”

Unable even to consider getting treated, Fermi slumped forward onto his desk.

Mother.

How did she do it?

While everyone only looked out for themselves, what allowed her to keep fighting that lonely battle?

The Law of Large Numbers.

She had a perspective that transcended ordinary people.

‘I was being impatient. My narrow view caused my fear… and made me small-minded.’

While it’s nearly impossible to predict the next action of a single human being—

‘The more data you have…’

The more accurate the statistics become, and the population can be modeled in predictable patterns.

‘She was a prophet.’

The sample contained in Yolga’s wide-area vision encompassed almost all of humanity. Her judgment surpassed prediction and entered the realm of prophecy.

‘She even knew about her own death. She must’ve seen the end of human history already.’

That’s why she had left her son’s side.

As his emotions settled, Fermi slowly stood and healed his broken finger.

Wrapping a bandage around it, he checked the watch on his wrist.

Fermi’s gaze turned cold.

“…It’s Havitz Time.”

“Kekeke! Kee!”

Havitz laughed like a madman.

“You have no idea who I’m going to kill, do you? Hm? You’ll never know. Because I don’t know either!”

His unusual excitement stemmed from a kind of rage.

After failing to assassinate the King of Corona, he killed a maid as a desperate fallback.

It wasn’t against the rules—but the shame of making the game less fun weighed heavily on him.

‘What’s done is done.’

Havitz didn’t dwell on the past. In fact, he was thinking of using it to his advantage.

“Who is it? Who’s going to die?”

The tension of not even knowing himself grew stronger as the appointed time approached.

And finally—

‘Found you!’

In the kitchen of the Delta Main Hall, Havitz spotted a man loading supplies.

Elikia activated.

“Time to—ugh!”

The man’s Vanishing spell shattered, and he felt his own neck separate from his body.

“Haa… haa…”

Now what are you going to do?

‘Wizard.’

Just as her position was detected through Elikia, Wizard appeared—causing Havitz to lick his lips.

“Come on. This time, I’ll…”

But in that very moment, Havitz realized Wizard wasn’t moving—Elikia’s signal confirmed it.

His face twisted.

“Grrr…”

Shirone was standing in front of Wizard.

As Elikia’s light enveloped the Delta Main Hall—

‘One… two… three…’

The high-ranking officials of every nation froze in place, unable to breathe for ten seconds.

‘He’s dead.’

The man in the kitchen had been beheaded.

“Phew… okay, it’s done. For the next hour, at least.”

Some sighed in relief.

“Damn it. How long do we have to keep living like this? I’m going to die of a heart attack before anyone kills me.”

While others grumbled—

“Brother.”

Wizard spoke to Shirone.

“No… Master.”

Shirone said nothing.

While Tormia was preparing for the Summit of Kings as Code One, Shirone had come to find Wizard.

‘We had to meet at least once.’

But now, standing before her, he didn’t know what to say.

‘First off…’

She’d gotten stronger.

She’d already been complete after finishing her training, but now there was a look in her eyes that said she’d broken through another wall.

Even so, the moment those fierce eyes landed on Shirone’s face, they softened.

“…I’m sorry.”

She had failed to kill Havitz.

Though the fight still wasn’t over, Shirone had surely hoped for a different outcome.

“I’ll take full responsibility—”

“Wizard.”

Shirone interrupted her. Wizard flinched like she had been scolded.

“I’m sorry.”

She was already a child who transcended logic, so the only thing he could offer her was sincerity.

“I got your hands dirty. Because of me.”

It had to happen once—but how he had wished that once could also be the last time in her life.

“I dragged you into Hell. So let’s stop now. Leave the battlefield.”

Wizard’s heart clenched.

‘He’s disappointed in me. If only I’d been a little stronger…’

Still, she couldn’t go against his will.

‘So it ends here…’

But just as she was starting to feel a strange lightness at the thought of finally being free from Havitz—

‘No.’

Her footsteps stopped cold.

‘This is a dangerous gamble. I can’t stop now.’

God’s frequency.

The moment she unlocked the lock on her heart, Havitz would know everything.

‘I can’t escape.’

Not until the truth was revealed.

Standing still at the scene of his own murder, Havitz focused entirely on his senses.

‘Blocked.’

Just as he was about to sense something through God’s frequency, Wizard had shut her heart.

‘You said you loved me once.’

If her heart was truly with Shirone, then Havitz would automatically lose the game.

‘No, even a trace of affection would tip my heart toward a lie.’

If that happened—

Then even Havitz himself wouldn’t be able to predict what chaos might emerge.

Wizard spoke.

“My mission isn’t over yet. Please, give me one last chance.”

Shirone guessed why she had changed her tone, but he firmly shook his head.

“I’m not blaming you. But I can’t let you play this kind of game with Satan. Let me handle it from here.”

“This isn’t something you should take responsibility for, Master. Since the moment I failed the assassination—this has been my fight.”

Shirone looked at her with sorrow in his eyes.

“I know why you’re doing this. But from now on—”

Just as he reached to grab her wrist, the avatar of the Void God shimmered into form and swayed heavily.

“…First Form.”

There was no helping it.

If she stayed any longer, she felt she really would open the door to her heart.

‘Hundredfold Strength!’

Wizard channeled all her might and delivered her strongest blow to Shirone.

BOOM!

One frame vanished. The air expanded. The walls of the building exploded outward.

“Haa… haa…”

Wizard fixed her eyes on the space ahead.

‘I’m sorry, Master.’

Then, as the dust cleared, Shirone reappeared, wrapped in a haze of light.

“…Yes, you really are strong.”

Wizard blinked, stunned. Shirone gave a bitter smile, not a single scratch on him.

“But… who do you think taught you that?”

“Huh…?”

If divine embodiment was the expression of one’s ego, then Shirone was her superego.

“Honestly, if I didn’t know, I might’ve taken the hit. But I know you well. So…”

Wizard’s heart pounded fast.

‘I’m so happy.’

Just the fact that he blocked her attack lightened the cosmic burden she carried by half.

‘Someone I can always lean on… my only adult.’

“So…”

After thinking for a moment, Shirone said,

“This time, I’ll respect your decision. But make sure you win. Yahweh’s disciple can’t lose to Satan, right?”

Wizard clenched her fists.

‘I’m ready!’

She had proven herself.

Meanwhile, Shirone watched her joyous expression with a heavy heart.

‘A seven-year-old surpassing her limits…’

He had blocked her strike, yes—but it was a technique no one else could possibly imitate.

He gave her one last piece of advice.

“You can do it. Whatever it is. Right?”

Wizard repeated his words in her mind, then curled one side of her lips into a cold smile.

“Yes.”

It was perfection.
