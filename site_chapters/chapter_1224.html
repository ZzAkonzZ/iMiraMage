Chapter 1224:

What it means to be strong.

Perhaps human intellect, and even the systems we’ve built, are not born from strength—but from weakness.

In front of Ymir…

“Heh heh heh.”

The moment Ymir’s fist slammed into the ground, the earth scattered like shamed dust.

“Is this what they mean when humans find flies annoying?”

Tons of dirt exploded from the edge of the crater, and Kangnan emerged.

“Ugh, damn it…”

She spat out the dirt in her mouth, trying not to think about the fact that all of this was Gaian flesh.

“Thousand-Armed Avalokiteśvara.”

Miro moved her arms, and the avatar of Thousand-Armed Avalokiteśvara filled the sky, launching a flurry of strikes.

As massive palms slammed the ground, Ymir’s joints buckled downward.

“That’s all?”

It was merely an impact—knocking Ymir down was a completely different matter.

“You call this breaking into my world?”

As Ymir flexed his arms, Deep Layer 1 began to tremble with frightening intensity.

Miro clenched her teeth in horror.

‘What the hell is this thing?’

If every aspect of power could be quantified, then Ymir had pushed them all to the absolute limit.

“True Fist.”

The moment Ymir threw his punch, his fist was already right in front of her.

‘Thousand-Armed Avalokiteśvara.’

Time slowed. With each sliver of split time, residual images of arms extended in waves.

And every one of those afterimages—

‘Ten Thousand Palms.’

—became real impacts, a manifestation technique that struck with crushing force.

“Ghhrrraaah!”

The blow was nearly seismic, bending Ymir’s back under the barrage.

‘Is this what pain is?’

He had so rarely felt it that he didn’t even have a proper definition for it.

‘I thought it was supposed to feel unpleasant.’

Ymir grinned.

“Why do people hate something that feels this good?”

‘You bastard…’

Miro’s eyes blazed.

‘You need to understand it too.’

Even one ten-thousandth of the pain Gaold felt.

As Miro moved her arms, the avatar followed in perfect sync.

‘Here it comes again.’

Ymir realized.

‘This ability can’t be dodged.’

From the far reaches of the universe, Thousand-Armed Avalokiteśvara sees all. There are no blind spots.

Ymir scoffed.

“Tch.”

He threw a punch straight into the center of the next storm of attacks.

The moment his fist struck the overlapping impact zone—

Miro’s mind went hazy.

‘What…?’

The scenery twisted, and suddenly she saw children with identical faces laughing madly, and thousands of dogs wagging their tails in unison.

‘Mental disarray.’

This was a trademark of Ymir’s attacks—he didn’t just destroy the physical.

‘No!’

Miro, grounded in the realm of Extreme Good, brought her consciousness back into focus—and Ymir’s massive form filled her vision.

“Die.”

Time slowed again, but this time, even Miro wasn’t sure she could keep up.

‘Damn it!’

Just as the world moved in slow motion—

Boom!

Something flew in at immense speed and struck Ymir’s temple.

The giant’s head jerked violently to the side, and he stopped charging.

As Miro’s team snapped out of the mental chaos, they blinked in disbelief.

Blood streamed from Ymir’s temple.

“…Ugh.”

Displeasure. Clear, unmistakable pain.

Ymir turned to find Gaold standing there, air gun raised.

“It was you?”

The one who had opened Deep Layer 1.

Gaold gave Miro a quick glance, then started walking forward.

“What, stings a bit?”

At Gaold’s remark, Ymir’s expression shifted back to amused.

“Heh, you tore a little skin. I’ll admit it—pretty good, for a human.”

Sein grimaced.

‘Ymir’s right. The situation’s still hopeless.’

All they’d done was deliver a little pain.

True battle is about destruction—getting excited over drawing some blood was childish.

“Is that so?”

Gaold charged in again, and Ymir, smiling with anticipation, got ready to meet him.

“Wait! We need to attack together—!”

Before Miro could finish her sentence, they clashed—and another wave of mental disruption began.

‘This is so annoying.’

Meanwhile, Ymir was intrigued. Despite the magic’s power, Gaold wasn’t weakening.

“Is it mental strength?”

“No.”

Through excruciating pain, madness gleamed in Gaold’s eyes.

“I was crazy from the start.”

His air gun exploded like a shotgun blast, and Ymir’s expression began to twist.

‘This guy…’

If pain beyond the limit was the only way to break boundaries—then Gaold had shattered more of them than anyone else.

“Air Press.”

The agony he summoned matched the pain of all humanity.

And yet, he remained at the center of the deepest extremes of moral good and evil.

“GRAAAAAHH!”

Unable to endure it, Ymir let out a monstrous scream.

The sound of something snapping echoed sharply.

The earth collapsed into a cylindrical crater, and an enormous explosion tore through the ground.

Had this been reality, it would have rivaled a natural disaster.

“Ugh!”

Miro’s group was blasted away by the gust, coughing as they returned and brushed off the dust.

They stood frozen, mouths agape, unable to believe what they saw.

“T-that’s…”

Ymir’s right arm was broken.

“How’s that?”

Gaold asked.

“Hurts now, doesn’t it?”

Ymir looked at his grotesquely twisted arm.

It hurt.

Not just discomfort—true, undeniable pain.

‘It’s similar to the sensation Ogent gave me. No… it might be even stronger.’

As the realization hit, Ymir’s demeanor shifted. He smiled at Gaold.

“I see.”

His eyes turned pitch black, with a single red dot like a grain of rice in each.

“This is what it means to be alive.”

It was so alien, so terrifying, that it felt impossible they lived in the same universe.

“Hnnnngh…”

Kangnan couldn’t hold back her groan.

‘I’m scared.’

Nothing in the world could compare to this terror—but Gaold remained calm.

“Good. Glad you feel alive.”

A philosopher who had reached the deepest depths of human nature feared nothing.

“You have to be alive… to be killed.”

“Heh.”

Ymir chuckled.

His body vanished. Gaold twisted his torso violently.

In that instant, Sein realized—

‘It’s coming.’

A clash beyond all reason.

There was no sound, no vision, no shock—just the sensation of pure flame, of raw destruction.

A blow that transcended the senses brought the dream's timeline frighteningly close to reality.

And within that overwhelming flood of data—Shirone's body, deep in transcendent dreaming, trembled.

Another massive piece of information had just arrived.

The most intense event in the life of the first Yora, Yorahan, was being transmitted.

“Haha! That’s a good point too.”

Married to Armand, Yorahan went out to the village square each day and preached universal love to the Fireborn.

“If someone hits me, should I hit them back? It’s a hard question, I know. But here’s what I think. If I retaliate, then violence gains legitimacy. But if I forgive them, that person may go on to forgive someone else—and eventually, we’ll…”

The Fireborn shouted together:

“A world full of love will come!”

“Exactly. Of course, it’s not easy. But if we can’t reach that place, then far in the future, life might cease to exist on this land.”

Yorahan’s life may have been short, but the ideals he planted would pass through eternity.

We have to decide now—where our journey ends.

“Teacher! I have a question!”

“Sure, ask anything.”

“Today’s lesson was about love, right? Then… how do you love Armand?”

“Class dismissed.”

Yorahan closed the book and stood up, and the Fireborn students began to pout.

“Aww! Please tell us, Teacher!”

He practically fled home as their voices echoed behind him.

“Sheesh. What am I going to do with them…”

Inside their hut, Armand sat on the floor, sharpening her sword on a flat whetstone.

“What’s wrong? Did the kids cause trouble again?”

“Haha! No, nothing like that. Hey, why the sword all of a sudden? Are you going hunting?”

“Yeah. We’ve run out of birds. I might need to change hunting grounds.”

In just two years, human territory had expanded enough to reach into Fireborn lands.

“I’ll go with you.”

“Not today. It’s too late. I’ll be back in an hour if I go alone.”

Even if Yorahan had grown used to hiking, keeping up with the strongest Fireborn warrior was impossible.

Yorahan grumbled.

“I just worry about you. Even the Blue River region’s been cleared now. I heard it’s the Mikhas Kingdom.”

“What kind of place is that?”

“I only know rumors. It used to be a provincial territory, but apparently it grew really fast.”

Such rapid expansion usually meant the leader was aggressive.

“We may have to relocate. I’ll look for a suitable place tomorrow.”

Armand suddenly froze mid-sharpening.

“…Honestly, I don’t know.”

“I know how strong you are. I just mean—even if the odds are low…”

“No, I mean your philosophy. This idea of universal love… do you really think it’s possible?”

She turned to him.

“Look at me. I’m about to kill birds just to eat. Isn’t that still violence?”

Yorahan slumped into his chair.

“…Yeah. I guess it is.”

In reality, this might not seem like a dilemma—but for a philosophy meant to last forever, contradiction was unacceptable.

Universal love. A world where everyone cares for one another will always be blocked by the issues of consumption and reproduction.

If he couldn’t solve this, the philosophy of universal love would eventually collapse.

“What about going out into the human world?”

Yorahan’s ears twitched.

“Huh? What did you say?”

“No one here challenges you. But if you put your beliefs into practice outside, maybe you’ll find a better way.”

Of course, it was the obvious next step. Yorahan had never planned to stay with the Fireborn for long.

But now it’s different. They’re all my family.

More than anything, he was worried about Armand.

“No. I won’t leave. It’s enough here. If I just research a little more, I’m sure I’ll find the answer…”

“Yorahan.”

Armand approached.

“I never trusted humans. I never inherited the will of our predecessors. But meeting you changed me. If every human could become like you… then I could find the courage too.”

“Armand…”

“Let’s go together. We’re one, aren’t we? If it’s what you want, of course I’ll go too.”

She smiled gently.

“Besides, I’m not like the other Fireborn. I’m not passive. I can eat food. I can live like a human.”

“H-Honey…”

Yorahan’s eyes welled with tears.

“Thank you. Really… thank you. I love you.”

“…Big crybaby.”

At that moment—

Even while receiving the transcendent dream, Shirone’s unconscious mind posed a question.

Consumption and reproduction.

Despite the passage of countless years, the biological paradox still remained unresolved.

But he was the first Yora.

Standing before the greatest challenge of life—consumption and reproduction—

Could Yorahan truly define a love with no trace of evil? A philosophy of absolute compassion?
