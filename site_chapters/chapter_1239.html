Chapter 1239:

“Honored guests from around the world, we have achieved a grand unification in the face of a global crisis. All agreements have been delivered in written form, and by tomorrow morning, for the first time in history, the election of a unified World Government...”

As the closing ceremony commenced, the performers gathered in the waiting room, steadying their breath one last time.

“Whew. Whew.”

Kayden handed a bottle of water to Maya.

“Here, take this.”

Her throat was parched enough to finish a whole bottle, but she only rinsed her mouth.

“There’s no need to be nervous. You’re the best, after all.”

Maya probably wouldn’t even remember not replying.

Seeing her unfamiliar cold expression, Kayden swallowed hard and moved to a corner of the room.

El Kiana shot him a disapproving glare.

‘Seriously… can’t he see she’s trying to focus? The moment a professional gets nervous, it’s over, you idiot.’ And the fact that she was having these thoughts was, in itself, proof that she was nervous.

‘No, focus. Focus!’

The room quieted again. Only the sound of the conductor Panier flipping through sheet music filled the space.

‘Annoying guy.’

If Kayden had been just a bit more clueless, he would've been kicked out of the waiting room.

‘Let nothing happen.’

Performers are slaves to superstition— even a slight feeling can make the world seem like it's collapsing.

Perhaps, in that moment, their hypersensitivity could be intense enough to kill someone.

‘Thirty minutes left.’

If she didn’t know how to control it, she wouldn’t have made it this far.

‘Let nothing happen. Stay exactly like this. Pour everything out and leave the stage empty.’

Afterward, whether she got drunk out of her mind or was taken to the ER—it didn’t matter.

Then, a knock came at the door.

‘No...!’

It was a jinx.

Panier suppressed the urge to scream and responded with a cold expression.

“Who is it?”

A man with cold eyes and a sword at his hip opened the door. Reina stood up from her seat.

“Rai.”

“Can we talk… just for a moment?”

She looked at the conductor, who nodded with a blank face. Getting angry would only worsen the jinx.

“Go ahead.”

Reina, just as tense, let out a low voice the moment they stepped into the hallway.

“Are you crazy? Do you know where we are? Even if you’re with the royal guard, you shouldn’t be allowed here.”

“Grandfather told me. I can’t go into detail, but it’s dangerous. You need to come with me.”

“Grandfather?”

Currently, Clump was the commanding officer of the Tormia garrison stationed at Delta.

“What are you talking about? There’s less than 30 minutes until the performance.”

“You idiot, everyone’s going to die. I think even Grandpa pretended not to see me because of it. Let’s go now.”

“I can’t. I’m not going.”

What pianist walks off less than 30 minutes before a performance?

“If I’m not there, over thirty people will lose their minds. I can’t do that to them.”

“Then you’ll die...!”

Rai clenched his jaw.

Being family, he knew—her stubbornness matched his own.

“Do what you want.”

“Go back to headquarters. Don’t stay behind because of me.”

There was no response.

Reina’s heart grew heavy, knowing her younger brother would stay behind.

‘A kid whose whole world is his family.’

He may seem cold, but he was always the most tenderhearted of the three siblings.

The eldest was the eldest, the second daughter was the second, the youngest was the youngest—each received attention in their own way.

‘Maybe he always felt a little distant. From me, from Guy, from Father and Grandfather.’

Perhaps Rai’s obsession with the sword was his way of proving something to his family.

‘You can go your own way now. No matter what kind of person you become, we’ll always be right here.’

Reina exhaled deeply.

“Whew.”

Having fought off countless jinxes, her trained mind returned to focus.

“Let’s do this. The performance.”

That’s all a performer can do.

Havitz asked:

“You’re saying I can’t kill you? Why?”

“Because that’s the law. Every being can kill me. But I can also kill them. In other words, we share the same law.”

Shirone said,

“But Satan can always escape Yahweh, no matter the situation. Have you ever thought about what kind of law that must be? What law lets you escape me in every possible scenario?”

“I don’t know. What is it?”

Havitz defines nothing.

“It’s because you die.”

“Satan exists outside the law. That gives you a statistical advantage in most cases—but not with Yahweh. Your survival rate is 0%. Because your death is absolute, you can escape me with 100% certainty. In short, you and I are the extremes of law.”

“No, wait. I just swung a sword at you earlier—”

“To summarize,”

Shirone interrupted.

“You can escape me anytime you want. But precisely because of that, the moment you try to kill me, in any situation, you will die.”

That was why Havitz’s instincts had screamed.

“I tried to stab you, though?”

“That’s just the outcome. I don’t know what would’ve happened—but if you had stabbed me in the back...”

After a brief pause, Shirone said,

“You would’ve died. One way or another, I would’ve found a way to kill you.”

A thought crossed his mind.

‘Maybe Satan... corrupts other humans because he can’t kill Yahweh, who has attained enlightenment.’

“Haha.”

Havitz let out a dry laugh, then returned to his eerie expression.

“Hell Prison.”

The twelve fanatics surrounded Shirone once again as Havitz used Vanishing to reposition.

‘He’s spewing nonsense.’

The twelve hidden codes of Hell Prison began distorting Shirone’s mind again.

“Yahweh,”

If you are strong, defeat us. Prove your power. Rise to the top of the world. Make everyone follow you.

“Whew.”

Shirone was not indifferent.

In fact, having reached enlightenment, he suppressed all desire—which made him all the more unstable.

“Stand down.”

But he would not break.

That’s why he was Yahweh.

“Graaah!”

Frustrated that the hidden code didn’t work, Hell Prison prepared to attack—

“Ugh!”

—but once again, Havitz, having dropped Vanishing, tumbled past Shirone into the mud.

“S-Satan has…”

Even Hell Prison had to admit it this time.

‘Is it true?’

Satan cannot kill Yahweh.

“Hmm.”

Sitting in the rain, Havitz looked down at his trembling legs.

Fear?

No. That couldn’t be it.

“What’s going on?”

Just as Havitz raised his sword to cut off his legs, Shirone approached and said:

“It’s no use.”

“Hold on. Let me fix this first and then I’ll fight you again. Why does my body keep…?”

“Havitz, do you believe in God?”

Havitz froze mid-motion and turned his head.

“God?”

“Not the kind of god we define, but a true god—one that rules over all dimensions. Honestly, I have my doubts.”

“If we’re talking theology…”

“But still,”

Shirone continued.

“I kind of wish there was a god.”

And then he kicked Havitz in the face, sending him sprawling on the ground with a splash.

“Yahweh!”

Hell Prison bared its teeth, but Havitz lay still, quietly soaking in the rain.

“If a god really existed—if everything were fair and equal, if no one got hurt, if lightning struck you when you did something evil…”

Gritting his teeth, Shirone asked:

“Why are we the only ones who suffer? Why does no one take responsibility? Is it because there’s no god? Because lightning doesn’t fall from the sky? Because there’s no blade hidden somewhere that strikes you down when you cross a line? Does the world seem like a joke to you? Because someone is enduring, sacrificing, being considerate—you think it’s okay to do whatever you want?”

“Hm.”

Havitz thought carefully.

“I think I get what you mean. Like when I throw trash on the ground, someone else eventually picks it up and throws it in the bin. If no one cleaned it, the streets would get filthy, and over time, everyone would be living in a garbage dump.”

“Exactly.”

“But the person who cleans it up ends up feeling resentful. ‘Why am I always the one cleaning up?’ they wonder. ‘Why do I have to make things nice while others just throw their filth everywhere?’ And then, at some point, they start to think…”

Havitz suddenly sat up straight.

“If nobody threw trash in the first place, the streets would stay clean. No one would have to sacrifice. Everyone could be happy.”

Shirone responded sincerely.

“...Right.”

“I don’t really get it.”

“What?”

“Of course people throw trash—because someone’s going to clean it. Even if all 100 people throw garbage, someone will step up and clean it. The one person who really doesn’t want to live in a dump will end up taking on the burden of the other 99.”

Shirone was left speechless.

“Sure, that one person might feel bitter. They cleaned everything, but the clean streets are enjoyed equally by all 100. But no one forced them to do it, right? If they don’t like it, they don’t have to clean. Then someone else will take their place.”

Havitz pointed at Shirone.

“Someone like you.”

Heavy breathing mixed with the sound of rain.

“Everyone kept passing it off… and in the end, you took it on. But that’s just how it is. You had to do it. So you’ll keep doing it.”

He added, as if tired of the whole conversation:

“Go clean up everyone’s trash for the rest of your life, you idiot.”

“…Photon Cannon.”

Shirone grabbed Havitz by the collar and yanked him up, then swung his left fist.

‘Infinity!’

As Havitz’s jaw twisted, a blinding flash of light exploded inside his brain.

“Aaaaaargh!”

A splitting headache followed, and he clutched his head, screaming.

“Hell Prison!”

“Protect Satan!”

Hell Prison activated 0.666 seconds of time that didn’t exist in the world and pulled Havitz out of the law’s reach.

“…Faster than light.”

At Shirone’s words, Hell Prison’s eyes widened.

“H-How?”

The reason light speed is constant is because it’s the speed of the signals that construct the world.

Since nothing can exist before the signal arrives, light becomes a barrier.

“But you can reach unexisting time.”

Faster-than-light transmission pierces the zero-information space between signals—true vacuum.

“Photon Cannon.”

This was the secret behind Goffin’s trademark, and the lost frame of Wizard.

“Infinity.”

When this energy is applied to matter—

“AAAAAARGH!”

—It becomes an atomic-destruction spell that fires particles at 99.9999% the speed of light.

Where Hell Prison had screamed and vanished, only Havitz remained, trembling.

“Does it hurt?”

He wasn’t in any shape to respond.

“Pain becomes a tragedy because it’s lonely. In the celebration called life, you’re the one falling into Hell alone. No one can bear it for you. It’s the one thing you have to take full responsibility for.”

Havitz charged.

“Kiieeeek!”

He tried to channel all his rage into his longsword and slash Shirone’s neck—

“You made a mistake.”

Shirone calmly pressed his hand against Havitz’s chest.

“GUUAAAAAAAH!”

The atomic destruction magic tore through his body again, making him howl and stagger.

“Raaagh! Raaaaagh!”

As Shirone approached, Havitz’s legs instinctively stepped back.

R-Run. I have to run… huh?

That’s when it hit him.

Havitz turned to Shirone with a shocked expression.

This… is fear.

The one and only definition born from chaos.
