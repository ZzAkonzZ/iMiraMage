Chapter 750: Holy War (1)

The Kingdom of Tormia.

Heading south from the capital, Bashka, one arrives at the region of Mafal, known for its mines.

Though the area is responsible for 45% of the kingdom’s iron ore production, it remains relatively underdeveloped.

In other words, it suffered the most from the demons’ attacks, and hundreds of war orphans in Mafal were currently struggling with severe starvation.

“Here, over here.”

There was a small shack where miners once lived and worked, now abandoned due to the collapse of the tunnels.

When seven children from the Dorkin Orphanage opened the door, they found a disheveled woman with tangled hair and a gaunt face waiting inside.

“You came.”

She was now a drifter with nowhere to go, but she had once held a high-ranking position as a Minister of Spectrum in Radum.

She was Flarino of the Flower Clan.

The ancient weapon, the Living Flower, had broken, sending a powerful shock through her mind. Yet she had survived.

Her position as Radum's leader made her an enemy of the kingdom, and this abandoned mine in Mafal was where she had gone into hiding.

“Hi there. You must be hungry. Here, eat this.”

The children handed her leftover food wrapped in a cloth.

As Flarino hungrily stuffed the food into her stomach, she noticed the children’s gazes and lowered her head.

“Thank you... for always taking care of me.”

Her extreme passivity made even children hesitant to mistreat her—and to them, that was curious.

“It’s okay. Even the puppies at our orphanage eat this stuff.”

A mere illusion—that if she was beautiful and obedient to her master’s words, they would in turn risk their lives to protect her.

That illusion, one only flowers could believe in, was the greatest failure of the Flower Clan.

“So, what should we do now?”

“Let’s play tag! Flarino, you’re it. You have to find us!”

Still, children were innocent. And because of that, Flarino had found this place bearable.

“Alright. I’ll come find you.”

Just as the children grabbed her hands and were about to leave the shack, the door opened, and a boy entered—one head taller than his peers.

“Hey, you guys!”

The orphanage only accepted children up to age twelve, but his face looked at least sixteen.

Lying about one’s age was nothing new, and Director Dorkin needed someone to keep the other children in line.

“Ugh! Wh-What is it?”

The children didn’t know why he suddenly scowled like a goblin, especially since he’d always ignored what went on in the shack.

“Come with me. The director wants to see that woman.”

Dorkin Orphanage.

Established recently after a temple was opened, the orphanage was run by a man over sixty named Dorkin.

“Children are truly dreadful.”

He’d been through all sorts of trials and tribulations. The only reason he set up the orphanage was for the kingdom’s subsidies.

“Still, it’s quite the profitable venture.”

Success often came from crises.

“Director, I brought the woman.”

According to rumors, she was mentally slow and would do anything the children told her to.

Dorkin had dismissed it, thinking the kids were exaggerating—but a report from a sixteen-year-old couldn’t be ignored.

“Tell her to come in.”

He spoke kindly, and the door opened. Flarino stepped in, looking awkward and unsure.

“Oh?”

Dorkin’s eyes immediately changed.

If she’d just been a simple fool, he’d have made her do chores. But a beauty like this—even among nobles—was rare.

“Was she a prostitute who lost her mind?”

He stared at Flarino, who stood trembling in fear with her eyes downcast, and got up from his seat.

“So, I hear you’ve been stealing our food?”

Strictly speaking, it had been dog food.

“Yes. Thank you for your generosity.”

As she responded submissively, Dorkin dropped the pretense and raised his hand.

“Then I assume you know what you’re expected to do.”

“……”

Flarino had no choice but to accept her new master. But just then, noise erupted outside.

“Wh-Who are you, mister?!”

The moment she saw the man burst in through the door, Flarino’s shoulders began to shake violently.

The trauma from when the Living Flower was broken came rushing back.

“H-How are you here?”

It was Lupist, head of the Tormia Magic Association.

“How am I here? I’ve known exactly where you were and what you were doing this whole time.”

He had simply been lying low to avoid attention from the Ivory Tower.

“Where’s the seed of the Living Flower? Have you already planted it?”

To Flarino, Lupist was a terrifying predator. As she backed away, Dorkin stepped forward.

“Hey, who the hell are you? Her sugar daddy or something?”

Given Lupist’s scarred face and the conversation he overheard, it was an easy misunderstanding to make.

“Shut up. I’m taking her with me.”

Dorkin, once a street tough, wasn’t about to hand her over so easily.

“Listen to this punk talk! Do you even know where you are? This is a kingdom-certified orphanage funded directly by the royal capital—!”

Before he could finish, Lupist backhanded him. With a loud thud, Dorkin’s head smashed against the wall.

“Aaaugh…!”

Lupist ignored the trembling old man and strode toward Flarino.

“You’ve… come to kill me, haven’t you?”

“Kill you? Why would I kill someone as talented as you?”

To ensure the success of the Yongroe Project, the more members of the Flower Clan, the better.

“Just hand over the seed. If you follow orders, I’ll guarantee your safety as head of the Magic Association.”

Dorkin, forgetting his pain, turned his head in shock.

“M-Magic Association…?”

It was already unbelievable that the head of the Magic Association would come to such a rundown village—but to think it was because of her?

“Who the hell is that woman?”

Flarino trembled and muttered,

“You’re just going to break it again anyway…”

To her, Lupist was terror incarnate.

“I’m going to gather the Flower Clan. I’ll provide you a village where you can live. And there’s something I need you to do.”

“Something to do…?”

She assumed the price of such a generous offer would be cruel. But Lupist said something unexpected:

“We’re going to build a flower field.”

Of course, the things planted there would not be real flowers—but ancient weapons made of steel.

“……”

“Think about it. It’s an alliance. Probably your only chance—maybe your last—to ensure your clan’s survival.”

Though she didn’t answer, Lupist read her eyes and turned.

“Come. We’re heading to the capital.”

As the two approached the door, Dorkin scrambled to his feet.

“W-Wait… Association Head, sir!”

If the higher-ups heard about today, the orphanage would be shut down, and he’d be sent to prison.

Lupist stopped at the door.

“These are sensitive times. I don’t know what kind of trash you are, but don’t try to act big.”

Dorkin quickly understood his meaning.

“Yes! I’m sorry! This will never happen again!”

To say he wasn’t disgusted would be a lie, but Lupist said no more and walked out.

“Every organization lacks people. Whether they’re trash or criminals—this isn’t the time to be picky.”

According to Lupist’s philosophy of systems, people were like machines that only worked when a coin was inserted.

Even someone like Dorkin would provide labor for society once money was involved.

“Orphans are future resources. It’s best to have at least minimal controls in place.”

Even if the system was corrupted, once money had been invested, it couldn’t be destroyed carelessly. That was the reality of the kingdom.

“He’ll never understand that.”

Maybe it was because the world was rushing toward ruin—but today, of all days, the face of a man he hated more than anything suddenly came to mind.

“Are you really okay?”

Kangnan, who had clung to Gaold and cried for a long time, suddenly came to her senses and sat up.

“What if I’m not? What do you want me to do, die?”

Gaold’s body was still steeped in pain.

“You didn’t show any signs of waking up this whole time… How did you come back?”

“The door opened.”

He couldn’t recall his memories in Under Coder, but now that he was back in reality, his senses told him certain things.

He must have walked endlessly and, eventually, crossed the boundary of the dead and entered Hell.

Even amid searing pain, as countless surrounding souls melted away in Hell’s fire...

“I just kept walking.”

Gaold’s soul, tethered by a thread to life, never dissolved—it simply endured eternal suffering.

“And then I walked through the door.”

When the altar opened, a spirit realm had formed, and for Gaold, that place marked the end of Hell.

“Is that seriously what you’re going to say right now?”

Despite having gone through a journey beyond imagination, his calm tone left Kangnan stunned.

“But it’s the truth.”

Gaold took a long drag on his cigarette and then snuffed it out with his fingers.

“There’s no need to prolong the pain.”

“Pain, huh…”

Gaold looked down absently at his fingertips, then turned to Kangnan.

“My pain sensitivity has increased.”

The moment she heard that, her heart sank—because even before his deep sleep, the pain had already been unbearable.

“How… How can you live like that…”

Kangnan began to cry again, but Gaold only offered a bitter smile.

“Pain is the only proof that I’m alive. No one in this world is living as intensely as I am.”

“But in that condition…”

Just as Kangnan was about to speak again, the screams of the dead echoed all the way down to the lowest level of the pyramid.

“...Sounds like Zulu’s finished.”

Gaold immediately understood just from the sound, and shortly after, Zulu entered the room.

“The demons have been annihilated.”

Kangnan tried to explain that Gaold had woken up, but Zulu had already sensed it through the pyramid’s tremors.

“...You’ve returned.”

“Thanks to you. I owe you again.”

“Wandering through Hell… I doubt that time was meaningless to you. So, what did you come back with?”

Through a trial no human could ever imagine, what had Gaold gained? Was it Nane—or Shirone?

“Nothing special.”

Gaold clenched his palm.

“I just got stronger.”

Zulu nodded and dragged over a chair to sit beside Gaold’s bed.

“In that case, you’ll need to decide how to use that strength.”

She calmly recounted the events that had unfolded since Gaold had fallen into deep sleep.

Gaold didn’t react much, but when he heard about the Zion Project, his brow twitched.

“It’s chaos. Nothing is set in stone—only conflicting beliefs clashing against each other.”

“Nane is right.”

Kangnan’s eyes wavered at Gaold’s words.

“If not a single person remains, that would be the world’s true happy ending.”

No one would be left to feel wronged. No one would be left to feel anything.

“Then you should go to Nane.”

For someone like Gaold—undoubtedly the most tormented being in the universe—agreeing with Nane’s ideals seemed only natural.

“But… Miss Miro is standing against Nane.”

Gaold’s face twisted in frustration.

Obsession.

Even though he knew that closing his eyes would free him from pain, he had continued walking through the flames of Hell.

Nane is salvation.

The void, where everything is erased—nothingness—was the only way Gaold could sever the chains of obsession.

“Haah…”

A reckless, arrogant woman. A wild filly who treated a man’s sincerity like trash.

“Heh. Heheheh…”

The more he thought about it, the more ridiculous it seemed. He let out a hollow laugh.

“She’s really driving me insane.”

Having made up his mind, Gaold abruptly raised his head.

“So all I have to do is crush that bastard Nane?”

Zulu and Kangnan didn’t reply, but they knew he wouldn’t change his mind now.

Gaold’s resolve was larger than the universe.
