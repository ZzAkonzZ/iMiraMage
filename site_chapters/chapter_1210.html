Chapter 1210:

Because it was a matter of life and death, Marsha carefully reviewed the newly added clauses in the contract.

Shirone.

The person who had given her a second chance.

I hope you're happy. You're the one who gave me back my family and my life.

So...

I won't say a word. If it's what you want, I won't tell you no matter what happens.

Marsha signed the contract.

Even though she was behind the main system pillar, the distance to the battlefield wasn’t even fifty meters.

But even the sounds of combat felt faint, as she was deep in her own thoughts.

What should I do?

The file containing the history of Omega 999.

According to the contract, she had to send it directly to Fermi. But the problem was that it was only 83% complete.

How much of humanity’s future is left? What if that 83% only covered the past?

I have to check now. To send the data to Fermi, someone has to exit into reality. If it turns out to be empty data, it’d be wasting a crucial chance.

Since time flows differently between Apocalypse and reality, once you go out, it takes a long time to come back.

I have to look. This is the only chance I’ll get to undo things. As long as I don’t tell Shirone, it’s fine.

She pulled out an analog watch and calculated real-world time using the time difference.

Alright. If there’s any information dated beyond today, then go. If not, we mine again.

Marsha opened the file.

Records written in human language were packed tightly in chronological order.

Flip. Flip.

She barely registered the words.

She flipped through the pages quickly, just looking for dates—until her face lit up.

There it is.

There wasn’t much, but the record clearly included today’s date.

Okay, this is enough— Then, a single line of text caught her eye.

What...?

Before she could even form a thought, her brain absorbed an entire paragraph of events.

Marsha slowly lifted her head.

Even in shock, her hand automatically shut off the device.

So this was it.

Fermi had been right when he said she’d know as soon as she saw it.

Shirone.

Her eyes welled up.

So that’s how it was? You knew everything… and still chose to erase your memory?

At that moment, a thunderous sound erupted.

"Marsha!"

Turning her head, she saw Zets being destroyed in waves as Operator collapsed.

"We have to go now!"

As a path opened toward the elevator, Freeman pointed and Marsha leapt toward it.

"Hurry! Get in!"

Just before the doors closed, Operator, now in the form of a young girl, boarded the elevator.

"Whew, I thought I was dead."

"You did great, sis," said Candy Mark, glancing up at Marsha with a frown.

"Once this job is over, you have to give it back. And don’t mess with anything else. It’s personal."

"Oh? Yeah, okay."

What's up with him?

Unlike before, when she lost her mind over being called ma’am, her face now was filled with sorrow.

Smile Mark asked, "Do we have a plan? There are probably Zets outside. And we don’t have any more lifting equipment."

"We’ll hold them off. You all need to run. Find a safe place and leave Apocalypse."

Once, Shirone's discarded data—known as Rosciné—had dismantled Apocalypse.

Using that data, Fermi had created an escape code. That was...

Escape.

Smile Mark looked nervously at the pill in her palm.

It takes up to ten minutes to fully escape. Will there even be a suitable place?

As they reached the surface, just as expected, countless Zets had sealed off the entrance.

This is too much.

Black Circle Mark spotted something and muttered,

"Game over."

"Sis! Don’t say unlucky things like that—"

At that moment, a flash of light exploded from the left side of their vision and swept the Zets away in an instant.

Shirone landed in the empty space.

"Shirone!"

Candy Mark ran up, and the Parrot Mercenaries all let out a breath of relief.

"What took you so long?! We’ve been waiting forever."

"Sorry."

Shirone immediately checked the group’s numbers.

"Is everyone okay?"

"Yeah. Let’s move. We need to run one last check before escaping."

Using Hand of God, Shirone swept up the group and soared into the sky.

Zets chased after them, but none could catch him once he was committed to fleeing.

It really never ends.

The same patterns, the same cityscapes—there wasn’t a trace of nature anywhere.

"This won’t work. Let’s land for now."

Once they reached a point out of sight from the Zets, Shirone asked Marsha about the excavation.

"How did it go? Were you successful?"

Marsha nodded, struggling to keep her expression in check.

"Yeah. It’s not perfect, but we achieved our goal. Once we send it to Fermi, it’s over."

"That’s a relief."

Letting out a sigh, Shirone scanned their surroundings.

"There’s nowhere to hide. Zets will be on us soon. Let’s get out of here."

No one objected, and everyone gripped their escape pills.

Marsha asked, "What’s the order?"

There was no need to decide.

"You all go ahead. I’ll move one more time, then I’ll go last."

"Alright."

In the end, Shirone taking responsibility was the best choice, but Marsha felt overwhelmed with sadness.

‘Why?’

Shirone’s figure, watching the far edge of the city, felt unusually forlorn today.

'You take on everything. You fight for the world… so why? Why can’t you be happy?'

What Marsha saw was—

'Shirone.'

A signal flare announcing the end of Omega, the final chapter of humanity’s history.

'Amy is going to die.'

It would happen six hours from now.

'I won’t be able to stop it.'

It was that powerful of an event—but if Shirone knew, everything could change.

'But I...'

Marsha clenched her teeth, holding back her tears.

'I can’t tell you!'

The moment someone becomes attached to someone special, Yahweh’s standards begin to collapse.

'How much must it have hurt?'

Even after hearing about Amy’s death from Fermi, he erased his own memory—for the sake of the world.

'How many tears did he cry?'

He must have asked over and over. Denied it again and again. Desperately searched for every possible way to change the outcome.

"Marsha."

Shirone turned around.

"Why do you look so sad? Don’t worry. I’ll find a safe place to get out too."

Marsha looked away and swallowed the pill.

"Okay. I’ll be waiting for you."

Of course, by the time Shirone returned, the data would already be in Fermi’s hands.

"Yeah. See you outside."

Just as she began to feel lightheaded, Marsha suddenly widened her eyes and called out.

"Hey… Shirone."

"Hm?"

His innocent face, like he would accept anything she said, only made her more conflicted.

'I should tell him.'

Wasn’t that the right thing to do?

'What does humanity matter? That kid forgave me. He gave me a new life. So I should…'

If the price of speaking was death, she would offer her life without hesitation.

But this was Shirone’s decision.

A sacrifice so vast it was unimaginable to someone like her—a mere criminal.

"It’s just… thank you."

Marsha hid her emotions and smiled softly.

"I’ll take care of you for the rest of my life, brat."

"Haha."

Marsha’s body—or rather, her spirit—dissolved like a mirage and disappeared.

"You really are something else."

Shirone shook his head and looked up at the sky, stretching his arms.

"Guess I’d better get going too."

Even though the future, ruled by God, remained unchanged, Shirone did not fall into despair.

'Thank you.'

Because there was heart here, too.

Even if it was a once-in-a-lifetime error, that heart would shape the future of humanity through Shirone.

And so—

'Goodbye, Apocalypse.'

"Mika."

"Construction progress is at 31.6548%."

The dramatically improved speed of Shirone Sphere’s work came only at the cost of pain.

"Rrghhhh!"

In construction that exceeded all human knowledge, method and efficiency meant nothing.

'I can go even faster.'

As he focused, his brain began to burn—and then he actually smelled something burning.

'...Is this for real?'

At that moment, a solar wind swept in.

'Amy.'

Shirone, using his transcendent telescopic vision, pulled in the light and caught the collision of fire and flame.

The Phoenix let out a shriek.

"KIIIIIIII!"

Since they shared the same elemental affinity, the stronger flame would eventually consume the weaker one.

"No way…"

The Phoenix locked onto Amy, who had suddenly moved 3,000 kilometers away.

"You’re faster than me?"

"Because I’m the sun."

The two avatars, resonating with the sun, communicated through reflected light.

"That applies to you too, I’m sure. But—"

Amy grinned.

"I’m a professional mage."

With her unique power source—spontaneous combustion—she could move anywhere within the sun.

"Our batteries are different. Did you know? On my planet, you’re classified as a Tier-2 creature."

Of course, that didn’t mean it was weak.

"...Is that so?"

The Phoenix grew larger, and even from 3,000 kilometers away, its ferocious eyes were visible.

"A mortal dares mock me!"

The Phoenix charged at full power, swallowed Amy’s body, and soared into the sky.

"Rrghhh! I am fire!"

Just as it tried to dive into the sun, a small vortex began to form in its chest.

"Huh?"

In the next instant, the convection intensified, and the Phoenix’s wings and neck twisted violently.

"GRAAAAAH!"

Complete combustion.

Engulfed in a pillar of flame, the Phoenix realized its very essence was being sucked away.

"No! No—!"

The vortex exploded, and Amy—her body ablaze in female form—stood upright in midair.

"Raaaah! Raaaah!"

Before her was a bird now so small, it couldn’t even be considered larger than a human.

"Grghhh…!"

The Phoenix, having no essence left to maintain its form, bowed its head in resignation.

"Well, it’s not like you were wronged. You were the Phoenix… and now you become something else."

"Yeah."

Amy nodded.

"And one day, we’ll both be broken down and become something else in the universe."

"Heh."

The Phoenix looked up.

"To think I would go from fire to human. Usually, it’s the other way around. Although… is it rude to say that? You’ve reached a state beyond humanity—so why don’t you rule the universe?"

"Because I’m not alone."

Amy walked up to the Phoenix.

"If I’d been alone, I never would’ve made it this far. The people who helped me. The ones who died for me. The ones who fought me. The ones who hated me. I’m just one human who owes them all."

For some reason, the faces of everyone she had met up to now flashed through her mind.

"I’m not just a person. I’m human."

Amy cradled the half-melted Phoenix on her lap and gently stroked its beak.

"You don’t have to shine alone anymore. Let’s go bring light to everyone—together."

"Human, huh…"

Gazing into the endless expanse of the universe, the Phoenix smiled faintly and closed its eyes.

"Yeah… that doesn’t sound so bad."

As the Phoenix’s form melted away, its essence flowed into Amy’s flame.

It was the fire of the heart—stronger than law itself.
