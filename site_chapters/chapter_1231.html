Chapter 1231:

Abel was horrified.

Cain stood motionless, soaked by the rain, with an expression Abel had never seen before—something completely alien, completely foreign.

Run.

It must have been that kind of instinctual warning, but Abel forced a smile, trying to steady his emotions.

“What’s wrong? Why that look on your face?”

“Abel.”

Cain hadn’t done anything yet—but even that was enough to send a chill down his spine.

‘How free are we, really?’

If God was indifferent, what would even change by acting on a thought?

‘This isn’t right.’

Within that tremor of insight, Cain discovered the only brake still holding him back.

‘Killing my brother… is wrong.’

So wrong, in fact, that even this infinite freedom granted by God felt as though it was now bound by some invisible restraint.

“Abel, I…”

Cain started to speak—when suddenly a voice came from inside the house.

“Who’s here?”

Lulua stepped out of the bedroom. Abel turned toward Cain with an awkward smile.

“She said she was scared because of the thunder, so…”

Thud.

Even now, after all this time, Cain couldn’t remember exactly what had happened in that moment.

“...Cain?”

In the blink of an eye, the world went pale—and when he came to, a dagger was lodged in Abel’s chest.

The first murder.

Abel couldn’t comprehend what had just happened to him.

“...Why?”

His massive frame collapsed to the ground with a heavy thud. Lulua blinked for a moment, lost in thought.

‘What… was that?’

Why had Cain stabbed Abel?

‘Why isn’t he breathing? Is he… dead? Like an animal? Like the ones we eat… is he just gone?’

Her mind, unable to analyze this emotional aftershock, was overwhelmed by its sheer magnitude.

“Aaah!”

Lulua sank to her knees, and Cain stared at his brother’s cooling body.

‘I killed him.’

A thousand thoughts raced through his mind.

Feelings that, even if he spent a lifetime contemplating, would never be sorted out.

But—‘Lulua is mine now.’

Cain forced himself to ignore everything else and focus solely on his original purpose.

“Lulua.”

Even as Cain approached, Lulua couldn’t bring herself to blame anyone.

Because she was the first.

Even though something had clearly happened, she questioned the reality of it before she could even begin to judge it.

“Abel is gone now. He’s disappeared from this world. It’s just you and me left.”

“…Brother.”

Lulua was confused.

She had never defined anything as wrong or evil—so Cain’s words carried weight.

“Come here.”

But as he took her hand and led her toward the bedroom, she made up her mind.

‘I love Abel.’

Even if she had to live with Cain for the rest of her life, that wouldn’t change.

“No. No, brother! I can’t be your wife!”

“That’s not true.”

There were only about sixty siblings. Humanity had only just begun—there weren’t many choices.

“This is already done. Abel can’t come back. Now you’ll be mine.”

His brain was on the verge of exploding from animalistic desire.

For a brief moment, half-lost to reason, Cain let himself be swept away by that craving.

But the moment he heard Lulua’s heartbroken sobs, everything became clear.

“Lulua…”

The way she looked at him—the man who killed her beloved brother—

‘I… I…’

Her eyes contained a disgust that could have shattered someone’s soul, even without a blade.

‘What have I done?’

Cain recoiled from her like he’d touched burning stone.

“N-no…”

He’d done it because he could—that’s all.

“Lulua, please…”

Don’t look at me like that.

“AAAAAGHHH!”

Grabbing his head, Cain bolted from the room—only to slip on a puddle of blood.

“Huuuuuh!”

Abel’s lifeless eyes stared back at him.

“No!”

As he burst through the door and fled the house, lightning streaked endlessly across the sky.

KRA-KOOOM!

Rain fell in sheets, drenching him in an instant—and each drop felt like Abel’s blood on his skin.

KRA-KOOOM!

“No!”

Cain couldn’t see anything anymore.

He was terrified—praying for time to freeze, for everything to just stop.

Like something was chasing him, he ran to Lilith’s door and pounded on it.

“Mother! Mother!”

In the end, the only person he could turn to… was her.

“Who is it?”

Lilith stared at Cain, whose soaked appearance seemed heavier than the rain itself.

And the ominous bloodstain on his chest…

“Mother…”

Cain’s voice trembled.

“I killed someone.”

Lilith calmly absorbed the words, then took his wrist and pulled him inside.

“Come in.”

Meanwhile, back at Abel’s house, Lulua’s sobs echoed like broken glass.

“Abel… Abel…”

What she finally realized was this:

Something that should never have happened had happened.

“Aaaah!”

Clutching Abel’s body, Lulua wept uncontrollably.

“No! You can’t leave me like this…!”

The shock blurred her mind—her pupils rolled upward, revealing the whites of her eyes.

“U-uh?”

And at that moment, creeeeeak—the door opened.

A towering silhouette appeared—far taller than any human.

And as lightning lit up the darkness—

“…Dad.”

Back at Lilith’s house, she handed a warm cup of tea to Cain.

“Here. Drink this.”

“Thank you.”

Drying himself with a towel, Cain sat on the edge of the bed and sipped the tea slowly.

“Haaah…”

His hands still trembled, but being in his mother’s home brought a strange comfort.

He’d never actually been here before.

No matter what Lilith did in this house, her children had always simply accepted it.

“What happened?”

Lilith asked softly.

Cain answered honestly—his jealousy of Abel, his hope that removing him would bring Lulua to him, and the blinding flash.

“I thought it’d be okay…”

Cain’s tears spilled.

“Just because I could… I thought that meant I should. Mother… what do I do now?”

Lilith was heartbroken.

Because she understood better than anyone the desire to claim the one you love.

“Come here.”

When she opened her arms, Cain collapsed into her embrace with a face full of despair.

Her pupils trembled at the intensity of it.

“Ah…”

How long had it been since she felt the warmth of another human being?

“It’s all right, my son. I’m here. This mother will protect you. Do you understand?”

Cain realized something then.

“Mother…”

As long as there was one person who supported him, he could keep on living.

“Haaah…”

Lilith let out a strained sigh through clenched teeth and gently caressed Cain’s chest.

It was… a kind of pull.

There were endless excuses she could give—but in truth, none of them mattered.

Their bodies, trembling with unbearable spasms, slowly surrendered their reason.

Cain had no one else to turn to, and he was already intoxicated with the thrill of murder and lust.

Perhaps that’s why.

‘This is madness.’

Lilith fought desperately to come to her senses.

‘Stop now. If I stop now, it’s not too late. Cain will listen to me.’

She opened her mouth to speak.

“Now, let’s—”

But in that moment, she saw it—the crystallization of desire that was fading from her memory.

A serpent.

The most beautiful snake in existence, whispering softly:

Take me.

“Ah… ahhh…”

As the buried depths of suppressed desire surged upward, she felt like a volcano on the verge of eruption.

‘I’ll have him!’

And so, Lilith embraced that hardened body and accepted it with her whole being.

Her scream rang throughout the room.

“Aaaahhh!”

Unfathomable pleasure surged beyond the physical world into something primal.

—Covet! Steal! Kill!—

Caught in a vortex of demonic energy beyond reason, the two of them were suddenly struck by realization.

Sin.

This was sin.

As terror and shame filled their bodies, the pleasure only intensified in equal measure.

‘We’ve eaten the forbidden fruit.’

Good and evil.

For the first time since humanity’s birth, what was right and what was wrong had been defined.

“Lilith!”

Adam’s voice rang out, and Cain jolted like he’d been struck by lightning, falling from the bed.

“F-Father…”

Even the witch of lust, Lilith, couldn’t bear the shame—she pulled the blanket up to cover herself.

“W-Why are you here…?”

A radiant glow flickered in Adam’s Gaian eyes.

“So you’ve become a beast. You threw away your last chance to enter Heaven.”

Lilith snapped.

“All you ever think about is Heaven! Do you really think Goffin will summon you back just for expanding the Gaian race? It’s over! I don’t exist just to give birth!”

“Oooohhh!”

Light poured like a waterfall from Adam’s eyes—tears of divine sorrow.

‘Forgive me.’

He had failed to uphold Goffin’s will.

Now that the last Gaian rule had been broken, Ultima could no longer exist.

“We’ve created sin!”

In one bound, Adam leapt past Cain and wrapped his hands around Lilith’s neck, squeezing hard.

“Ghk—!”

Her neck snapped without resistance, and Cain collapsed, legs trembling.

“F-Father…”

Adam looked down at him.

“…Leave.”

Who could he blame?

All of their siblings were born from Adam and Lilith.

“I must carry this sin as well.”

Under Ultima, it didn’t matter what you hunted or who you slept with—it was all deemed righteous.

But now—murder and incest.

There were things one must not kill, people one must not touch.

Good and evil had now been defined.

“We are no longer pure righteousness. From now on, bound to consumption and reproduction, we will commit endless sins.”

Adam said:

“None of us can escape from the origin of this.”

That was what would be known as Original Sin.

And when Adam left Eden…

Cain collapsed to the ground, clutching his head.

“Ughhh… why… why!”

Cain had only tilled the earth. Abel had only raised sheep. That’s all.

“Why me?! Why Abel?!”

What could he do about it? The cold, unfeeling world and its laws had chosen Abel, not him.

“Huuuuh…”

How long did he cry?

Eventually, the storm passed, and the sky began to lighten.

Cain lifted his head abruptly.

‘I have to leave.’

He couldn’t face his siblings.

‘But… where?’

There was no one else in the world besides his brothers and sisters, and yet Cain realized there was nowhere to hide.

It felt as if the entire world condemned him. That someone would come and kill him.

The first guilt.

Before the sun rose completely, Cain left Eden.

He wandered aimlessly across the world.

He wasn’t free anywhere, and even as his body wasted away, he kept thinking—

‘Why did I do it?’

If dying could resolve anything, he would gladly take his own life.

Tears—dry and cracked—streamed down his face.

‘I should’ve held back. I had dozens—no, hundreds of chances. Why didn’t I stop…?’

Cain fell to his knees.

“Please… just let me go. I can’t endure even a moment of this! I said stop!”

He was going mad, caught in a noose called guilt.

“What did I do wrong?! Abel could’ve killed me too! Anyone could’ve done it! Is that what makes it evil?!”

He broke down again.

“God, please save me. If only that hadn’t happened… if only I hadn’t done that one thing…”

And then—

A thick mist settled before his eyes, and a golden building appeared.

Cain stared, entranced.

Was it madness? It felt like the building was speaking to him.

—Come. None shall condemn you.—

It was the Hall of Evil—Melkidu.
