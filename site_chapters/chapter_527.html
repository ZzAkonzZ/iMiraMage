Chapter 527:

Regulation Exception – Depreciation Trade.

Fermi extracted magic from the seller and provided it to the buyer in the form of chips, taking a commission in return.

The price of the magic was subject to depreciation, meaning its value would gradually decrease over time.

Basically, the buyer paid the seller directly, and Fermi only took a cut—so he didn’t need capital to operate his ability.

However, when Fermi himself was the buyer, he faced a restriction: he could only make contracts using the profits he had earned from previous Depreciation Trades.

At present, Fermi’s total commission earnings from Regulation Exception amounted to roughly 600 million gold.

Of course, to maintain both his school life and his broker activities, he had to regularly purchase chips.

For instance, one of the chips he used to score perfect marks across all 20 categories in a specialty exam was something called “Basic Air Magic,” which he bought from a retired certified mage for around 10 million gold on a three-month contract.

After covering costs, the funds Fermi had available for active use were about half that—around 300 million gold. But that amount still fell far short of what he was ultimately aiming for.

Then, a deal came in—offering a return six hundred times greater than his total earnings to date.

Fermi wasn’t someone who showed emotion easily, but even he had no choice but to concede to Miro this time.

“You extract the magic safely through depreciation. Then you use the profit to enhance your own. It’s a very efficient system—if used properly. But the issue is the commission. I don’t know how much you’ve made so far, or what kind of spell you’re after that led you to develop something like this. But the fact that you still haven’t graduated means you haven’t reached your goal.”

Fermi’s eyes turned cold.

“But now I’ve offered you a deal worth 190 billion gold. With a 99 percent commission. Which means you’ll pocket 189 billion over three years. Are you really going to turn down a chance to buy something far beyond anything you’ve accessed so far?”

“Ha ha ha! Hahahahaha!”

Fermi leaned back and laughed.

Miro couldn’t read the emotion behind that laugh.

Then, abruptly, the laughter stopped. Fermi gave her a wide smile.

“Correct. I can’t fool you, Aunt.”

“Of course not. So don’t bother with cheap tricks. Like I said, I didn’t come to you just to extract a spell. If there’s no way to bypass Shirone’s moratorium, I’ll find another Regulation Exception user. And that 190 billion will vanish.”

“Alright, alright. I get it, okay? Just hear me out.”

Even as he said it, he didn’t speak for a while.

Fermi rested his chin on his hand, slouched, and muttered under his breath.

“There’s… not exactly no way.”

“Knew it. What is it?”

Fermi scratched his head, looking annoyed.

“But it’s extremely difficult. I wasn’t holding out to get more—just didn’t want to even think about it. It’s not just risky. It’s incredibly inefficient.”

“That kind of talk just makes my Adrias blood boil.”

“There’s something some magical loan sharks try from time to time. Usually when the original contract holder dies. In those cases, there’s only one thing left to do.”

Fermi snatched the contract off the table and pointed at the signature line.

“You forge the signature.”

“Aha.”

Miro let out a hollow-sounding exclamation.

“But how? With a magical contract, the signer’s will has to be present, right?”

“Which is why illegal methods are needed. If Shirone’s avatar is under moratorium, we get a replica of the avatar to sign instead.”

“How do you replicate an avatar? Even with Regulation Exception, you can’t duplicate it if Shirone’s avatar is dormant.”

“It’s not about replicating it. It’s already ‘somewhere.’”

Arius abruptly lifted his head.

“No way…”

Miro turned to him.

“You know what he’s talking about?”

“It’s the same principle as when I found your trauma in Drimo. Human consciousness flows into Drimo through dreams. So then, what happens to the minds of the dead?”

Miro blinked.

“When the mind is no longer productive, it breaks apart into fragments and merges with other data. The leftover fragments that can’t combine settle into the lowest layer. Mental-type mages refer to this as ‘Minority Conception.’”

Fermi picked up the explanation.

“Minority Conception consists of information that’s unnecessary for constructing the world. Data too common to pair with anything, too unique to blend, or too twisted to be accepted—this is where it all ends up. These fragments accumulate into a world known as ‘The Abyss.’”

Arius continued.

“That’s what makes it so serious. Information has no meaning by itself. For instance, if all you had was the idea of ‘apple,’ you wouldn’t know what it was. Only by linking it with things like ‘red,’ ‘fruit,’ ‘tree,’ and ‘sunlight’ can you understand what an apple is. Minority Conception is made of things rejected from those links. Most of the world’s information denies it. Concepts like ‘murder’ or ‘torture’ are still somewhat accepted. But anything worse becomes the exclusive property of a few.”

“So the Abyss is a mass of those rejected ideas.”

“Exactly. And in time, all information in this world will eventually become Minority Conception. As entropy increases, old data is replaced by new. The Abyss is essentially the final resting place of all data.”

Information is the foundation of everything in this world.

And if the Abyss was the graveyard of that data, Miro understood what that meant.

“The final future this world can reach.”

Arius nodded.

“The apocalypse. In simple terms, it’s a virtual world that simulates the end of everything.”

Fermi added,

“If the present changes, the Abyss changes with it. But its nature as a world of ruin doesn’t change. It’s where discarded data ends up.”

“And Shirone’s data might be buried there?”

Fermi circled back to the point.

“It’s possible. The Abyss is also called hell because the minds of the dead become Minority Conception and sink there. In Shirone’s case, though, we don’t know how much of his avatar has been picked up as Minority Conception. It might be intact—or heavily damaged.”

“If we could recover that information and use it to sign a contract, would it carry the same power as if the real Shirone had signed?”

“If it’s properly restored, yes. The data is effectively the same as the real Shirone.”

Arius looked uncertain.

“But how do we get there? As living beings with productive data, even if we enter Drimo, we won’t be pushed into the Abyss. To go there, we’d have to die—or destroy our minds with extreme shock. And if we do that, we won’t be able to carry out the mission.”

Fermi gave a cryptic smile.

“You can get there. In one piece.”

Both Miro and Arius looked at him.

“You can? How?”

“When data enters Drimo, it gets mixed with other data. The combinations are endless. But even if they seem chaotic to humans, the information still forms a complete world. The bits that get rejected from that system? They get flung out and fall into the Abyss. So…”

Fermi reached his conclusion.

“We just need to be rejected too. Get kicked out of Drimo. In this field, people call that space: ‘Under Coder.’”

It was a term even Miro hadn’t heard before.

“Those who deal with Minority Conception are extremely closed off. Reality doesn’t matter to them. They’ve built their own worlds in the Under Coder, so even you might not be familiar with it. If you go, you’ll be surprised. Some of those worlds are quite interesting.”

“So basically, they’re freaks. Whatever, I won’t ask about your preferences. So how do we enter this Under Coder?”

“Can’t tell you yet. There’s no point in going to the Abyss right now anyway. To recover Shirone’s avatar, we need people who’ve shared a deep connection with him. And that’s not us… right?”

“Hmm… then what if we ask his friends from magic school?”

Fermi held up a hand.

“No good. The academy is my livelihood. You’ll have to find someone outside the school who had a close bond with Shirone—and someone who can handle the mission. Those are my two conditions.”

“You’re setting conditions now? Don’t you want the money?”

Fermi shook his head without budging.

Even with a 190 billion gold deal on the table, he wouldn’t risk the academy. That alone made it clear that Fermi’s endgame wasn’t just about money.

But for now, waking Shirone was the priority—arguing wouldn’t help.

Miro thought,

“Either way, if we’re going to bypass the moratorium, we’ll need Fermi’s help.”

Just then, a maid called from outside the door.

"Young master, dinner is ready."

Fermi dusted off his clothes and stood up.

"Let’s eat first. We’ll spend the night here and leave tomorrow morning. In the meantime, I’ll finish preparing to enter the Under Coder."

"A deal’s a deal, then."

As they went downstairs, the rich aroma of meat pie filled the air.

It was a dish made by spreading tomato sauce on dough, layering it with bacon and vegetables, topping it with cheese, and baking it in the oven.

Miro opened her mouth wide to bite into a slice, cheese stretching long as she pulled it away, and her face lit up in bliss.

"Ah, it’s so good. I’ve been dying to eat this again."

"Anyone would think you hadn’t eaten in days."

At Enrique’s remark, Miro held up two fingers.

"Twenty years."

While trapped beyond the dimensional wall, she had been entirely free from bodily needs. But once she returned to the world, all of her metabolism had come back to life.

An unknowable secret passed on by Goffin.

And likely the very reason Shirone had been able to borrow the outcome of 127 years.

'Forge the contract. Find out what Shirone’s collateral really is.'

To resolve both, she’d need someone with a deep connection to Shirone.

'I guess it’s time to head to Kashan. I have to get the signature anyway.'

Enrique asked bluntly.

"So, when exactly are you leaving this house?"

Miro didn’t answer right away, lost in thought. Fermi replied in her place.

"She’ll probably be coming and going often. We have some matters to discuss. Right, Aunt?"

"Huh? Oh, right. I figured since he’s just wasting time instead of graduating, I should step in and teach him a thing or two."

"How fortunate. To study under the world’s greatest mage."

Enrique eyed the two of them suspiciously.

The fact that even family wasn’t being told meant some kind of deal had already been struck.

And since that deal was with Miro, it didn’t take much imagination to guess they were caught up in something beyond normal reasoning.

‘You tormented me all those years, and now you're dragging my son into it?’

Enrique let out a sigh, resigning himself.

Getting tangled with Miro had brought about plenty of impossible situations, but it was also the most fun he’d ever had in his life.

‘Fine, let it be. She’s not going to drag him into hell… right?’

It turned out to be quite a cheerful dinner.
