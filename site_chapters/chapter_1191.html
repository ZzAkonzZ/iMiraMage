Chapter 1191: The Organization of Darkness (1)

Yorahan felt a lump in his throat.

‘I wasn’t wrong.’

He had visited many cities, but his philosophy had never been popular among the masses.

‘But there is someone who understands. If even one person can empathize, then it’s not wrong.’

Even if they weren’t human—if their hearts could connect, then that was all that mattered.

“Thank you. Not only for saving my life but also… for giving me great encouragement.”

“You’re very welcome. Your beliefs are truly admirable. There is no flaw in your logic.”

That was precisely Yorahan’s dilemma.

‘If everyone made even a small effort to be considerate, suffering would disappear. There is no contradiction in that. So why can’t we reach it?’

Because human existence itself was a contradiction.

‘Convincing them is the reason I exist. Yes, let’s begin again. From this village.’

Yorahan asked,

“I know it’s shameless of me, but… may I stay here for a while? I’ll help in any way I can.”

“Please, there’s no need for such humility. Teach the Fire Tribe all that you can. Everyone will listen.”

The Fire Tribe helps humans.

As long as the teachings—presumably those of Buddha—continued to be passed down through the lineage of the lotus.

‘This person is different. He’s not like the humans who have tormented us.’

Lutia recalled the words of her predecessor.

— Lutia, even so, you must believe. Humans make mistakes, but in the end, they are a species that finds the right path.

Perhaps this man was…

“Please rest well. Your body must still be weak. I’ll inform the others.”

“Thank you.”

Yorahan hesitated for a moment, remembering the conversation he had overheard earlier.

“Oh, and… you don’t have to treat me so well. I’m used to hardship. Haha!”

Lutia smiled.

“I see.”

Perhaps this was what he had preached all along—a small act of kindness.

“Well then, I’ll head back for now. I need to unpack and check my notes.”

Yorahan returned to his hut.

“Phew, thankfully it’s not wet.”

He grabbed his notebook, which doubled as a journal and memo pad. Just as he pulled back the tent flap—

“Waaah! What the—?!”

Armand was standing right in front of him.

“You.”

“Ah, yes?”

She shoved her face close with fierce eyes, and his heart clenched as if he had already committed a crime.

“You’re staying in this village?”

“… That’s how it turned out. But I swear, I won’t ca-

“You know about the Fire Tribe, don’t you?”

“A-Ah, no! I mean, I have a general sense, but I don’t know any details…! I swear I’m not a bad person…!”

“No matter what you say, I don’t trust the words of a human.”

Yorahan shut his mouth.

“From now on, I’ll be watching you all day. When you eat, when you go to the bathroom, when you sleep—every second. Got it? The moment you leave my sight, think carefully about what might happen.”

“Even the bathroom?”

It seemed extreme, but if what he had heard about the Fire Tribe was true, he could understand.

‘A species that can’t refuse the requests of others? If they’re going this far, then it must be true.’

“Well? Your answer?”

Yorahan looked into Armand’s burning gaze for a moment, then let out a sigh, as if resigning himself.

“… Yes. If that’s what it takes to make you feel at ease.”

And so… their strange cohabitation began.

Melkidu’s inner track.

Shirone and his companions entered from section 321 of the outer track, a place known as the Pilgrim’s Village.

There was another entrance at section 122, but they had heard it was not the recommended route.

“We got here faster than expected,” Nade said.

He looked around at the scenery of section 7, the first area they had arrived in within the inner track.

“This… isn’t a city?”

It was, but only a fragment of one.

Gray buildings stretched endlessly along the horizon, with each area walled off into separate zones.

Iruki observed the layout.

“It looks like a cylindrical structure divided into 197 sections. If the outer track is managed by non-player entities, then this place might be directly controlled by administrators.”

Eden nodded in agreement.

“That makes sense. Things like dice enhancement and system-altering items fall under the administrators’ jurisdiction. But still… this feels way too blatant.”

Shirone stepped forward.

“Let’s take it as a sign that we’re getting closer to the Core. Since no system message has appeared, missions must only trigger under certain conditions. The hidden mission we’re looking for is probably one of them.”

Nade added, “Gathering information about bribery is important, but our biggest priority right now is resource management. We currently have about 206 million points. We need to save at least 50 million for bribery, which means we’re working with limited funds. And that’s just the minimum required.”

Iruki nodded.

“Right. If we assume that conserving Crime Points is our priority, we need to account for enhancement costs and item prices.”

Upon entering the administrative office, they found themselves at a junction beneath a large sign marked 7.

“One side is for system services, and the other is for shopping. That means dice enhancement and item purchases.”

“Let’s start with system services.”

Since Curtis had strongly advised enhancing their dice, no one objected.

“Welcome. I am the administrator of Sector 7.”

A woman in an immaculate suit sat behind the counter—an appearance completely unfitting for a place called the Sanctuary of Murderers.

“Uh… so…”

“This is your first time here, isn’t it? Welcome. This is where you apply for Crime Dice enhancement.”

“Yes. How much does it cost?”

“The standard Crime Dice consists of two six-sided dice. There are two ways to enhance them: increasing the number of faces or increasing the number of dice.”

The administrator pointed to the price chart on the table.

“The cost varies depending on the enhancement. Sector 7 only offers basic upgrades. Please take a look.”

They examined the price list.

Upgrading from six-sided to seven-sided cost 10 million points. From seven to eight, the cost was a staggering 100 million.

Increasing the number of dice was even worse.

From two dice to three cost 100 million, and from three to four, a full 1 billion points.

Considering that the key—the standard method to access the Core—cost 10 billion points, the prices were overwhelming.

Nade turned to Shirone.

“This is serious.”

“Yeah. Curtis had one eight-sided die and two six-sided ones. That means he spent at least 210 million points on enhancements.”

That was more than Shirone’s current balance.

The administrator interjected, “Enhancement is extremely important. No matter how good your items are, weak Crime Dice will lower your overall efficiency.”

Eden frowned.

“But why did Curtis upgrade that way? Wouldn’t it be more effective to turn all three of his dice into seven-sided ones?”

Iruki rested his chin on his hand.

“Because of doubles.”

“Doubles?”

“Yeah. No matter how many dice you have, if at least two of them land on the same number, you trigger the double effect, right?”

The administrator nodded.

“Yes, that’s correct.”

“So the more dice you have, the higher the chance of rolling a double. Even if you have three or four dice, you only need two to match. On the other hand, increasing the number of faces actually reduces the probability of doubles—because it adds more possible outcomes.”

“That’s it. It might seem like a small probability difference, but in Melkidu, where everything depends on dice, that slight edge makes a huge impact. It’s worth paying an extra 80 million points just for that difference. So increasing the number of dice is more important than increasing the number of faces.”

That was the conventional wisdom.

But it didn’t matter for them. Or rather, it didn’t matter for Shirone. He could manipulate the numbers however he wanted.

Shirone was thinking the same thing.

He would have to spend 100 million to add a third die anyway. The difference between having two and three was too significant. In that case, going for a 7, 7, 7 setup would give him a total advantage of plus one compared to other setups.

It was just one point, but in a game where numbers decided everything, that one could one day become a thousand, then ten thousand, and ultimately determine victory or defeat.

Iruki asked, “Do you have any recommended builds?”

“There are advantages and disadvantages to all builds. It depends on the user’s playstyle and which items they use. For example, an item called Bonanza cubes the difference between the highest and lowest dice rolls. That makes asymmetrical dice setups statistically more favorable.”

“Hm. I’ve heard about items, but isn’t Wind God more effective?”

“It depends. Wind God is a high-tier item. Some players prefer to play with a few powerful items, while others rely on multiple weaker items. For the latter group, Bonanza provides excellent cost-efficiency.”

The administrator continued, “The best hint I can give is statistical data. Among three-dice users in Melkidu, 17 percent use an 8-6-6 build. That means there’s a wide range of viable strategies. The 8-6-6 setup provides stability and synergizes well with items. However, here in Sector 7, the limit is 7-6-6. If you want to upgrade from 7 to 8, you’ll need to visit Sector 40 or higher.”

“I see,” Iruki nodded.

“There are two entrances to the Pilgrim’s Village in the outer track—Sections 122 and 321. But entering through 122 forces you to travel backward from Section 197 in the inner track. That could cause problems, like buying expensive items too early and not being able to afford critical ones later. Or messing up your enhancements.”

Eden added, “But if you already have your items and dice upgrades done, it wouldn’t matter, right? A veteran could go in reverse without issue.”

“Exactly. This structure is meant to prevent beginners from getting lucky and snowballing their advantage. If someone stumbles into the inner track early and buys a key item, the gap between players will only widen.”

Nade grumbled, “For a game about killing people, they sure put a lot of effort into balance. What, are they telling us to kill each other fairly?”

The administrator took it as a compliment.

“Thank you. That fast-track strategy was popular at one point. It forces players to move backward through all 197 sections, but they can still purchase items and escape into Section 321, where the high-difficulty zones begin. However, this strategy requires exceptional player skill.”

Shirone considered it—if they made it to their second cycle, it might be worth trying.

The administrator brought them back to the present.

“Have you understood everything? Then let’s proceed with your enhancements. You have two options. First, you can pay the full amount. Second, you can get a discount.”

The group blinked.

“A discount? What is this, a holiday sale?”

“It’s part of the system’s design. If you can convince me, I can grant you a 20 percent discount on Crime Dice enhancement.”

“20 percent?”

That was higher than expected.

If they assumed spending 100 million to add another die, that would save them 20 million points.

Nade asked, “How do we convince you? Do we just sit down for tea and chat?”

The administrator gave a chilling smile.

“Of course not.”

Curtis’s words suddenly resurfaced in Shirone’s mind.

In the inner track, dice are king.

And here, their fate would once again be determined by a roll.
