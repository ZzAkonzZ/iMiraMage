Chapter 785:

Uorin’s Room.

The Ex Machina team sat in a row of chairs, their eyes fixed on a single woman standing before them.

As Shirone and his group entered, Uorin, who had been quietly observing from a corner, raised her index finger to her lips and approached.

“You’re here. We just got started.”

Shirone looked at the woman.

“So she’s the Alpha Fish.”

He had expected someone more hysterical, but she stood nervously with her gaze lowered, looking almost shy.

“She’s surprisingly normal.”

“No, she’s not,”

Minerva said, her eyes narrowing with intensity as she stared at the woman.

“She’s like me.”

A woman born with the fate of a witch.

While there’s no specific “look” that marks someone as a witch, those born under that fate share a certain aura.

“A presence that stirs desire.”

The kind of look that made people want to torment, break, and watch her sob into her pillow before they felt satisfied.

Uorin, who once lived bearing the same “witch’s aura,” recognized the same thing Minerva did.

“She has the archetypal witch’s air. But surprisingly, she’s not a witch. She runs a small flower shop now.”

Minerva’s eyes thinned to slits.

“She’s not… a witch?”

If defying fate were that simple, Minerva would never have created Lawyslayer.

Shirone asked, “What happened?”

“First… let’s watch.”

As Iruki joined the Ex Machina team, Uorin turned and gave a small nod in that direction.

Taking the cue, Gudio spoke.

“Miss Yus Abella.”

Minerva’s eyebrow twitched.

“A survivor of the Yus?”

Once a major nomadic tribe rivaling the top ranks of the gypsy clans, they were thought to have been wiped out.

“Yes… Please speak.”

Abella’s voice was barely above a whisper.

“No need to be nervous. This isn’t an interrogation. We just need to confirm a few things. First, I assume you know why you were brought to the capital?”

“Yes, I was told. The mustache guy—no, Mr. Havitz…”

Abella lifted her head for the first time.

“He’s a really bad man.”

She seemed to understand that Havitz was evil intellectually, but emotionally, she hadn’t accepted it.

Iruki spoke.

“You’re right. He’s killed countless people—still is—and will continue to do so. I assume you’ve heard about the mass burial incident—over 70,000 people. We need your help.”

“M-me? How can I help…?”

Maice asked,

“Do you think Havitz shouldn’t die?”

Abella quickly shook her head.

“No! If he did bad things, he should be punished! I think killing people is wrong!”

Iruki let out a long breath.

“So that’s the witch’s fate… I get it now.”

Even now, without meaning to, the Ex Machina team was unconsciously tormenting her.

“You can be honest with us. Even if you loved Havitz, we wouldn’t harm you.”

She was a gypsy without a homeland, and having grown up in the Gustav Empire, Kashan was enemy territory.

And standing in front of the Empress—of course she’s nervous.

Abella took a breath, calming herself. Her gaze locked clearly onto Iruki.

“No. I believe Havitz deserves to die.”

And yet… her words lacked emotional weight.

“I’ll explain from here,”

Uorin stepped forward.

“They first met 14 years ago. Havitz was 27. Abella was 9.”

All eyes turned to her.

“Gustav royal bloodlines are infamous for their tradition of familial purging. Havitz gained his reputation by ruthlessly killing his own siblings. Some say when the emperor exiled Havitz the 17th to Arkaba, the farthest eastern region of the empire, it wasn’t strategy—it was fear. Even Havitz the 16th may have feared his own son.”

Abella seemed unaware of this history.

“In the end, Havitz the 17th killed 781 of his siblings and became emperor. Across three resets, the methods he used to kill them were always different. In total, he murdered them using 2,343 different techniques.”

That is why he is chaos incarnate.

“And 14 years ago, during what we now call the Great Purge, his method was…”

Uorin’s eyes chilled.

“He tied his brother and sister-in-law to the ceiling—and held a grand feast where he boiled and ate their child.”

Abella’s body trembled violently as if struck by lightning.

Fourteen Years Ago – Arkaba, Easternmost Region of the Empire

“AAAGHH! STOP IT, YOU BASTARD! YOU’RE NOT EVEN HUMAN! YOU’LL BE CURSED!”

Havitz’s half-brother screamed, suspended from the ceiling, spitting blood.

Next to him, his wife had already gone insane. She laughed uncontrollably, soiling herself in madness.

“Hm… what part is this?”

Slicing meat with a knife, Havitz examined the cut with a fork.

“AAAHHH! STOP!”

At 27, Havitz looked much the same as he would later as emperor—save for the elegant mustache.

“Fondue.”

He dipped the meat in soup and popped it into his mouth with relish. His brother wept openly.

“Please… I beg you. You can be emperor. I’ll help you. I’ll make it happen. Just… stop…”

“Emperor?”

Havitz set down his cutlery and wiped his lips.

“You’re such a strange man. What’s so fun about being emperor? It’s dreadfully boring.”

“Then why… why kill even your own nephew…?”

Havitz looked up at the ceiling.

“Because becoming emperor is the fun part.”

“Ghhhaaa…”

His brother realized then.

Even though dozens of siblings still plotted against him, the next emperor had already been decided.

“Just kill me. End it.”

“I’ll eat you for dinner. Your wife will be dessert.”

Havitz grinned, nostrils flaring.

His brother felt like the world was spinning.

“You monster! My siblings will destroy you! I curse you! Even if I die, I’ll pray from hell that you suffer!”

Havitz yawned.

“Boring.”

He snapped his fingers, and the rope snapped—his brother plummeted 20 meters.

With a thud, another corpse joined the pile, and blood slowly pooled beneath the outstretched limbs.

Havitz turned to a maid.

“Isn’t there anything fun to do?”

He had no loyal advisors, no flattering sycophants. Only servants frozen in terror.

The maid trembled as she answered,

“T-the remaining relatives are in the dungeon. Perhaps… you could torture them…”

“Torture stopped being fun after I turned seven.”

The maid’s shoulders quivered.

“I-I’m sorry! I dared to speak beyond my station! Please forgive me!”

Havitz stared at her.

“Did something sad happen to you?”

She raised her face in panic and shook her head.

“What? N-no, I’m not sad!”

“Then why are you crying? Are you scared of me?”

Among Havitz’s entourage, nothing was more terrifying than being asked a question.

“W-what am I supposed to say…?”

Saying she was scared might get her killed. Saying she wasn’t might get her killed too.

In the end, emotion overwhelmed her.

“I’m scared! N-no, I mean—I’m sorry! Please forgive me! I’m worthless—!”

Her voice broke into madness as her mind blurred. Havitz stepped forward.

“You’re scared of me? Why?”

Up close, his face—and especially his eyes—were impossibly clear and beautiful.

“B-because I was afraid of dying…”

The words escaped her lips before she could stop them.

“Hmmm. So you’re that scared of dying?”

Havitz stroked his mustache thoughtfully, then nodded as if coming to a conclusion.

“Then I won’t kill you.”

“R-really?”

She couldn’t help but ask again.

“Yeah. Happy now? Since I won’t kill you, nobody else will either, right?”

Never in her life had she felt such overwhelming relief.

“Thank you! Thank you so much!”

As the maid bowed her head repeatedly in gratitude, Havitz slipped on his coat and muttered,

“Bored out of my mind. Guess I’ll go out for some fun. Oh, and take my brother’s retainers—rip out all their knee joints.”

The maid’s face went pale.

“M-me, sir?”

“If it’s too much trouble, tell the Minister. All of my brother’s assets and retainers are yours now. How many? Two hundred? Play with them until you’re bored.”

While she stood there dazed, Havitz turned from the doorway and winked.

“Live with a smile. Have fun.”

The room he left behind still reeked of blood.

Two hours by carriage from Arkaba Palace, Havitz arrived at the eastern sea.

It was a place famous for fishing, but all the nobles had cleared out as soon as word spread that he was coming.

“So quiet. Guess I’m lucky today.”

In Arkaba, there wasn’t a single noble or commoner who didn’t know the name Havitz.

In a secluded part of the forest near the coast, Havitz settled in and cast a line—without even bothering to bait the hook.

“Fwaaaaaaah.”

His long, exaggerated yawn radiated sheer boredom.

Havitz’s playgrounds were always empty.

“Nothing’s fun these days.”

Why did he remain in a world where no one came to play?

“What, nothing interesting?”

This was before he ever met the 4 Gustavs.

“Boo. Maa. Oooo.”

He played with the shape of his mouth, making random sounds, then crossed his eyes as he stared into space…

“WAAAAAAAH!”

Suddenly, he whipped his head around and screamed, shattering the tranquil silence.

“Boo. Maa. Oooo.”

Just then, a girl’s voice called from behind him.

“Hm?”

When Havitz turned, he saw a filthy little girl in gypsy-like clothes dramatically mimicking his mouth movements.

“Boo. Maa. Oooo. What kind of sound is that?”

Blinking, Havitz pointed to the sea.

“Fish noises.”

“Haha! Fish don’t make sounds! And they don’t bite here anyway! You’re dumb, mister!”

“Huh? Mister?”

Havitz pointed to himself.

“I’m not a mister.”

“You are a mister! You’ve got a mustache! Haha! Mustache Mister! Mustache Mister!”

Havitz smacked his lips and grabbed his fishing rod again.

“I hope I catch a shark or something.”

The girl giggled and, amused by the mustache, walked up and gave it a playful tug.

“Ow!”

“Oh wow, it’s a real mustache?”

Tearing up a little, Havitz adjusted his mustache.

“This is mine. And you’re a girl, so even when you grow up, you won’t have hair like this.”

A shadow fell over the girl’s face.

“I know what a girl is.”

“Oh yeah? What is it?”

She smiled brightly again and plopped down beside him.

“Hey Mister, I’m bored. Wanna play?”

Havitz thought for a moment, then nodded.

“Sure. But what should we play?”

“Let’s play dolls. You be the monster, and I’ll be the beautiful girl running away from the monster.”

She pulled out a rag doll made of stitched cloth.

“Here. This is the monster.”

The “monster” just looked like a man with exaggerated features.

“Alright, I’ll be the monster.”

Taking the doll without protest, Havitz started banging it on the ground and growled,

“Graaah! Run all you want! I’ll grind you up with my sharp teeth!”

“Eeeeeek! Somebody save me! It’s the monster!”

Havitz moved the doll, chasing after her.

“Stop right there! You beautiful…!”

Then, he suddenly paused and asked,

“Wait, time out. What’s your name?”

The girl looked up at him, stepping out of the game, then flashed a bright grin full of white teeth.

“Yus. Yus Abella.”
