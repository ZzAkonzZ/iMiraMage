Chapter 1212:

Pyramids constructed across the world hijacked people’s minds through magnetic fields.

Those affected became Ascended, and began to move in groups according to the will of God.

Like the branching streams of a river, humanity split into countless paths—some of which led toward the Holy War.

“Toward the Sacred Land of God.”

Others, however, began marching away from Jive long before the end, as if they already knew where and when the final event would take place.

“We’re heading to Delta Headquarters.”

Even in the Kingdom of Jive, where one of the pyramids had been raised, many Ascended had already emerged.

Among them were Holy Knights from the Anti-Demon Division, originally dispatched by order of Pope Constantine to investigate the pyramid.

“Kill Maya.”

Clad in heavy armor, they moved with loud, mechanical clanking as they advanced toward Delta Headquarters.

The protests still raged on, and Albino turned away from the window, as though declaring the game had already ended.

“Everyone’s shouting that they’re right. And because everyone insists they’re right, no one dares say anyone is wrong. That’s the real problem.”

Lupist followed him.

“After the Demon Invasion, Jive adopted a democratic monarchy—at least within the capital. That’s how Gis rose to power. I imagine the pride of the citizens who rebuilt the capital is immense.”

“No doubt. Democracy is a fine system. But it shouldn’t be treated as sacred. Ideology itself holds no inherent value. Monarchism didn’t fall because it opposed humanism—it collapsed because it became outdated. The bloody revolutions? They were just transitional costs compared to the wealth a system shift brought.”

“With all due respect, your tone seems... somewhat disdainful of the current age.”

“Heh, maybe so. Freedom’s a good thing. But to me, freedom isn’t something you’re given—it’s something you fight for. That belief is pretty deeply rooted in me. I guess that makes me an old fossil.”

“If I’ve spoken out of turn—”

“No, don’t worry. It’s the truth. I’m just living my age. What worries me is how the concept of ‘freedom’ now seems to override all other values.”

Albino continued, gazing out the window again.

“We say this society values the individual, but really it’s just throwing out a handful of options and saying, ‘Choose one.’ I heard there was a hot debate in Rodenin a while back about legalizing homosexuality. That’s fine. One side is straight, the other gay. But what about someone who’s kind of gay? Half gay? Where do they go? In the end, it’s binary. And all those subtle in-betweens get crushed. That’s not negotiation—that’s the kind of thinking you use in war. The protesters? They’ll never unify.”

If the goal is simply to win...

“I’m not advocating a return to monarchy. Rodenin also had a time when the system itself censored people. But after that dark age passed, now it’s individuals who censor each other. No one asked them to—but people watch and judge each other constantly. And once the sides are drawn, the only thing left is merciless punishment.”

“There may not even be a right answer, but people are caught in this illusion that everything can be perfectly defined. Imagination—especially the imagination to picture how others live—has become a useless trait in society. Is this evolution? Maybe. If everyone’s happy, then fine. Let them live that way. But… I guess people like me will just keep feeling bitter.”

“Someone who thinks like you doesn’t count as an old fossil.”

“Ha! That’s only because you’re starting to become like me. These days, they call us ‘boomers.’ Think back to when we were young. We couldn’t stand anything an adult said. But maybe—just maybe—there’s value in following someone’s lead, even when it’s imperfect or uncomfortable. Whether it’s right or wrong, maybe that’s the natural order. In the end, they’ll be the ones inheriting the future.”

Perhaps because the ugly politics of the Holy War would finally end tomorrow morning, Albino’s reflections ran deep.

When he opened the door to the security room, the air was thick with smoke, and Dante was smoking a cigarette.

“Ah, my apologies.”

Albino reached out a hand.

“No need. Come in.”

Dante, currently the busiest man around—tasked with tracking down Rom, Garto, and Temica—was visibly worn.

“You’re working hard. I should be buying you a drink.”

“No need. Please, have a seat.”

After stubbing out his cigarette, Dante launched into a briefing.

“Roughly twelve hours until the vote. Depending on how the alliances shift, things could still change—but right now, the leading candidate for World Leadership is Jive. The odds are at 42%.”

Because it was a matter of tallying votes, broad probability estimates were possible.

“Well… they’ve done a decent job.”

Ever since the Holy War began, Jive had endured countless events and crises. If they truly deserved the position, it was because they’d navigated those variables with stability.

Dante continued.

“The next most likely candidate is Corona. However, their own nation is currently unstable. The Ivory Tower is on the brink of collapse. Their odds are 34%.”

No one disagreed.

“Well, maybe it’s meaningless, but let’s hear who’s in third place.”

They probably hoped it would be Tormia.

“Third place is very close. Kashan and Iron are at 21% and 19%, respectively.”

“Kashan, huh…”

“Yes. Uorin’s identity hasn’t been confirmed yet, but if she survives until tomorrow morning, there’s a chance for a comeback. That’s why their odds are higher than Iron’s.”

Albino looked pained.

“A country where the Empress fled without even grabbing a clean pair of underwear is in third place? Then Tormia probably never had any hope to begin with.”

Lupist added:

“Up until things fell apart, Kashan was the overwhelming favorite. Just the fact that they’ve fallen this far is an enormous incident in itself. As for Tormia…”

He finally couldn’t hold it in anymore and asked bluntly:

“So what place are we in?”

“Sixth. Jive, Corona, Kashan, Iron, Jincheon… then Tormia. Current odds: 16.4%.”

Albino stroked his beard.

“So, the fact that no nation has surpassed 50 percent… means there are still risk factors in play. Is there still a chance we could overtake them within the next twelve hours?”

Dante flipped through the charts.

“Currently, groups of people near the pyramids are marching toward Rodenin. If they clash with the protesters, things could spiral out of control. There’s a real possibility that Gis could be impeached.”

“If that happens, he’ll lose his voting rights. Even if someone is appointed as acting monarch, twelve hours is far too short a window to recover.”

There was still the option to play their final card—Plu—but it would be a massive gamble.

Lupist spoke up.

“So you're saying there's still a shot at turning things around.”

“Yes. Unpredictable events could happen at any time. And this is just my personal opinion…”

As Dante trailed off, the two others looked at him.

“What is it?”

“It’s easier to extract meaningful insight from a single event. It’s a matter of intuition and calculation.”

“Something’s bothering you?”

Dante pointed at the Kingdom of Keshia listed on the chart.

“Fermi. That guy definitely knows something. These numbers wouldn’t make sense otherwise.”

“What’s the ranking?”

“Twelfth. Below even the Southern Tribal Union. Keshia’s odds of becoming the World Leadership Nation are 0.1 percent.”

Lupist rubbed his chin.

“A number that low… it’s practically inhuman.”

“Right. There’s no alliance. No movement. Even after the previous king was assassinated, they’ve been completely silent. Either they’ve given up, or they’re aiming for something enormous. It’s one of the two.”

“The latter,” Albino said firmly. Then he asked, “So what is it? What’s the big thing they’re after?”

There was a knock at the door, and Lilia peeked in, her face framed by the half-open door.

“Hey, got a moment?”

She had stayed behind to care for Pony after correctly predicting the fall of the Moon Kingdom.

“What’s the matter?”

“Her Highness has regained consciousness.”

Though Dante was concerned for his old classmate, he had no time to spare at the moment.

‘Pony is no longer a variable.’

Right now, the one they needed to focus on was Fermi.

“Understood. I’ll check on her later—”

“Shirone is there too. In her room.”

Lilia added hastily.

Though she’d said it indirectly, it was clear Shirone had called them all.

Albino stood from his chair.

“Let’s go. We’ll hear the rest there. Who knows? Maybe that 16.4 percent will shift.”

He stepped out of the room, followed by Lupist, Dante, and Lilia. Lupist asked,

“You said the 16.4 percent might move. Do you think Shirone could be our variable?”

“He must’ve felt something after seeing the protests. But that’s not the real reason. The moment that girl spoke just now, a thought crossed my mind: Fermi holds the 0.1 percent lie, and Shirone holds the 99.9 percent truth. And something tells me…”

Albino’s eyes sharpened.

“Fermi’s lie and Shirone’s truth—they’re connected. Those two pulled something off. Using a method no one else knows.”

He wondered for a moment—maybe even they themselves didn’t fully understand it.

‘If not, then how could this be happening…?’

—

In a secret chamber in Keshia Sector, Fermi received the data recently sent from the excavation team.

As a High Gear administrator, he trusted their coding skills—there would’ve been no issue with the transmission.

‘Whether they saw it or not doesn’t matter...’

All that mattered was one thing:

‘As long as Shirone doesn’t come into contact with this information. That’s why I made the contract with Marsha.’

Not because she prioritized transactions, but because she cared for Shirone deeply.

With a slightly trembling hand, Fermi opened the data—and records of events yet to come unfolded before him.

Since the download had stopped at 83 percent, there were occasional corrupted sections scattered throughout.

More importantly, he couldn’t see how Omega 999 would ultimately end.

‘But this is enough.’

Fermi quickly scanned the data.

Then his eyes caught on an entry that hadn’t changed—the record of Amy’s death.

‘As I thought. This won’t be easy. Amy’s life is too closely tied to the fate of this world.’

After Pisho’s funeral, when Shirone had visited the Golden Wheel, Fermi had told him the truth.

‘What a disaster that was.’

They had fought countless times since their days at the Magic Academy, but that day—Fermi realized something for the first time:

Even he could go mad with grief.

‘Honestly, I’m not confident. But no matter how I look at it, this is the only way. I can’t even calculate the odds. The only thing that keeps me going is…’

Shirone’s approval.

‘He came to the same conclusion I did. That’s why he accepted the deal. Even though he can’t stand me…’

He said he trusted him.

“…Hah.”

Fermi returned to the beginning and began reviewing the Omega 999 records more carefully.

And this time, his gaze stopped—not on Shirone—but on someone he loved.

‘Seriel.’

An event that would occur… in just one hour.
