Chapter 1218:

When they arrived at the rendezvous point after collecting supplies from the warehouse, Penna was already speaking with the captain.

“How did it go?” “It went well. I’ll tell you more in a bit.”

The captain had already been bribed, but it seemed better to talk elsewhere.

The captain said, “It’s time to go. Let me make this clear—my role is just to guide you. I can’t guarantee I’ll be able to bribe the warden. He’s a strict rule-follower.”

Leaving the port, Shirone’s group entered the royal castle—not the front, but through the back gate.

The structure was identical to the palace in the real world, and many people were busily working without rest.

Iruki commented, “Each of them is a program performing their role. Keeping a world like Melkidu running must not be easy. From what we’ve seen, honestly, it’s a well-made system.”

Shirone agreed, though the reason such a world was created still pained him.

“Cain…”

According to Omega’s records, Cain was the son of Mitochondrial Eve—or more precisely, the son of Lilith.

“Here we are,”

The captain pointed to the warden’s office and knocked.

“Come in.”

When they opened the door, a man with a rugged face and a scar waited for them.

“Good evening, Warden. Here’s the list of supplies delivered tonight. I need your signature.”

It was a process requiring high clearance.

As the warden slowly reviewed the papers, he glanced up and glared at the group.

“Who are they?”

“Well…”

Shirone stepped forward.

“My friend is wrongly imprisoned. I ask for your leniency.”

Once the bribery process began, the logic didn’t matter much, but Shirone couldn’t be as bold as Nade.

“Your friend? Who?”

According to Curtis, someone needed to already be in prison in order to bribe the warden.

“Curtis.”

The warden’s eyes narrowed.

“No way. That guy’s a serious criminal. If you listed all the murders and robberies he’s committed, you’d fill dozens of pages. I can’t let him go.”

“But in Melkidu, Curtis is innocent. Please give me a chance to persuade you.”

The warden, who had been glaring at Shirone, finally opened his mouth.

“You want to persuade me? My personality is ‘unyielding.’ It’ll cost you 50 million points.”

Just as expected.

“Yes.”

As Shirone answered, the room transformed into a phase space, leaving only the accomplices behind.

A message rang out:

The bribery program has activated. Break the warden’s principles with your Crime Dice.

From the other end of the phase space, the warden stepped forward.

‘That’s unexpected…’

While Shirone’s group was caught off guard, the warden revealed his dice—four eight-sided dice.

Nade scowled.

“What the hell? He’s stronger than us? I thought persuasion was done with the same dice?”

Shirone’s group had three seven-sided dice—both in number and size, a tier lower.

The warden smirked coldly.

“Hmph. I don’t know how you’ve bribed your way this far, but I’m different.”

Shirone had a gut feeling.

“A rule-follower. Unlike other administrators, the warden doesn’t bend his own rules.”

Another message appeared:

Each side rolls their dice. The resulting numbers are deducted from their respective Crime Points. Each face on the Crime Dice is worth 10,000 points. The team or user who brings the opponent’s Crime Points to zero first wins.

“Damn…”

Nade grasped the severity of the situation.

“This isn’t a one-shot match. With three seven-sided dice, the maximum we can get including doubles is 42. We don’t know how many points the warden has, but Shirone can’t handle it alone. We’ll all have to roll.”

Iruki asked,

“How many Crime Points does the warden have?”

50 million points.

Eden frowned.

“And we…”

Since they’d just paid 50 million to initiate the persuasion, they had about 8 million points left.

‘Simple math: if the total rolled dice add up to 800, we’re bankrupt.’

Shirone rested his chin on his hand and thought.

‘So that’s what Curtis meant by needing at least 50 million. Were we just too unprepared?’

No, not exactly.

‘We’re just a special case. Other players probably come here with around 100 million. For them, this kind of dice battle might be fairly easy.’

Which meant this showdown would depend on…

“Items.”

Shirone asked,

“Can we use items?”

The warden’s principle is to go all in. There are no restrictions for this persuasion.

“We can change the turn order too, right?”

You can. However, before your turn, no participant may act twice. Also, one person may not use two items at once under this rule.

“So that’s how it works.”

Fortunately, they had secured a good number of items while looting the port warehouse.

“Nade’s kleptomania actually helped this time.”

“Hey!”

Nade shouted in protest, looking wrongfully accused—but deep down, he felt relieved.

‘Good thing I insisted we use points after all.’

Shirone said,

“If we use the items well, this won’t go beyond twenty turns. But that just means each turn is even more important.”

The warden spoke.

“I’ll go first.”

That was unusual too, but there was no point in arguing with a rule-abiding man.

He pulled out a card.

“Warden’s Authority.”

“What? What kind of item is that?”

—The warden uses an administrator-only item. This cannot be purchased in shops.

The card’s effect was revealed.

—Divide incoming attack power by the lowest number rolled on the Crime Dice. Effect lasts for 3 turns.

As they listened to the explanation, the dice were rolled.

‘Come on… roll a 1… please!’

But contrary to Nade’s wish, the dice landed on 3, 6, 2, and 7.

‘Divide by 2.’

This meant that for the next three turns, any damage dealt to the warden would be halved.

“What kind of item is that?!”

“It’s for administrators only. Anyway, let’s rearrange our turn order. We have enough items, but if Shirone attacks while Warden’s Authority is active, it’ll be inefficient.”

Shirone’s turn had the potential for a critical hit.

“I’ll go first, then.”

Eden pulled out a card.

It was the high-tier item Genius Insight, which combines the values of the dice for the maximum possible total.

However, in the case of a double, both results are added together—making the best-case outcome 15.54 million.

“7, 3, 4.”

The maximum value of 743,000 was halved to 371,500.

“…Not great.”

It was a solid number, but considering the warden had 50 million points, it wasn’t nearly enough.

The warden drew another card.

“Doctrine of Punishment.”

—Each turn, the value of your roll will be doubled. Lasts for five turns.

Iruki’s eyes narrowed.

‘That means 32x critical on the fifth turn. So he plans to end this within five turns.’

The warden rolled 5, 1, 7, 4.

“340,000 deducted. Still manageable, but things are going to get rough from the next turn onward.”

Iruki’s eyes sparkled.

“Wait, let me go next.”

Pushing Nade aside, he used Accountant’s Delusion.

—The value this turn will be applied to the next turn.

‘Even if we go all out, we don’t have enough points left. This is our best shot.’

A double came up, totaling 27.

“Fools. You were too afraid of Warden’s Authority and wasted your chance to use real offensive items.”

The warden went again. With the 4x multiplier, 1.64 million points were deducted.

He smirked.

“I’ll only get stronger from here. You’ll never break my resolve.”

No one replied—because everyone understood Iruki’s plan.

‘This persuasion isn’t based on 100% probability. Even Quantum Collapse won’t guarantee a win.’

They couldn’t speak the plan aloud, or the enemy might use a defense item.

“Accountant’s Delusion.”

Nade used the same item, making the warden frown.

‘Again?’

—Accountant’s Delusion stacks twice. This turn’s result will carry over again.

‘These guys… they’re going to use Wind God.’

The warden realized it.

‘A user-exclusive item. A good strategy, but not likely to pay off. Unless you get big rolls back to back…’

The multiplicative effect quickly loses power with small numbers.

‘I’ll win regardless. The chances of deducting 50 million in one go are laughably low.’

Nade threw the dice hard.

‘Please! Double! Double!’

Though his heart sank when all the numbers were different, it pounded again when he saw the result.

‘Yes!’

7, 4, 6—totaling 17.

If they used Wind God next turn, they’d guarantee at least 4.59 million points.

The warden played another card.

“So you like gambling? Then let fate decide. Activate: Emergency Situation.”

—All dice values gain +2.

He hit another double—totaling 4.8 million. Shirone’s team was now down to about 1 million points.

“The next turn is the end. You’d better prepare to lose.”

“It’s too early to feel safe, isn’t it? Your Warden’s Authority is gone now.”

“So what? It doesn’t change anything. You think you’ll knock off 50 million in one shot? The odds are…”

“Give it to me.”

Shirone took the dice.

“Wind God.”

—All operations between dice rolls become multiplication.

Shirone asked,

“Why can’t you release Curtis?”

“Don’t be ridiculous. He committed countless crimes.”

“You’re the ones who made him that way.”

The warden went silent.

“Criminals should be punished, of course. But what about the people who created this system? If you have points, you can do anything and be innocent. If you don’t, you’re guilty. The more crimes you commit, the more points you get. Meanwhile, those who can’t adapt always start from the bottom again. Does this system seem fair to you?”

Shirone’s words weren’t aimed at the warden alone.

“I don’t understand what you mean. No one forced him. If he didn’t want to go to prison, he should’ve avoided crime. Or are you saying we should hate the sin but not the sinner?”

“No.”

Shirone gritted his teeth.

“If you really hate the sinner that much…”

He threw his arm, casting the three Crime Dice into the air.

“Try hating the sin too.”

The dice bounced—7, 7, 7.

The warden stared, dumbfounded.

“W-what is this…”

—Double! You gain another roll.

“If you really want to punish criminals…”

Shirone picked the dice back up and cast them again, into the same spot.

“Then ask yourself—why does sin even exist in the first place?”

Again: 7, 7, 7.

—Accountant’s Delusion activates. Wind God effect converts all operations to multiplication.

27 × 17 × 7 × 7 × 7 × 7 × 7 × 7 = 54,000,891

Iruki smiled.

“We win.”

The phase space vanished, and time began to flow once more.
